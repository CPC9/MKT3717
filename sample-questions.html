Lecture 1: Course Introduction

What is the difference between a product and a brand?

Show Answer
A product is anything that can be offered to a market for attention, acquisition, use, or consumption that might satisfy a need or want. A brand goes beyond the product -- it is built through consumer perceptions, expectations, and experiences. The course uses the analogy that "product and brand is like body and soul": companies make products, but consumers make brands through their perceptions and associations.

What are the three dimensions of the Innovation Challenge framework, and why must a successful product satisfy all three?

Show Answer
The three dimensions are: (1) People desirability -- customers must want it, (2) Business viability -- the business model must work, and (3) Technical feasibility -- it must be buildable. A product must satisfy all three simultaneously because missing any one dimension leads to failure. For example, a product that is desirable and feasible but not viable cannot sustain a business, while one that is viable and feasible but not desirable will not attract customers.

What are the five phases of the New Product Development (NPD) process?

Show Answer
The five phases are: (1) Opportunity Identification and Selection, (2) Concept Generation, (3) Concept/Project Evaluation, (4) Development, and (5) Launch. This structured sequence provides quality assurance, promotes coordination in the development team, provides a benchmark for performance assessment, and identifies opportunities for improvement.

Explain the difference between a product manager and a brand manager.

Show Answer
A product manager is a cross-functional role responsible for the life cycle of a product, driving its vision, strategy, design, and execution. They are responsible for creating a successful product. A brand manager is an advocate of the organization who cultivates brand image and worth by building brand equity (the intrinsic value a brand holds over competitors). In short, the product manager ensures a successful product is created, while the brand manager sustains that success over time.

Lecture 2: NPD Opportunity Identification

What is the difference between TAM, SAM, and SOM in market sizing?

Show Answer
TAM (Total Addressable Market) is the entire potential revenue opportunity, answering "How big is the dream?" SAM (Serviceable Addressable Market) is the portion of TAM your business can realistically target with its current offerings, answering "Where can you actually play?" SOM (Serviceable Obtainable Market) is the share of SAM your business can capture considering competition and constraints, answering "What can you credibly win?" For example, for Nike yoga apparel in Singapore: TAM = SGD 300M, SAM = SGD 180M (premium segment), SOM = SGD 18M (10% capture).

What is the RWW (Real-Win-Worth It) analysis, and how does it relate to the Three Innovation Challenge?

Show Answer
The RWW Analysis (Day, 2007) is a framework for evaluating opportunities by asking three questions: "Is it Real?" (maps to People/Desirable -- is there a real market need?), "Can we Win?" (maps to Technical/Feasible -- can we deliver the product and establish competitive advantage?), and "Is it Worth It?" (maps to Business/Viable -- will it be profitable at acceptable risk?). It provides a structured way to screen opportunities against the same three innovation challenge dimensions.

What is the difference between explicit, unfulfilled, and latent customer needs? Why are latent needs important for innovation?

Show Answer
Explicit needs are easy for customers to express and are likely already fulfilled (e.g., a candle that smells good). Unfulfilled needs are easy to express but known to be difficult to address (e.g., a candle with no burn hazards). Latent needs are hard for customers to express because they are not yet widely understood (e.g., a smart scent device for better sleep). Latent needs are important because when they are addressed, products can delight customers in new ways and exceed expectations. The Swiffer is a famous example -- P&G discovered through ethnography that "it's hard to clean the broom," a latent need that generated $100M in its first four months.

When would you use PESTEL analysis versus Porter's Five Forces?

Show Answer
PESTEL analysis is used to evaluate the broad, uncontrollable general environment (Political, Economic, Social, Technological, Environmental, Legal factors). Porter's Five Forces is used to evaluate the industry or operating environment (threat of new entrants, bargaining power of buyers, bargaining power of suppliers, threat of substitutes, intensity of rivalry). Use PESTEL when assessing macro-level conditions that affect all players in the market, and use Porter's Five Forces when analyzing the competitive attractiveness and dynamics of a specific industry.

Lecture 3: NPD Concept Screening and Testing

What is the Jobs To Be Done (JTBD) framework, and how does it differ from traditional product management thinking?

Show Answer
The JTBD framework asserts that "people don't buy products -- they hire them to get a job done in their life." A JTBD statement follows the format: "When [situation], I want to [motivation], so I can [desired outcome]." Traditional PM thinking asks "What features should we build?" or "What do users say they want?" whereas JTBD asks "What progress is the user trying to make?" and "In what situation do they hire this product?" This reframing prevents feature-bloat and keeps the team anchored in real customer motivation rather than surface-level requests.

What is the difference between a Concept Screening Matrix (Pugh Method) and a Concept Scoring Matrix?

Show Answer
The Concept Screening Matrix is a rapid first-pass tool that compares concepts against a reference concept using simple +/0/- ratings on unweighted criteria, producing a net score to quickly eliminate weak concepts. The Concept Scoring Matrix is more rigorous: it uses weighted criteria (importance weights from customer surveys) and a 1-5 rating scale, producing weighted total scores for detailed ranking. Screening is for narrowing a large set of concepts cheaply; scoring is for making rigorous final selections among the surviving concepts.

What are the five guidelines for writing proper customer needs statements?

Show Answer
The five guidelines are: (1) Write in "what" not "how" -- describe what the product does, not the specific solution. (2) Be specific -- avoid vague terms like "versatile." (3) Use positive language, not negative -- describe what the product does, not what it avoids. (4) Frame as an attribute of the product, not an action by the user. (5) Avoid prescriptive language like "must" and "should" -- use "can" instead. These guidelines ensure needs statements are neutral, actionable, and translatable into measurable product specifications.

What three inputs are needed to define a product concept, and which starting point is the least risky?

Show Answer
A product concept requires at least two of three inputs: (1) Form -- the physical thing created or service process, (2) Technology -- the source by which the form is attained, and (3) Benefit/Need -- the advantage to the customer. Starting from the customer need (need-first approach) is the least risky, because the product is grounded in a known demand. Technology-first and form-first approaches are riskier because the team must find a market fit after the fact, as illustrated by the Post-It Note's 12-year journey from technology to product.

Lecture 4: Concept Testing Review

Why is the goal of concept evaluation "concept improvement" rather than just "concept selection"?

Show Answer
The best product concept is often created by merging the strongest features from multiple candidates rather than simply picking the highest scorer. A concept scoring matrix can favor mediocre all-rounders -- the "best average" that excels at nothing. Teams should examine standout features from lower-ranked concepts and combine them into an improved design. This approach produces a final concept that is stronger than any single original candidate.

What role does the Product Innovation Charter (PIC) play in concept testing?

Show Answer
The Product Innovation Charter acts as a strategic filter during concept testing (Phase 3 of NPD). It defines the boundaries of a product development effort, including target markets, technology requirements, and innovation goals. Concepts that fall outside these boundaries -- requiring technologies the firm lacks, targeting unfamiliar customers, offering wrong levels of innovativeness, or failing on cost/competitive dimensions -- should be eliminated before development resources are committed. Concept testing is fundamentally a "stage of elimination."

How does testing evolve across the different stages of the product development process?

Show Answer
Testing evolves from qualitative to quantitative methods across six stages: During Planning and Concept Development, firms use specification testing, benchmarking, and qualitative concept testing ("Should we build this?"). During System-Level and Detail Design, prototype testing and alpha testing validate design decisions. During Testing and Refinement, quantitative market testing and beta testing with real users occur. During Production Ramp-Up, A/B testing and release versioning optimize the product at scale ("How do we make it work better?"). Go/No-Go decision gates at key transition points determine whether the project advances.

Why should sensitivity analysis always be performed on a concept scoring matrix?

Show Answer
Sensitivity analysis tests how changes in importance weightings or uncertain ratings affect the concept ranking. This is essential because close scores between concepts (e.g., 594 vs. 587 vs. 585 in the cup holder exercise) mean that small changes in assumptions could reverse the outcome. If the top-ranked concept's lead is not robust to reasonable changes in weights or ratings, the team should reconsider its choice. Additionally, different customer segments may yield different rankings, so concept selection should be performed separately for each target group.

Lecture 5: Conjoint Analysis for Product Design

What is conjoint analysis, and what key assumption does it rely on?

Show Answer
Conjoint analysis is a survey-based statistical technique used to determine how consumers value different attributes of a product or service. It works by presenting respondents with product profiles combining different attribute levels and asking them to rank, rate, or choose among them. Its key assumption is that a product is a bundle of attributes and that customers combine the values they derive from individual attributes to generate their overall valuation -- in other words, product value equals the sum of its parts. Regression analysis then decomposes reported preferences to infer the utility each attribute level provides.

How do you calculate the relative importance of attributes in conjoint analysis?

Show Answer
Relative importance is calculated by finding the range of coefficients for each attribute (the absolute difference between the highest and lowest coefficient values, including the baseline which is 0), then dividing each attribute's range by the sum of all ranges. For example, in the salsa study: Spiciness range = 10.67, Thickness range = 6.17, Color range = 1.00. Sum = 17.84. Spiciness importance = 10.67/17.84 = 59.8%, Thickness = 34.6%, Color = 5.6%. This tells the product manager which attributes have the greatest influence on consumer preference.

When would conjoint analysis NOT be appropriate to use?

Show Answer
Conjoint analysis is not appropriate when: (1) product value cannot be approximated as the sum of its parts because attributes interact strongly (e.g., fragrances, meal ingredients), (2) the product cannot be decomposed into discrete attributes (e.g., clothing design aesthetics), or (3) attributes are subjective and ambiguous (e.g., "friendly" or "sporty"). It works best for products like computers, credit cards, hotel packages, and financial products where attributes are independent, discrete, and unambiguous.

What is delayed differentiation (postponement), and how did Benetton use it to reduce waste?

Show Answer
Delayed differentiation is a production strategy that postpones the product-differentiating steps until customer preferences or demand signals are known. Benetton originally dyed yarn before knitting sweaters, which required forecasting demand for each color far in advance -- resulting in 33% unsold stock. They reversed the process: knit white garments first, then dye finished sweaters based on actual demand. This reduced unsold stock from 33% to 8%. The strategy requires modular product architecture where the differentiating element (color, in this case) can be applied as the last step.

Lecture 6: Market Experiments & Project Management

Why is correlation not the same as causation, and how do experiments solve this problem?

Show Answer
Correlation means two variables move together, but one does not necessarily cause the other -- a confounding variable may be responsible (e.g., ice cream sales and drowning rates both rise in summer because of hot weather, not because one causes the other). Experiments solve this by randomly assigning subjects to treatment and control groups, which ensures the groups are comparable on all factors except the one being tested. This isolates the causal effect of the variable of interest. The key concept is the counterfactual: "What would have happened if we had not taken this action?"

What is the critical path in project management, and why does it matter?

Show Answer
The critical path is the longest path through a project network diagram, and it determines the minimum project duration. All activities on the critical path have zero slack, meaning any delay to a critical activity directly delays the entire project. It matters because no amount of optimization on non-critical activities will shorten the project -- management attention and crashing efforts must focus on the critical path. If an activity duration changes, the critical path may shift, requiring recalculation of all paths.

What is the difference between a factorial design and a simple A/B test?

Show Answer
An A/B test compares two versions of a single variable (e.g., webpage layout A vs. B) to determine which performs better. A factorial design tests all possible combinations of two or more independent variables simultaneously. For example, a 2x2 factorial design tests 2 levels of Factor A crossed with 2 levels of Factor B, yielding 4 conditions. The advantage of factorial design is that it reveals not only the main effect of each factor but also interaction effects (where the effect of one factor depends on the level of another), and it is more efficient than running separate A/B tests for each factor.

Explain what slack (float) means in project scheduling. How is it calculated?

Show Answer
Slack (or float) is the maximum time an activity can be delayed without delaying the entire project's completion date. It is calculated as: Slack = LS - ES = LF - EF, where LS is Latest Start, ES is Earliest Start, LF is Latest Finish, and EF is Earliest Finish. Activities with zero slack are on the critical path and cannot be delayed at all. Activities with positive slack can absorb that many time units of delay without affecting the project deadline, which is essential information for resource allocation and risk management.

Lecture 7: Brand Management

What is customer-based brand equity (CBBE), and what two components make up brand knowledge?

Show Answer
Customer-based brand equity is the differential effect that brand knowledge has on consumer response to the marketing of a brand. When consumers react more favorably to a product because they recognize the brand (compared to when they do not), the brand has positive equity. Brand knowledge consists of two components: (1) Brand awareness -- whether and how easily consumers can identify the brand (through recognition and recall), and (2) Brand image -- the perceptions, associations, and meanings consumers hold about the brand in memory. Both must be strong for robust brand equity.

What are the four levels and six building blocks of Keller's Brand Resonance Pyramid?

Show Answer
The four levels (bottom to top) are: (1) Identity -- "Who are you?" with the building block of Brand Salience (depth and breadth of awareness). (2) Meaning -- "What are you?" with Brand Performance (functional needs) and Brand Imagery (psychological and social needs). (3) Response -- "What about you?" with Brand Judgments (quality, credibility, consideration, superiority) and Brand Feelings (warmth, fun, excitement, security, social approval, self-respect). (4) Relationships -- "What about you and me?" with Brand Resonance (behavioral loyalty, attitudinal attachment, sense of community, active engagement). The pyramid must be built from the bottom up.

What is a brand mantra, and how does it differ from a tagline?

Show Answer
A brand mantra is a short (three- to five-word) internal articulation of the heart and soul of the brand. It consists of three components: an emotional modifier (how the brand provides benefits), a descriptive modifier (the nature of the product/experience), and a brand function (the type of benefit delivered). For example, Nike's brand mantra is "Authentic Athletic Performance." A tagline is an external, consumer-facing phrase used in marketing communications. The brand mantra guides internal marketing decisions and ensures consistency, while the tagline communicates to consumers. The two should be aligned but serve different purposes.

What is the difference between points of parity (POPs) and points of difference (PODs) in brand positioning?

Show Answer
Points of parity are associations shared with competitors that are necessary for the brand to be considered a legitimate player in the category. There are two types: category POPs (must-have associations to compete) and competitive POPs (associations that negate a competitor's advantage). Points of difference are strong, favorable, and unique associations that consumers believe they cannot find to the same extent with a competitive brand -- they form the basis of the brand's competitive advantage. Effective positioning requires establishing both: POPs to ensure credibility and PODs to drive preference.

Lecture 8: Brand Sustainability & Personal Branding

What is disruptive repositioning, and what risks does it carry for a luxury brand?

Show Answer
Disruptive repositioning is a bold strategic shift that fundamentally changes a brand's target segment, image, or value proposition rather than making incremental adjustments. For luxury brands, this carries the risk of alienating core existing customers while pursuing new demographics. There is also the risk of brand dilution -- when the brand extends into more accessible product categories (like Gucci entering beauty) or broadens its consumer base too aggressively, it may weaken the perception of exclusivity that is central to luxury brand identity.

What are the key differences between the "old luxury" and "new luxury" paradigms?

Show Answer
Old luxury emphasized status symbols, passive customer engagement, ownership-focused consumption, destination shopping (exclusive in-store experiences), and resistance to online channels. New luxury has shifted toward self-expression, active customer co-creation, experience-driven consumption, omnichannel browsing, and fully integrated digital presence. This transformation is driven by Millennials and Gen Z (projected to represent 55% of the luxury market by 2025), who value experiences, digital engagement, sustainability, and inclusivity over traditional status signaling.

How does the personal branding framework connect to the product development frameworks taught earlier in the course?

Show Answer
The personal branding framework directly maps the Desirable-Feasible-Viable innovation challenge from product development to individuals: what you are passionate about (desirable), what you are skilled at (feasible), and what the market values (viable). The sweet spot lies at the intersection of all three. Additionally, personal branding uses the same brand management tools: brand mantras (emotional modifier + descriptive modifier + brand function), mental maps (associative networks of how others perceive you), positioning statements, and points-of-difference -- all applied to building and managing an individual's professional identity.

Why is geographic market dependence a strategic risk for luxury brands?

Show Answer
Geographic market dependence occurs when a disproportionate share of a brand's revenue comes from consumers of a single nationality or region. For example, Chinese consumers accounted for 35% of luxury purchases in 2019 and were projected to reach 50% by 2025. This creates vulnerability to geopolitical tensions, economic downturns, and health crises (such as COVID-19) in that region. When Gucci's H1 2020 sales declined by 33.8% during the pandemic, it highlighted how over-reliance on a single consumer base can severely impact business performance.
