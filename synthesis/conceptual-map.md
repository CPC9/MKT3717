# Conceptual Map: MKT 3717 — Product and Brand Management

## Course Narrative

MKT 3717 guides students from first principles of new product development through the full lifecycle of building and sustaining a brand. The course's first arc — spanning Lectures 1 through 6 — traces the NPD process from market opportunity identification through concept generation, concept screening and scoring, consumer testing via conjoint analysis, and real-world market experiments, culminating in the project management tools needed to coordinate complex product launches. Each phase in this arc is both a decision gate (reducing the set of viable concepts) and a learning mechanism (deepening the team's knowledge of what customers value).

The second arc — Lectures 7 and 8 — shifts from making products to making meaning. Keller's Customer-Based Brand Equity (CBBE) pyramid provides the structural model for building a brand from basic identity (salience) up to deep customer relationships (resonance). Lecture 8 extends this into the real challenges brands face over time: maintaining equity under extension and dilution pressure, repositioning when market conditions change, and managing luxury brands where scarcity and cultural narrative are as important as functional performance.

Running beneath both arcs is a persistent methodological theme: quantitative tools in service of marketing judgment. Market sizing, concept scoring matrices, conjoint analysis regression, and critical-path calculations all exist to give managers defensible, data-grounded answers to inherently uncertain questions. Students who master both the frameworks and the calculations — and understand when to use which — are prepared for the types of decisions the exam tests.

## Lecture Progression

### Lecture 1: Course Introduction
**Driving Question:** What is a new product, and how do firms systematically develop and manage them?
**Core Concepts:** NPD Process, Product Manager Role, Crawford's Product Types, Three Innovation Challenge, Brand Manager vs. Product Manager
**Builds On:** Foundation — no prerequisites
**Leads To:** Lectures 2–6 (NPD arc), Lectures 7–8 (Brand Management arc)
**Key Relationships:**
- The NPD Process framework (Lecture 1) is the skeleton on which all subsequent NPD lectures (2–6) hang — each subsequent lecture covers one or more NPD phases in depth
- Crawford's six product types sets up the classification questions that appear in exam practice MCQs (Lecture 10 supplementary)
- The distinction between Brand Manager and Product Manager roles previews the Brand Management arc (Lectures 7–8)
- Three Innovation Challenge (Desirability/Viability/Feasibility) from Lecture 1 maps directly onto the RWW framework introduced in Lecture 2

### Lecture 2: NPD Part 1 — Opportunity Identification and Concept Generation
**Driving Question:** How do firms identify genuine market opportunities and generate product concepts to address them?
**Core Concepts:** VoC, Latent Needs, Market Sizing (TAM/SAM/SOM), PESTEL, Porter's Five Forces, RWW Analysis, Push vs. Pull Innovation
**Builds On:** Lecture 1 (NPD Process Phase 1 — Opportunity Identification/Selection)
**Leads To:** Lecture 3 (concept screening evaluates ideas generated here), Lecture 4 (concept testing refines surviving concepts), Lecture 10 (exam guide tests RWW, PESTEL, Porter's, TAM/SAM/SOM)
**Key Relationships:**
- PESTEL and Porter's Five Forces are complementary environmental scans that feed RWW "Is it Real?" and "Can We Win?" respectively
- TAM/SAM/SOM produces the SOM figure that feeds RWW "Is it Worth It?"
- Latent Needs (uncovered via JTBD) → Needs Statements (formalized) → Product Specifications (Lecture 3) → Concept Scoring Criteria (Lecture 3)
- Push vs. Pull innovation types determine which opportunity identification tools are most relevant in a given context

### Lecture 3: NPD Part 2 — Concept Screening and Testing
**Driving Question:** How do teams narrow many concepts down to the best candidate using systematic evaluation?
**Core Concepts:** Pugh Concept Screening Matrix, Concept Scoring Matrix, JTBD, Needs Statement, Product Specifications, Weighted Score
**Builds On:** Lecture 2 (concepts generated in Phase 1 are screened here; product specifications operationalize needs)
**Leads To:** Lecture 4 (concept testing continues the evaluation), Lecture 5 (conjoint analysis as a quantitative scoring tool), Lectures 10 and 17 (supplementary depth on selection methods)
**Key Relationships:**
- Concept Screening Matrix (coarse, +/0/−) → Concept Scoring Matrix (fine, weighted score) is a two-stage funnel for concept selection
- Product Specifications from Lecture 2 become the evaluation criteria in the Concept Scoring Matrix
- Weighted Score formula (Σ w_i × r_i) is the core quantitative tool tested in the Final Test Study Guide (Lecture 10)
- JTBD philosophy reinforces that criteria weights must come from customers, not the internal team — a rule repeated in exam preparation materials

### Lecture 4: Concept Testing Review and Guest Lecture
**Driving Question:** How do you validate that a product concept will succeed before committing to full development?
**Core Concepts:** Alpha Testing, Beta Testing, A/B Testing (at product stage), PIC, Go/No-Go Decision Gate, Sensitivity Analysis, Brainstorming
**Builds On:** Lecture 3 (surviving concepts are tested here against customer preferences)
**Leads To:** Lecture 5 (conjoint analysis as a quantitative concept-testing method), Lecture 6 (market experiments as post-development validation)
**Key Relationships:**
- Alpha/Beta Testing (Lecture 4) → A/B Testing (Lecture 6) represent different stages of validation — internal technical testing vs. market-level causal experiments
- PIC (Product Innovation Charter) establishes strategic constraints before concept testing begins; acts as the reference document for Go/No-Go gates
- Sensitivity Analysis in concept scoring (Lecture 4) previews the analytical rigor applied in conjoint analysis importance calculations (Lecture 5)
- Go/No-Go Decision Gates formalize the filtering function of the NPD process introduced in Lecture 1

### Lecture 5: Conjoint Analysis for Product Design
**Driving Question:** How can we quantify the relative importance of product attributes in consumer preferences, and use that to design better products?
**Core Concepts:** Part-Worth Utility, Relative Importance, Additive Utility Model, WTP Derivation, Market Simulation, Modular Architecture, Postponement
**Builds On:** Lectures 3–4 (extends concept evaluation from qualitative ratings to statistically estimated part-worths)
**Leads To:** Lecture 6 (conjoint findings guide what hypotheses to test in market experiments), Lecture 10 (exam tests conjoint calculations directly via smartphone and salsa examples)
**Key Relationships:**
- OLS Regression coefficients (β_k) = Part-Worth Utilities per attribute level; all relative to the omitted baseline level
- Relative Importance = Range of part-worths for an attribute ÷ Sum of all ranges × 100%
- WTP = −(Part-worth of feature) ÷ (Part-worth of $1 price increase)
- Modular Architecture (Lecture 5) is the product design response to the heterogeneous preferences revealed by conjoint analysis
- Postponement and Poka-Yoke are operational strategies for implementing modular designs cost-effectively

### Lecture 6: Market Experiment and Project Management
**Driving Question:** How do you establish causality in market tests, and how do you manage complex NPD projects to schedule and budget?
**Core Concepts:** RCT, A/B Testing (market-level), Factorial Design, Endowment Effect, WBS, CPM, Activity Slack, Critical Path, Project Crashing
**Builds On:** Lecture 5 (conjoint identifies what to test; experiments validate at scale), Lecture 1 (project management supports the full NPD process)
**Leads To:** Lectures 10 and 15 (exam tests CPM forward/backward pass and crashing calculations directly)
**Key Relationships:**
- Endowment Effect explains why market experiments must use random assignment — self-selected treatment groups produce biased results
- CPM Forward Pass → EF = ES + Duration; Backward Pass → LS = LF − Duration; Slack = LS − ES
- WBS decomposes the project → Network Diagram shows dependencies → Critical Path is the longest path (minimum project duration)
- Project Crashing requires identifying the critical path activity with the lowest crash cost per unit time, then re-identifying the critical path after each step

### Lecture 7: Brand Management Overview
**Driving Question:** What is brand equity and how is it systematically built from the ground up?
**Core Concepts:** CBBE Pyramid (Keller), Brand Salience, Brand Performance, Brand Imagery, Brand Judgments, Brand Feelings, Brand Resonance, POPs, PODs, Brand Mantra, Brand Elements
**Builds On:** Lecture 1 (Brand Manager role), Lectures 2–6 (the product whose brand is now being built and communicated)
**Leads To:** Lecture 8 (managing brand equity over time and under stress)
**Key Relationships:**
- CBBE Pyramid layers (Salience → Performance + Imagery → Judgments + Feelings → Resonance) must be built sequentially — each level depends on the one below
- POPs (parity with competitors) are prerequisites; PODs (differentiators) are only meaningful once POPs are established
- Brand Mantra (3-word brand essence) guides all brand-building decisions and acts as the filter for evaluating brand extensions
- Brand Elements (name, logo, tagline, character) must satisfy the MEMORABL criteria to compound brand equity over time

### Lecture 8: Brand Sustainability and Personal Branding
**Driving Question:** How do you maintain and revitalize brand equity as markets, consumers, and competitive conditions evolve?
**Core Concepts:** Brand Dilution, Brand Repositioning, New Luxury Paradigm, Three D's (Desirability/Deliverability/Differentiation), Perceptual Map, Scarcity
**Builds On:** Lecture 7 (managing the equity built in Lecture 7 across extensions, market shifts, and competitive dynamics)
**Leads To:** Course end — final synthesis of product and brand management principles
**Key Relationships:**
- Brand Dilution (Lecture 8) is the risk of brand extensions that violate brand mantra constraints identified in Lecture 7 — overextension erodes the meaning behind the equity
- New Luxury Paradigm's Three D's (Desirability/Deliverability/Differentiation) echo the Three Innovation Challenge from Lecture 1, completing a course-level loop
- Scarcity as a luxury lever explains why brand accessibility strategies must be managed carefully — distribution that maximizes short-term revenue can destroy long-term premium positioning
- Perceptual Map (Lecture 8) visualizes repositioning opportunity — moving the brand relative to competitors on two strategically selected axes

## Cross-Cutting Themes

### Theme 1: Customer-Centricity
**Appears in:** Lectures 2, 3, 4, 5, 7 (and supplementary notes 11, 12, 14, 16)
**Evolution:** Begins with VoC and latent needs (Lecture 2) → operationalized as needs statements and product specifications (Lecture 3) → validated through concept testing (Lecture 4) → quantified through conjoint analysis (Lecture 5) → embedded in brand equity as brand resonance and loyalty (Lecture 7) → extended to luxury's cultural resonance (Lecture 8)
**Key Insight:** Every tool in the NPD arc is designed to replace team assumptions about customers with actual customer data. The Keller brand pyramid is itself a customer perception model — brand equity exists in customers' minds, not in the product.

### Theme 2: Decision-Making Under Uncertainty
**Appears in:** Lectures 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 8
**Evolution:** Lecture 1 introduces NPD failure rates motivating systematic processes → Lecture 2 provides environmental scanning tools (PESTEL, Porter's, RWW) → Lecture 3 funnels ideas through screening/scoring → Lecture 4 adds Go/No-Go gates and sensitivity analysis → Lecture 5 quantifies preference uncertainty via part-worths → Lecture 6 uses RCTs to establish causality → Lecture 8 addresses brand equity decay risk over time
**Key Insight:** The entire course toolkit is a risk-reduction system. Every framework — RWW, Pugh matrix, conjoint analysis, CPM — exists to improve decision quality when outcomes are uncertain and resources are finite.

### Theme 3: Quantitative Tools in Service of Judgment
**Appears in:** Lectures 2, 3, 5, 6, 10 (supplementary)
**Evolution:** Market sizing introduces guesstimation under uncertainty (Lecture 2) → Concept scoring introduces weighted multi-criteria decision making (Lecture 3) → Conjoint analysis introduces regression-based preference modeling (Lecture 5) → CPM introduces network-based scheduling optimization (Lecture 6)
**Key Insight:** The exam tests calculations, but the calculations are only meaningful when you understand what management question each tool answers. Attribute importance percentages answer "which feature should we invest in?"; slack time answers "where can we accept scheduling flexibility?"; SOM answers "is this opportunity financially worth pursuing?"

### Theme 4: Frameworks as Shared Communication Tools
**Appears in:** Lectures 2, 3, 6, 7, 8
**Evolution:** PESTEL/Porter's Five Forces structure a complex environment into a format teams can debate → Pugh Matrix creates a shared, documented basis for concept selection → WBS/Network Diagram communicates a project schedule across functions → CBBE Pyramid aligns a brand team on equity status → Perceptual Map frames a repositioning conversation
**Key Insight:** Most course frameworks are as valuable for aligning cross-functional teams as they are for producing analysis. A shared framework reduces ambiguity, makes assumptions transparent, and accelerates decisions.

### Supplementary Reading: Slack Launch Strategy
**Source Type:** Supplementary Reading
**Related Lectures:** Lectures 2, 4 (opportunity identification, concept testing/validation)
**Core Concepts:** Bottoms-Up Adoption, Category Creation, Buchheit Principle, Magic Number (2,000 messages → 93% retention), Preview Release, Word-of-Mouth Growth
**Key Insight:** Slack's success exemplifies identifying a latent need (team communication without email overload) through internal use before external launch — the NPD process in startup form, with product-market fit defined by a specific behavioral threshold.

### Case Study: Gucci Brand Revitalization
**Source Type:** Case Study
**Related Lectures:** Lectures 7, 8 (brand equity, brand sustainability)
**Core Concepts:** Brand Revitalization, Brand Dilution, Culture of Purpose, Shadow Committee, Staples-Trendy Portfolio Split, Recommerce, DTC Strategy, Kering
**Key Insight:** Gucci's turnaround under Alessandro Michele demonstrates that brand equity can be rebuilt through cultural repositioning even after severe dilution from mass licensing — the luxury brand levers (scarcity, cultural narrative, gender-fluid identity) can be more powerful than any single product decision.

### Supplementary Reading: Product Concept Selection Methods
**Source Type:** Supplementary Reading
**Related Lectures:** Lecture 3 (concept screening and scoring)
**Core Concepts:** Concept Screening Matrix, Concept Scoring Matrix, Pugh Matrix, Datum, Sensitivity Analysis, Dominance Effect, Group Consensus, Secret Ballot Voting, Groupthink, Weighted Criteria Matrix
**Key Insight:** Surveys all 10 concept selection methods (from informal gut-feel to structured Pugh Matrix) and argues that systematic methods are superior because they are documented, collaborative, and resistant to groupthink — informal methods produce low quality and low team buy-in.
