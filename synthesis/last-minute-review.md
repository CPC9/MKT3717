## Lecture 1: Course Introduction

### Must-Remember Facts
- **Product:** Anything offered to a market for attention, acquisition, use, or consumption that might satisfy a need or want.
- **Brand:** Built through consumer perceptions, expectations, and experiences -- "companies make products and consumers make brands."
- **Product Manager:** Cross-functional role responsible for vision, strategy, design, and execution of a product's life cycle.
- **Brand Manager:** Advocate of the organization who cultivates brand image and equity to sustain product success over time.
- **New Product Failure Rate:** ~40% of new products fail (research evidence); press sometimes quotes up to 90%.
- **Six Types of New Products:** New-to-the-world, new-to-the-firm, additions to existing lines, improvements/revisions, repositioning, cost reductions.
- **Three Innovation Challenge:** Successful innovation = People (desirable) + Business (viable) + Technical (feasible).
- **Five-Phase NPD Process:** Opportunity Identification > Concept Generation > Concept/Project Evaluation > Development > Launch.
- **Best Firms:** Top 25% need only 4.5 ideas per success (vs. 11.4 for the rest), achieve 82% success rate, derive ~48% of sales from new products.

## Lecture 2: Opportunity Identification & Concept Generation

### Must-Remember Facts
- **RWW Framework:** Is it Real? Can we Win? Is it Worth it? -- maps to Desirable, Feasible, Viable.
- **PESTEL:** Political, Economic, Social, Technological, Environmental, Legal -- analyzes the **general** (macro) environment.
- **Porter's Five Forces:** Threat of new entrants, bargaining power of buyers, bargaining power of suppliers, threat of substitutes, intensity of rivalry -- analyzes the **industry** (operating) environment.
- **TAM/SAM/SOM:** Total Addressable Market > Serviceable Addressable Market > Serviceable Obtainable Market.
- **Explicit Needs:** Easy to express, likely already fulfilled.
- **Latent Needs:** Customers cannot articulate; greatest innovation potential (e.g., P&G Swiffer -- $100M in first 4 months).
- **Needs Statement Guidelines:** "What" not "How," specific, positive, attribute of the product, avoid "must"/"should."
- **Product Specification = Metric + Value + Units.**
- **Post-It Note:** Technology-push innovation; 12 years from invention to market; latent need no one could articulate.

### Key Formulas

| Formula | Variables | When to Use |
|---------|-----------|-------------|
| `Market Size = # Customers x Avg Units/Customer x Avg Price/Unit` | Customers = Population x Filter %; Units = Frequency x Qty | Estimating TAM/SAM/SOM in market sizing exercises |
| `# Customers = Baseline Population x Filter Ratio (%)` | Chain of successive filters narrowing the population | Breaking down who actually buys |

### Critical Distinctions

| Explicit Needs | vs. | Latent Needs |
|----------------|-----|--------------|
| Easy for customers to express | | Customers cannot articulate them |
| Widely known, likely already fulfilled | | Not yet understood or addressed |
| Incremental improvement territory | | Breakthrough innovation territory |
| Discovered through surveys/interviews | | Discovered through ethnography/observation |

### Common Exam Traps
> **Remember:** PESTEL is for the **general** environment; Porter's Five Forces is for the **industry** environment. Do not confuse which framework applies where.

## Lecture 3: Concept Screening & Testing

### Must-Remember Facts
- **Product Concept** requires at least **2 of 3 inputs:** Form, Technology, Benefit/Need.
- **Need-first** concept generation is the **least risky** path.
- **Concept Screening (Pugh Method):** Quick first pass; compares concepts to a reference using +/0/-; unweighted; eliminates weak concepts.
- **Concept Scoring (Weighted Matrix):** Detailed second pass; weighted criteria x numerical ratings; produces ranked total scores.
- **Goal of concept evaluation:** Create and develop the **best** concept (combine and refine), not merely select the top scorer.
- **JTBD Statement:** "When [situation], I want to [motivation], so I can [desired outcome]."
- **Specification Freeze:** Early freeze enables optimization; late freeze enables market responsiveness.
- **Marginal Value:** Minimum acceptable performance. **Ideal Value:** Aspirational best-in-class target.

### Key Formulas

| Formula | Variables | When to Use |
|---------|-----------|-------------|
| `Screening Net Score = (# of "+") - (# of "-")` | +/0/- ratings vs. reference concept | First-pass narrowing of many concepts |
| `Weighted Score = SUM(Weight_i x Rating_ij)` | Weight_i = criterion importance (sum to 1.0); Rating_ij = concept score (1-5 scale) | Detailed concept comparison after screening |

### Critical Distinctions

| Concept Screening | vs. | Concept Scoring |
|-------------------|-----|-----------------|
| Qualitative (+/0/-) | | Quantitative (numerical ratings) |
| Unweighted criteria | | Weighted criteria |
| Fast, rough first pass | | Rigorous, detailed second pass |
| Eliminates weak concepts | | Ranks and selects best concepts |
| Many concepts in | | Few surviving concepts in |

### Common Exam Traps
> **Remember:** The concept scoring matrix can select the **"best average" product** -- one that scores adequately everywhere but excels nowhere. Always check if standout features from lower-ranked concepts can be borrowed.

## Lecture 4: Concept Testing Review

### Must-Remember Facts
- **Concept Testing** is a "stage of elimination" -- concepts failing on PIC criteria (unavailable technology, unfamiliar customers, wrong innovativeness, bad cost structure) are killed.
- **Testing evolves** across the PD process: qualitative concept testing (early) > prototype/alpha testing > quantitative market testing > beta testing > A/B testing (launch).
- **Sensitivity analysis** should always be performed on scoring matrix weights and ratings.
- **Different customer segments** may yield different concept rankings -- evaluate separately.
- **Brainstorming rules:** Defer judgment, build on others' ideas, encourage wild ideas, one conversation at a time, stay focused.

### Key Formulas

| Formula | Variables | When to Use |
|---------|-----------|-------------|
| `Total Weighted Score = SUM(Category Weight x Sub-weight x Rating)` | Category weights sum to 100%; sub-weights within categories; ratings per concept | Detailed concept scoring with sub-criteria |

### Critical Distinctions

(See Lecture 3 for the Screening vs. Scoring distinction, which is the primary testable comparison from this material.)

### Common Exam Traps
> **Remember:** Close scores in a concept scoring matrix (e.g., 594 vs. 587 vs. 585) mean the ranking is **sensitive to weight changes**. Never treat a narrow win as definitive.

## Lecture 5: Conjoint Analysis & Product Architecture

### Must-Remember Facts
- **Conjoint Analysis:** Decomposes overall product preference into part-worth utilities for individual attribute levels using regression.
- **Dummy Variable Coding:** One level per attribute is the **baseline** (omitted); all others get 0/1 dummies.
- **Intercept (B0):** Predicted preference when all attributes are at baseline levels.
- **Direction matters:** With rank data (1 = most preferred), a **negative** coefficient = **more preferred**. With rating data (higher = better), a **positive** coefficient = more preferred.
- **R-squared:** Proportion of variance explained (salsa model = 0.956; notebook = 1.0).
- **Three conditions for conjoint:** (1) Product value = sum of attribute values, (2) attributes are independent, (3) attributes are discrete and unambiguous.
- **Bad candidates for conjoint:** Fragrances, clothing aesthetics, meal ingredients, subjective attributes ("sporty").
- **Modular Architecture:** Interchangeable modules with standardized interfaces; enables mass customization and delayed differentiation.
- **Delayed Differentiation (Postponement):** Benetton reduced unsold stock from 33% to 8% by dyeing finished garments instead of yarn.
- **Poka-Yoke:** Fail-safe design to prevent user errors (e.g., battery compartment shape).

### Key Formulas

| Formula | Variables | When to Use |
|---------|-----------|-------------|
| `Rank_i = B0 + B1*X1 + B2*X2 + ... + Bk*Xk` | B0 = intercept; Bk = coefficient for each dummy; Xk = 0/1 dummy variables | Predicting preference for any product profile |
| `Relative Importance (%) = Range_A / SUM(all ranges) x 100` | Range_A = max coeff - min coeff for attribute A (baseline = 0) | Determining which attribute matters most |
| `WTP = (Coeff_X / |Coeff_Price|) x Price Increment` | Coeff_X = attribute level coefficient; Price Increment = dollar gap between price levels | Converting preference into dollar value |
| `Total Profiles = L1 x L2 x ... x Ln` | L1...Ln = number of levels per attribute | Calculating full factorial design size |

### Critical Distinctions

| Modular Architecture | vs. | Integral Architecture |
|----------------------|-----|----------------------|
| Each module maps to one function | | Functions distributed across coupled components |
| Standardized interfaces | | Tightly coupled interfaces |
| Enables variety and customization | | Enables performance optimization |
| Independent module upgrades | | Compact form factors |
| Higher manufacturing flexibility | | Higher performance ceiling |

### Common Exam Traps
> **Remember:** When computing Relative Importance, the **baseline level has a coefficient of 0** -- do not forget to include it as the min (or max) when calculating the range. Also, do not confuse the coefficient's magnitude with its statistical significance.

## Lecture 6: Market Experiments & Project Management

### Must-Remember Facts
- **Correlation is not causation.** Only experiments with random assignment establish causal effects.
- **Counterfactual:** What would have happened without the intervention; control groups serve as the proxy.
- **A/B Testing:** One variable, two versions, random assignment, measure outcome.
- **Factorial Design:** Tests multiple factors simultaneously; reveals **interaction effects** that separate A/B tests miss. A 2x2 = 4 conditions.
- **Endowment Effect:** People value items more once they own them (~2x); supports free trial and sampling strategies.
- **p < 0.05** = statistically significant; confidence interval not containing zero = significant.
- **WBS:** Hierarchical decomposition of project scope into work packages.
- **Forward Pass:** ES = max(EF of all predecessors); EF = ES + Duration. Starting activities: ES = 0.
- **Backward Pass:** LF = min(LS of all successors); LS = LF - Duration. Final activity: LF = project duration.
- **Critical Path:** Longest path through the network; all activities have Slack = 0; any delay here delays the project.
- **Crashing Rule:** Only crash critical path activities; crash the one with the **lowest crash cost per unit time** first; re-check the critical path after each crash.

### Key Formulas

| Formula | Variables | When to Use |
|---------|-----------|-------------|
| `EF = ES + Duration` | ES = Earliest Start; Duration = activity time | Forward pass calculation |
| `ES = max(EF of all predecessors)` | EF values of predecessor activities | Forward pass -- finding when an activity can start |
| `LS = LF - Duration` | LF = Latest Finish; Duration = activity time | Backward pass calculation |
| `LF = min(LS of all successors)` | LS values of successor activities | Backward pass -- finding deadline for an activity |
| `Slack = LS - ES = LF - EF` | LS, ES (or LF, EF) for the activity | Identifying critical path (Slack = 0) and schedule flexibility |
| `Crash Cost/Unit Time = (Crash Cost - Normal Cost) / (Normal Duration - Crash Duration)` | Costs and durations at normal and crashed levels | Deciding which activity to crash first (pick lowest) |

### Critical Distinctions

| Correlation | vs. | Causation |
|-------------|-----|-----------|
| Two variables move together | | One variable directly produces change in the other |
| Observed in any data | | Requires experimental design with random assignment |
| May be driven by confounding variables | | Confounds controlled through randomization |
| Sufficient for prediction | | Required for intervention decisions |

### Common Exam Traps
> **Remember:** ES uses the **maximum** EF of predecessors (all must finish). LF uses the **minimum** LS of successors (cannot delay any). Getting max/min backwards is the most common scheduling mistake. Also: after crashing one activity, the **critical path may shift** -- always recalculate.

## Lecture 7: Brand Management Overview

### Must-Remember Facts
- **Brand (AMA):** A name, term, sign, symbol, or design that identifies and differentiates a seller's goods from competitors.
- **Customer-Based Brand Equity:** The differential effect of brand knowledge on consumer response to marketing.
- **Brand Knowledge = Brand Awareness + Brand Image.**
- **Brand Recognition:** Aided -- "Have you heard of X?" (lower bar).
- **Brand Recall:** Unaided -- "Name a brand of running shoes" (higher bar). Top-of-mind = first recalled.
- **Brand Image:** Strength, favorability, and uniqueness of associations in consumer memory.
- **Brand Elements:** Name, logo, symbol, character, slogan, jingle, packaging. Criteria: memorable, meaningful, likable, transferable, adaptable, protectable.
- **Brand Positioning:** Target consumer + competitive frame of reference + POPs + PODs.
- **Brand Mantra:** Emotional modifier + Descriptive modifier + Brand function (e.g., Nike: "Authentic Athletic Performance").
- **Brand Mantra is NOT a tagline** -- it is an internal strategic document.
- **Keller's Brand Resonance Pyramid (bottom to top):**
  1. **Identity** -- Salience (who are you?)
  2. **Meaning** -- Performance (rational) + Imagery (emotional) (what are you?)
  3. **Response** -- Judgments (rational) + Feelings (emotional) (what about you?)
  4. **Resonance** -- Loyalty, attachment, community, engagement (what about you and me?)
- **Brand Feelings (6 types):** Warmth, fun, excitement, security, social approval, self-respect.
- **Brand Resonance (4 dimensions):** Behavioral loyalty, attitudinal attachment, sense of community, active engagement.

### Key Formulas

| Formula | Variables | When to Use |
|---------|-----------|-------------|
| `Brand Equity = f(Brand Awareness, Brand Image)` | Awareness = recognition + recall; Image = strength, favorability, uniqueness of associations | Conceptual understanding of what drives brand equity |

### Critical Distinctions

| Brand Recognition | vs. | Brand Recall |
|-------------------|-----|--------------|
| Aided identification (brand as cue) | | Unaided retrieval (category/need as cue) |
| "Have you heard of X?" | | "What brands of Y can you name?" |
| Lower bar, easier to achieve | | Higher bar, stronger awareness |
| Matters at point of purchase (shelf) | | Matters when consumer thinks of a need |

| Points of Parity (POPs) | vs. | Points of Difference (PODs) |
|--------------------------|-----|-----------------------------|
| Shared with competitors | | Unique to the brand |
| Necessary for category legitimacy | | Basis of competitive advantage |
| "Must-haves" to be considered | | "Reasons to choose" this brand |
| Category POPs + Competitive POPs | | Must be desirable and deliverable |

### Common Exam Traps
> **Remember:** The Brand Resonance Pyramid must be built **bottom-up**. You cannot achieve resonance without first establishing identity, then meaning, then response. Skipping levels creates fragile equity. Also: behavioral loyalty alone (repeat purchase) is NOT resonance -- it may just be habit.

## Lecture 8: Brand Sustainability & Personal Branding

### Must-Remember Facts
- **Disruptive Repositioning:** Bold, fundamental shift in positioning (vs. incremental). Gucci moved from classic/older to contemporary/younger under Michele.
- **Brand Dilution Risk:** Extending into accessible categories (e.g., beauty) may weaken perceived exclusivity.
- **Old Luxury:** Status symbols, passive engagement, ownership-focused, destination shopping, resisted online.
- **New Luxury:** Self-expression, active co-creation, experience-driven, omnichannel, fully digital.
- **Millennials + Gen Z** projected to be ~70% of luxury market by 2025.
- **Online luxury sales** nearly doubled: $37.3B (2019) to $70.1B (2021), projected 30% share by 2025.
- **Geographic Concentration Risk:** Chinese consumers = 33% of luxury purchases (2019), projected 43% by 2025.
- **Gucci's Shadow Committee:** Reverse-mentoring committee of employees under 30 advising senior leadership.
- **Gucci Revenue Growth:** EUR 3.9B (2015) to EUR 9.6B (2019), ~20% CAGR.
- **Consumption as Signaling:** Luxury purchases communicate status, identity, and values to others.
- **Personal Branding = Passion (desirable) + Skills (feasible) + Market (viable)** -- same Desirable-Feasible-Viable framework.
- **Personal Brand Mantra:** Same structure as corporate -- emotional modifier + descriptive modifier + brand function.

### Key Formulas

No new formulas in this lecture.

### Critical Distinctions

| Old Luxury | vs. | New Luxury |
|------------|-----|------------|
| Status symbol | | Self-expression |
| Passive customer engagement | | Active co-creation |
| Ownership-focused consumption | | Experience-driven consumption |
| Destination shopping (in-store only) | | Omnichannel browsing |
| Resisted online channels | | Fully integrated digital presence |

### Common Exam Traps
> **Remember:** Heritage and innovation are **not mutually exclusive**. Gucci preserved core identity elements (double-G monogram, green-red-green stripe) while radically refreshing creative expression. Repositioning does not mean abandoning heritage.

## "Don't Forget" Checklist

- [ ] **Conjoint direction:** With rank data (1 = best), negative coefficient = more preferred. With rating data (higher = better), positive coefficient = more preferred. Know which scale is being used before interpreting.
- [ ] **Baseline level coefficient is always 0** in conjoint -- include it when calculating ranges for Relative Importance.
- [ ] **ES uses MAX of predecessor EFs** (all must finish); **LF uses MIN of successor LSs** (cannot delay any). Getting max/min reversed is the #1 scheduling error.
- [ ] **After crashing an activity, the critical path may shift.** Always recalculate all paths before crashing the next activity.
- [ ] **Only crash critical path activities** -- crashing non-critical activities does not shorten the project.
- [ ] **Brand Resonance Pyramid is built bottom-up:** Identity > Meaning > Response > Resonance. Cannot skip levels.
- [ ] **Brand mantra is internal; tagline is external.** They should align but are not the same thing.
- [ ] **Concept Screening then Scoring** -- screening is for narrowing (many concepts), scoring is for deciding (few concepts). Two-stage process.
- [ ] **PESTEL = general/macro environment; Porter's Five Forces = industry/operating environment.** Different scope, complementary use.
- [ ] **Modular design benefits under demand uncertainty:** product variety without forecasting specific configs, reduced inventory risk, faster response, component reuse.
- [ ] **p-value < 0.05 = statistically significant.** A non-significant coefficient (like Red color p = 0.138 in the salsa example) should not drive product design decisions.
- [ ] **Types of new products:** New-to-the-world, new-to-the-firm, line extensions, improvements/revisions, repositioning, cost reductions. Know examples of each.
