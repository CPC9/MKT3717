Course Narrative

MKT 3717 follows the lifecycle of a market offering from opportunity to managed brand. The first act (Lectures 1–6) covers product management: identifying opportunities, evaluating concepts, designing product architecture, validating through experiments, and managing project execution. The recurring thread is disciplined uncertainty reduction — each lecture introduces a more rigorous evaluation tool.

The second act (Lectures 7–8) pivots to brand management: building brand equity in consumers' minds and sustaining it over time. The course closes with personal branding, applying the same Desirable-Viable-Feasible lens to individuals.

L5: Conjoint Analysis for Product Design
▶
Driving Question

How can we quantitatively determine which product attributes matter most to consumers and design the optimal configuration?

Core Concepts
  • Conjoint Analysis — Decompose consumer preferences into part-worth utilities
  • Dummy Variable Coding — Encode categorical attributes for regression
  • Part-Worth Utilities — Regression coefficients measuring attribute-level value
  • Relative Importance — (Range of attribute / Sum of ranges) × 100%
  • Willingness to Pay — Convert utility differences to dollar values via price coefficient
  • Product Architecture — Modular vs. integral, mass customization, delayed differentiation
  • Poka-Yoke — Fail-safe design principles
Connections
  • Conjoint → Scoring (L3-L4) — Both evaluate concepts; conjoint uses consumer data instead of team judgment
  • Regression → Experiments (L6) — Same statistical tools interpret experimental results
  • WTP → Brand Equity (L7) — Brand-driven price premiums use same valuation logic

Innovation Challenge (Desirable / Viable / Feasible)
▶

Appears in: Lectures 1, 2, 3, 4, 6, 7, 8

Introduced as a Venn diagram in L1. Operationalized as RWW in L2. Embedded in concept evaluation criteria (L3–4). Resurfaces through the project "iron triangle" (L6). In L7, brand building requires a desirable product foundation. Comes full circle in L8 as Passion-Skills-Market for personal branding.

Key Insight: This is the most portable framework in the course. Every evaluation decision — from screening opportunities to repositioning luxury brands to crafting a career strategy — can be tested against these three dimensions.

Structured Evaluation Methods (Progressive Rigor)
▶

Appears in: Lectures 2, 3, 4, 5, 6

Methods progress from qualitative (PESTEL, RWW) to semi-quantitative (screening/scoring matrices) to fully quantitative (conjoint, A/B testing, factorial design). The progression moves from expert judgment toward empirical consumer data.

Key Insight: No single method is sufficient. Cheap, fast, qualitative methods filter early; expensive, quantitative methods handle high-stakes decisions. The goal is systematic uncertainty reduction, not finding the "right answer."

Customer Understanding (Needs, Jobs, Knowledge)
▶

Appears in: Lectures 2, 3, 5, 7, 8

Begins with customer need analysis (L2), deepens with JTBD (L3), quantifies preferences via conjoint (L5), shifts to brand knowledge and associative memory (L7), and shows how associations evolve over time (L8).

Key Insight: Whether building products or brands, the fundamental challenge is understanding what exists in the customer's mind. The methods differ (ethnography vs. brand tracking, conjoint vs. mental maps), but value is always defined by the customer, not the firm.

Product-to-Brand Lifecycle
▶

Appears in: All lectures

L1 establishes "companies make products, consumers make brands." L2–6 build the "body" (product). L7 builds the "soul" (brand). L8 keeps both alive. Fyre Festival (L1) and Gucci (L7–8) bookend the theme: festival had brand without product; Gucci had both heritage and reinvention.

Key Insight: Product and brand management are sequential phases of the same lifecycle. A brand cannot exist without a viable product, and a product's long-term success depends on brand equity. The course's two-act structure mirrors this lifecycle.
