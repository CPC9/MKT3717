Lecture 1: Course Introduction

Must-Remember Facts

  • NPD Process (Crawford): 5 phases: Opportunity ID/Selection → Concept Generation → Concept Evaluation → Development → Launch
  • Three Innovation Challenge: Desirability (do customers want it?), Feasibility (technically achievable?), Viability (business profitable?) — all three must be YES to proceed
  • Product Manager vs. Brand Manager: PM owns product roadmap and cross-functional coordination; Brand Manager owns brand equity, positioning, and communications
  • Crawford's 6 Product Types: New-to-world, New product line, Addition to existing line (line extension), Improvement/revision, Repositioned, Cost reduction
  • Generic PD Process: Opportunity → Concepts → Screen/Score → Test → Launch
  • Rapid Iteration Process: Build-Measure-Learn cycles (lean startup approach)
  • Spiral Development: Repeated design-build-test loops for complex/uncertain products

Critical Distinctions

Push InnovationPull Innovation
Technology-led; firm invents and finds marketCustomer-led; firm responds to identified need
Post-It Note, PenicillinMost line extensions, iPhone SE
New-to-WorldLine Extension
No predecessor product exists in any firmAddition to existing firm portfolio for same market
iPhone (2007), SpotifyNew Oreo flavor, detergent pod format
⚠ Remember: A "repositioned product" targets a NEW market with essentially the SAME product. Do not confuse with "product improvement" (same market, better product) or "new product line" (new category for the firm). Ace detergent pod = line extension (new format, same market).

Lecture 2: NPD Part 1 — Opportunity Identification

Must-Remember Facts

  • RWW Framework: Real (genuine need + technical feasibility), Win (competitive advantage), Worth It (financial return) — all three required to pursue
  • PESTEL: Political/Legal, Economic, Social/Cultural, Technological — each factor rated 1 (unfavorable) to 5 (favorable) for a specific product-market context
  • Porter's Five Forces: New Entrants, Buyer Power, Supplier Power, Substitutes, Rivalry among competitors — rated Favorable/Moderate/Unfavorable
  • TAM/SAM/SOM: TAM = total market at 100% share; SAM = realistic target segment; SOM = credibly capturable share; SOM is the planning figure
  • Latent vs. Stated Needs: Stated = what customers say they want; Latent = what they actually need but cannot articulate
  • Needs Statement Rules: Describe "what" not "how"; specific; positive; attribute of the product; avoid "must"/"should"

Key Formulas

Market Size = #Customers × Frequency × Qty/purchase × Price
#Customers = Population × relevant ratio — use for TAM guesstimation
SOM = TAM × segment% × capture%
segment% = SAM/TAM fraction; capture% = realistic market share — use for planning revenue

Critical Distinctions

PESTELPorter's Five Forces
Macro/general environment (outside the industry)Operating/industry environment
Political, Economic, Social, Technological factorsBuyer, Supplier, Rivalry, Entrants, Substitutes
Rates 1–5 (favorable)Rates Favorable/Moderate/Unfavorable
"Is it Real?" and "Is it Worth It?""Can We Win?"
⚠ Remember: SOM is the operationally relevant planning number — NOT TAM. Never cite TAM as your target market. TAM assumes 100% market share, which is never realistic. Always show the SAM → SOM reduction with a defensible rationale.

Lecture 3: NPD Part 2 — Concept Screening and Testing

Must-Remember Facts

  • Two-Stage Funnel: Concept Screening Matrix (coarse, +/0/−) → Concept Scoring Matrix (fine, weighted score)
  • Screening Matrix: Rates concepts as better (+), same (0), or worse (−) vs. a reference concept on each criterion; eliminates clearly inferior concepts
  • Scoring Matrix: Weights × Ratings = Weighted Score per criterion; sum all for total score; rank concepts by total
  • Combine and Improve: Never auto-select the highest scorer — always try to combine best features of top concepts
  • Criteria Weights: MUST come from consumer surveys, not internal team judgment
  • Sensitivity Analysis: Test whether top ranking holds if uncertain weights shift within a plausible range
  • Product Specification: Translates Needs Statement to measurable metric + target value

Key Formulas

Total Score = Σ(w ᵢ × rᵢ)
wᵢ = importance weight from consumer survey (sums to 100); rᵢ = concept rating on criterion i — use for ranking concepts in scoring matrix

Critical Distinctions

Concept Screening (Pugh)Concept Scoring
Relative to reference concept (+/0/−)Absolute weighted score
Coarse filter — eliminates clearly inferiorFine discriminator — ranks surviving concepts
Fast, qualitativeSlower, quantitative
⚠ Remember: The concept scoring matrix does NOT produce a final decision. You MUST apply the "combine and improve" step and run sensitivity analysis. The highest-scoring concept is a starting point, not a guaranteed winner.

Lecture 4: Concept Testing Review

Must-Remember Facts

  • Alpha Testing: Internal technical test — does the product work as designed? (pre-customer)
  • Beta Testing: External customer test — does the product satisfy real users in real conditions? (pre-launch)
  • A/B Testing (product stage): Comparison of two concept versions with customers — establishes relative preference
  • PIC (Product Innovation Charter): Strategic document defining scope, goals, constraints, and success criteria before development begins; the reference document for Go/No-Go gates
  • Go/No-Go Decision Gate: Formal checkpoint at end of each NPD phase; produces proceed/kill/hold decision based on PIC criteria
  • Brainstorming Rules: No criticism during ideation; quantity over quality; build on others' ideas; wild ideas welcome

Critical Distinctions

Alpha TestBeta Test
Internal (team-run)External (real users)
Technical performance checkUser experience and satisfaction
Earlier in developmentLater in development, closer to launch
A/B Test (Lec 4 — concept validation)A/B Test (Lec 6 — market experiment)
Compares two concept versionsEstablishes causal market impact
Preference-based comparisonOutcome metric: conversion, revenue, retention
Concept evaluation stagePost-development market validation stage
⚠ Remember: A/B testing appears in two lecture contexts with different purposes. In Lecture 4, it compares two product concepts (which do users prefer?). In Lecture 6, it establishes causality for a business metric change (did this change cause more purchases?). Distinguish by context.

Lecture 5: Conjoint Analysis for Product Design

Must-Remember Facts

  • Purpose: Decomposes consumer preference into attribute-level part-worth utilities using regression
  • Baseline Level: The omitted dummy variable level; all part-worths are relative to this omitted reference
  • Most Important Attribute: The attribute with the LARGEST range of part-worths
  • Relative Importance: Range for attribute ÷ Sum of all ranges × 100%; ALL percentages must sum to 100%
  • Rankings vs. Ratings: Lower rank number = MORE preferred; may need reverse coding (e.g., 9 − rank) so positive β = preferred
  • WTP: −(Part-worth of feature) ÷ (Part-worth of price per $1) — price must be a continuous variable
  • Modular Architecture: Standard interfaces + interchangeable modules → variety without full redesign
  • Postponement: Delay product differentiation as late as possible in supply chain to reduce finished-goods inventory risk

Key Formulas

Preferenceₐ = β₀ + Σ βₖ × Xₖₐ
βₖ = part-worth for level k; Xₖₐ = dummy (0/1) — estimating part-worths via OLS regression
Relative Importanceᵢ = Rangeᵢ / Σ(All Ranges) × 100%
Rangeᵢ = max(β) − min(β) for attribute i — identifying which attribute matters most
WTP = −β_feature / β_price_per_$1
β_price = regression coeff on continuous price — pricing decisions for new features
Avg Rank Method: Range = |Avg_rankA − Avg_rankB|
Avg rank per level = sum of ranks in profiles with that level ÷ # such profiles — when using rankings without regression

Critical Distinctions

Ratings-Based ConjointRankings-Based Conjoint
Preference score (1–10) is dependent variableRank number is dependent variable
Positive β = more preferredLower rank = more preferred; positive β may indicate LESS preferred level
Direct regression, no reverse coding neededOften reverse-code first (utility = max_rank + 1 − rank)
⚠ Remember: Relative importance = each attribute's range ÷ SUM OF ALL RANGES. Not divided by the maximum range. Not divided by total utility. Check that all importance values sum to exactly 100% before writing your answer.

Lecture 6: Market Experiment and Project Management

Must-Remember Facts

  • RCT: Randomized Controlled Trial — gold standard for causality; random assignment eliminates selection bias
  • Endowment Effect: People overvalue what they already own — makes self-selection into treatment biased; key reason random assignment is required
  • Factorial Design: Tests all combinations of multiple factors simultaneously; reveals interaction effects; more efficient than sequential A/B tests
  • WBS → Network Diagram → CPM: Decompose project → map dependencies → find critical path
  • Forward Pass: EF = ES + Duration; ES starts at 0; ES = max(EF of all predecessors) for subsequent nodes
  • Backward Pass: LS = LF − Duration; start at project end (LF = EF of final activity); LF = min(LS of all successors)
  • Slack = LS − ES = LF − EF → Critical path activities have Slack = 0
  • Project Crashing: Crash critical path activity with lowest Crash Cost Rate first; re-identify critical path after each step

Key Formulas

EF = ES + Duration
ES = 0 (first activity); or max(EF of predecessors) — forward pass
LS = LF − Duration
LF = EF of final activity (at start of backward pass); then min(LS of successors) — backward pass
Slack = LS − ES = LF − EF
Slack = 0 → on critical path
Crash Cost Rate = (Crash Cost − Normal Cost) / (Normal Duration − Crash Duration)
All in consistent $ and time units — deciding which activity to crash first

Critical Distinctions

CorrelationCausation
A and B move togetherA causes B (via experimental manipulation)
Can be spurious / confoundedRequires random assignment to treatment
Observational dataRCT or A/B experiment
Critical PathNon-Critical Path
Longest path; Slack = 0Shorter path; Slack > 0
Any delay = project delayCan be delayed up to Slack amount without impact
Crash here to shorten projectCrashing here does NOT shorten project duration
⚠ Remember: The critical path is the LONGEST path — not the shortest, not the cheapest. Critical path activities have ZERO slack. Crashing a non-critical activity wastes money and has no effect on project duration. Always re-identify the critical path after each crash step.

Lecture 7: Brand Management Overview

Must-Remember Facts

  • CBBE Pyramid (bottom to top): Salience → Performance + Imagery → Judgments + Feelings → Resonance
  • Salience: Brand identity — who are you? (aided/unaided recall, recognition)
  • Performance: How well does the product meet functional needs? (reliability, durability, efficiency, style)
  • Imagery: What are the brand's intangible associations? (personality, values, user profile, heritage)
  • Judgments: Consumer personal opinions (quality, credibility, consideration, superiority)
  • Feelings: Consumer emotional responses (warmth, fun, excitement, security, social approval, self-respect)
  • Resonance: Deepest level — behavioral loyalty + attitudinal attachment + sense of community + active engagement
  • POPs: Points of Parity — attributes shared with competitors (table stakes; prevent disqualification from consideration)
  • PODs: Points of Difference — attributes uniquely associated with this brand (true differentiators that drive active choice)
  • Brand Mantra: 3-word brand essence (e.g., Nike = "Authentic Athletic Performance") — guides all brand decisions
  • MEMORABL: Brand elements must be Memorable, Meaningful, Likeable, Transferable, Adaptable, Protectable

Critical Distinctions

POPs (Points of Parity)PODs (Points of Difference)
Shared with all competitors in the categoryUnique to this brand
Necessary but not sufficient for preferenceSufficient to drive active choice
Prevent disqualification (entry requirement)Drive selection over alternatives
Brand PerformanceBrand Imagery
Functional product attributesIntangible / psychological associations
Product features, reliability, efficiencyPersonality, values, user profile, heritage
Rational evaluation by consumersEmotional and symbolic evaluation
⚠ Remember: POPs and PODs are both required. A brand cannot skip POPs and only advertise PODs — consumers will not consider a brand that fails to meet category POPs. Establish parity first, then differentiate. Also: CBBE Pyramid is strictly bottom-up — you cannot build Resonance without first establishing Salience, Performance/Imagery, and Judgments/Feelings.

Lecture 8: Brand Sustainability and Personal Branding

Must-Remember Facts

  • Brand Dilution: Brand equity weakened by overextension into categories inconsistent with the brand promise (violates brand mantra)
  • Brand Repositioning: Changing the brand's position in consumers' minds — requires sustained communication effort, not a one-time campaign
  • New Luxury Paradigm: Mass-class consumers trade up to luxury for emotional/experiential satisfaction in selected categories
  • Three D's (luxury): Desirability (cultural aspiration), Deliverability (supply chain/quality), Differentiation (distinct from competitors)
  • Scarcity Principle: Artificial or natural scarcity increases perceived desirability; over-distribution destroys luxury premium
  • Perceptual Map: 2-axis plot showing brand positions relative to competitors; identifies white space for repositioning
  • Recommerce: Selling pre-owned products — extends brand reach while managing brand equity by controlling secondary market
  • Personal Brand: Consistent professional identity communicated across platforms; follows same CBBE building principles

Critical Distinctions

Brand ExtensionBrand Dilution
Leverages equity into a new categoryWeakens equity through inconsistent extension
Can strengthen brand if successfulErodes consumer trust in brand promise
Requires brand mantra fitOccurs precisely when mantra fit is absent
RepositioningBrand Refresh
Changes perceived category or target segmentUpdates visual identity without changing position
Requires sustained campaign investmentCan be a single design update event
Higher strategic riskLower risk, mostly aesthetic
⚠ Remember: Repositioning is not the same as a brand refresh. A new logo is a refresh. Moving from "affordable mass market" to "ultra-premium exclusivity" requires repositioning — changing consumer beliefs through sustained, consistent messaging over time.

Gucci Case Study

  • Pre-Michele: Brand diluted through 600+ licensees; required aggressive brand purification
  • Michele's Key Moves: Cut 50% of product lines; launched "Shadow Committee" of young creative talent; embraced gender-fluid aesthetics; Culture of Purpose over fashion cycles
  • Takeaway: Brand equity can be rebuilt through cultural repositioning even after severe dilution

Slack Launch Strategy

  • Magic Number: Teams that exchange 2,000 messages have 93% retention — this specific threshold defined product-market fit
  • Buchheit Principle: Build something 10 people love, not something 1 million people mildly like
  • Category Creation: Slack had to define "team messaging" as a category before demand existed — pure technology push into a latent need

Post-It Note History

  • Defect-to-Feature Reframing: Spencer Silver's "failed" weak adhesive became the defining feature — the latent need existed; the product discovered it
  • Technology Push: No market research identified the need; the product preceded customer awareness
  • Champion Role: Art Fry and Spencer Silver persisted through corporate inertia to reach launch

Concept Selection Methods

  • 10 Methods range from: Gut feel / intuition (lowest quality) → Pugh Matrix + Scoring (highest quality)
  • Best Methods: Pugh Matrix + Concept Scoring are most defensible, collaborative, and resistant to groupthink
  • Groupthink Risk: Informal methods (gut feel, group consensus) produce low-quality outcomes; structured voting and scoring prevent it
