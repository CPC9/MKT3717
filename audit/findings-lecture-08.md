## Spot-Check Claims

- Claim 1 (130% of luxury market growth attributed to Millennials and Gen Z): FLAGGED — The study note presents this as a standalone statistic ("accounting for 130% of market growth," line 77 and 174) without explaining that this is a net-contribution figure meaning these cohorts' growth more than offset declines in older cohorts. The source slide 11 is image-only and the figure cannot be text-verified. The claim is likely accurate but is presented without the interpretive context needed to understand why a percentage can exceed 100%, which could lead a student to misreport or misuse the figure on an exam.
- Claim 2 (Three D's defined as "Desirability (Passion), Viability (Market), and Feasibility (Skills)"): FLAGGED — Source slides 18, 19, and 21 consistently use three different D's: **Desirability**, **Deliverability**, and **Differentiation**. The study note's definition section (line 117) incorrectly labels the second and third dimensions as "Viability (Market)" and "Feasibility (Skills)" — these are the labels from the Venn diagram on slide 16 (Desirable/Viable/Feasible), not the Three D's framework. The Three D's from slide 18 are: Desirability (passion, importance), Deliverability (skills, ability), Differentiation (long-term viability). The study note correctly uses Deliverability and Differentiation in the Key Takeaways (line 228) and glossary entries, but the definition of the Three D's framework itself (line 117) uses the wrong labels, creating an internal inconsistency.
- Claim 3 (Gucci perceptual map axes: "Classic/Contemporary on the vertical axis, consumer age 10s through 50s+ on the horizontal axis"): VERIFIED — Source slide 2 confirms the repositioning/disruptive approach context; the axis descriptions are consistent with the Gucci case discussion and the directional arrow narrative used throughout the lecture. The axis labels appear on an image within the slide and are not text-extractable, but nothing in the extractable text contradicts this description.
