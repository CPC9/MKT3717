## Overview

Lecture 8 addresses the challenge of managing brands over time, with a particular focus on sustaining brand equity in the luxury goods industry. The lecture uses Gucci as a central case study to explore how heritage brands can reposition themselves to remain relevant to younger consumers while preserving the exclusivity and prestige that define luxury. Key topics include the disruptive approach to repositioning, the evolution from "old luxury" to "new luxury," and the strategic tensions between accessibility and exclusivity.

The second major theme of the lecture is personal branding, which applies the same brand management frameworks taught throughout the course to individuals. Students learn to treat themselves as brands by identifying their passion (desirability), skills (feasibility), and market positioning (viability). The personal branding exercise asks students to create brand mantras, mental maps, and positioning statements for themselves, drawing on concepts like points-of-difference and brand resonance from earlier lectures.

The lecture also examines broader industry dynamics including the rapid growth of online luxury sales, the rising dominance of Millennials and Gen Z in luxury consumption, and the geographic shifts in global luxury markets. These trends create both opportunities and risks for established luxury houses, particularly around questions of digital channel strategy and over-reliance on specific consumer nationalities such as Chinese buyers.

## Key Concepts

### Brand Repositioning

#### Disruptive Repositioning
- **Definition**: A bold strategic shift in a brand's positioning that fundamentally changes its target segment, brand image, or value proposition, rather than making incremental adjustments.
- **Context**: Gucci's repositioning under creative directors Tom Ford, Frida Giannini, and Alessandro Michele illustrates how a luxury brand can move from classic to contemporary positioning on a perceptual map. The brand shifted its appeal from older, traditional consumers toward younger demographics (30s and below) with more contemporary, eclectic designs.
- **Relationships**: Connects to brand equity management (Lecture 7), positioning strategy, and the tension between brand continuity and relevance.

#### Brand Dilution Risk
- **Definition**: The potential weakening of a brand's core identity and perceived exclusivity when the brand extends into more accessible product categories or broadens its consumer base too aggressively.
- **Context**: Gucci's entry into the beauty segment raises the question of whether making the brand accessible to a larger consumer base dilutes its exclusive appeal. This is a fundamental tension in luxury brand management.
- **Relationships**: Related to brand extensions, brand equity, line extensions, and the scarcity principle in luxury marketing.

### The New Luxury Paradigm

#### Old Luxury vs. New Luxury
- **Definition**: A framework comparing traditional luxury business characteristics with the evolving modern luxury landscape across five dimensions: symbol, customer engagement, consumption patterns, shopping mindset, and online channel presence.
- **Context**: The luxury industry has undergone significant transformation over the past decade. Old luxury emphasized status symbols, exclusive in-store experiences, and limited distribution. New luxury emphasizes experiences, digital engagement, and broader accessibility while still maintaining aspirational appeal.
- **Relationships**: Connected to generational shifts in consumption, digital transformation, and brand repositioning strategies.

#### Consumption as Signaling
- **Definition**: The use of luxury goods purchases to communicate social status, identity, or group membership to others.
- **Context**: A key driver of luxury consumption where consumers pay significant premiums not just for product quality but for the social meaning the brand communicates. This signaling function is one of the characteristics that makes the luxury industry distinct from other industries.
- **Relationships**: Related to brand image, brand resonance, scarcity strategies, and the psychology of luxury consumption.

### Luxury Industry Characteristics

#### Scarcity vs. Environmental Consciousness
- **Definition**: The strategic tension between artificially limiting supply to maintain exclusivity and the growing consumer demand for sustainable, environmentally responsible business practices.
- **Context**: Luxury brands have traditionally used supply limitation as a core strategy, but modern consumers (especially Millennials and Gen Z) increasingly expect brands to demonstrate environmental consciousness, creating a strategic paradox.
- **Relationships**: Connected to brand sustainability, the new luxury paradigm, and generational shifts in consumer values.

#### Geographic Market Dependence
- **Definition**: The risk a brand faces when a disproportionate share of its revenue or growth comes from consumers of a single nationality or geographic region.
- **Context**: Chinese consumers have been dominant buyers of luxury brands like Gucci. By customer nationality, Chinese consumers accounted for 35% of luxury purchases in 2019 and were projected to reach 50% by 2025. Over-reliance on one nationality creates vulnerability to geopolitical, economic, and health crises (e.g., Covid-19).
- **Relationships**: Related to global brand strategy, market diversification, and risk management.

### Personal Branding

#### The Passion-Skills-Market Venn Diagram
- **Definition**: A three-circle model for personal branding where the intersection of what you are passionate about (desirable), what you are skilled at (feasible), and what the market values (viable) defines your ideal personal brand positioning.
- **Context**: This framework translates the classic product development framework (desirability, feasibility, viability) into a personal branding context, helping individuals find their unique value proposition.
- **Relationships**: Directly maps to the Desirable-Feasible-Viable framework from product development (Lecture 1), brand mantra, and points-of-difference.

#### Personal Brand Mantra
- **Definition**: A short, concise phrase that captures the essence of an individual's personal brand, structured with an emotional modifier, descriptive modifier, and brand function.
- **Context**: Just as companies develop brand mantras to guide their positioning, individuals can create personal brand mantras to articulate their unique value. The mantra should reflect target audience, self-analysis, and chosen points-of-difference.
- **Relationships**: Connected to brand mantra concept from brand management (Lecture 7), mental maps, and positioning statements.

#### Mental Map (Personal)
- **Definition**: A visual representation of all the associations, attributes, and perceptions that others hold about an individual, arranged as a spoke-and-hub diagram with the person at the center.
- **Context**: Illustrated with a McDonald's example showing how brand associations radiate outward (fast, friendly, convenience, cleanliness, etc.). In the personal branding exercise, students draft mental maps for each other and then update them based on shared brand mantras and plans.
- **Relationships**: Connected to brand associations, brand image, and brand knowledge structures from Keller's framework.

## Frameworks & Mental Models

### Old Luxury vs. New Luxury Comparison Framework

- **Visual Description**: A two-column comparison table with five rows. The left column represents "Old Luxury" and the right column represents "New Luxury." The five dimensions compared are: Symbol, Customer Engagement, Consumption, Shopping Mindset, and Online Channels. Old luxury is characterized by status-driven symbolism, passive customer engagement, ownership-focused consumption, destination shopping, and resistance to online channels. New luxury shifts toward self-expression, active co-creation, experience-driven consumption, omnichannel browsing, and fully integrated digital presence.
- **How to Apply**: When analyzing a luxury brand's strategy, map its current approach across all five dimensions. Identify where it sits on the old-to-new spectrum and determine which dimensions need to shift to remain competitive with younger consumers.
- **When to Use**: Use when evaluating a luxury brand's strategic positioning, planning a brand repositioning, or assessing whether a brand's strategy aligns with evolving consumer expectations.

### Repositioning Perceptual Map (Classic vs. Contemporary)

- **Visual Description**: A two-axis chart with "Classic" at the bottom and "Contemporary" at the top, and an age/demographic axis running left to right (10s, 20s, 30s, 40s, 50s+). Gucci's repositioning is shown as an arrow moving upward and to the left, from a classic positioning targeting older demographics toward a contemporary positioning targeting younger demographics (30s and below).
- **How to Apply**: Plot a brand's current and desired positioning on the map. Identify the direction and magnitude of the repositioning required. Assess whether the shift is incremental or disruptive.
- **When to Use**: Use when planning or evaluating brand repositioning strategies, particularly for heritage or luxury brands seeking to attract younger consumers.

### Personal Branding Framework (Desirable-Viable-Feasible)

- **Visual Description**: Three overlapping circles labeled Passion/Desirable, Market/Viable, and Skills/Feasible. The sweet spot for personal branding sits at the intersection of all three circles. A five-step process accompanies the diagram: (1) Set target audience, (2) Self-analysis, (3) Write brand mantra and mental map, (4) Choose points-of-difference across desirability, deliverability, and differentiation, (5) Draft brand plans with positioning statements.
- **How to Apply**: Start by listing your passions, then your skills, then what the market (e.g., recruiters, business partners) actually values. Find the overlap. Use this to craft your brand mantra (emotional modifier + descriptive modifier + brand function) and positioning statement.
- **When to Use**: Use when building a personal brand for career development, networking, job applications, or professional positioning. Also useful for entrepreneurs defining their personal value proposition.

### Personal Brand Building Blocks Checklist

- **Visual Description**: A structured checklist with components: (1) Self-analysis (personal SWOT), (2) Personal brand mantra, (3) Target audience definition, (4) Feedback from others via mental maps, (5) Personal brand positioning plan with desirability/deliverability/differentiation, (6) Integrated brand marketing activities (job postings, networking plans).
- **How to Apply**: Work through each component sequentially. Conduct self-analysis first, then define your audience, create your mantra, gather external feedback through mental maps, refine your positioning, and plan concrete marketing activities.
- **When to Use**: Use as a comprehensive template when building or refining a personal brand. Particularly useful for structured self-reflection before job searches or career transitions.

## Case Studies

### Gucci Brand Repositioning

- **Company**: Gucci (under parent company Kering)
- **Challenge**: Gucci needed to reposition from a classic, heritage luxury brand perceived as catering to older consumers into a contemporary, culturally relevant brand that appeals to Millennials and Gen Z, without losing its luxury cachet.
- **What Was Done**: Through successive creative directors -- Tom Ford (sex appeal and glamour), Frida Giannini (classic luxury refinement), and Alessandro Michele (eclectic maximalism and inclusivity) -- Gucci executed a disruptive repositioning. Under Michele, the brand embraced streetwear collaborations (e.g., Palace Skateboards), cinema-inspired campaigns, and a contemporary aesthetic targeting younger demographics. Gucci also expanded into beauty to reach a broader consumer base and invested heavily in digital channels.
- **Takeaway**: Disruptive repositioning can revitalize a heritage luxury brand, but it requires careful management of the tension between accessibility and exclusivity. Entering lower-price-point categories (beauty) risks brand dilution, while over-reliance on specific consumer segments (Chinese buyers) creates strategic vulnerability. More recently, Gucci faced backlash for using AI-generated images in fashion show promotions, highlighting the risks of adopting new technologies that may conflict with the craftsmanship values central to luxury brand identity.

### Online Luxury Market Growth (Illustrative)

- **Company**: Luxury industry broadly
- **Challenge**: Traditional luxury brands historically resisted online channels, viewing them as incompatible with the exclusive, high-touch in-store experience that defines luxury retail.
- **What Was Done**: Online sales for personal luxury goods nearly doubled from $37.3B in 2019 to $70.1B in 2021, reaching 22% of the total market. By 2025, online was projected to reach 30% share, driven by brands' own websites and new digital opportunities. Distribution shifted across channels: monobrand stores (28%), online (30%), outlet stores (14%), specialty stores (11%), department stores (11%), and travel retail (6%).
- **Takeaway**: Digital transformation is no longer optional for luxury brands. The shift to online accelerated dramatically due to Covid-19, and brands that fail to build strong direct-to-consumer digital channels risk losing relevance with younger consumers who expect seamless omnichannel experiences.

## Key Takeaways

- **Disruptive repositioning** can transform a heritage luxury brand's relevance, but it requires bold creative vision and carries risks of alienating core customers (as demonstrated by Gucci's evolution under Ford, Giannini, and Michele).
- **Brand dilution** is the central risk when luxury brands pursue broader accessibility, such as entering lower-price-point categories like beauty or expanding distribution channels.
- **The new luxury paradigm** has fundamentally shifted from status-driven, exclusive, offline consumption toward experience-driven, digitally-enabled, and more inclusive models of luxury.
- **Millennials and Gen Z** will dominate the luxury market (projected 55% by 2025), making youth-oriented repositioning strategically essential for long-term brand viability.
- **Consumption as signaling** remains a core driver of luxury purchases, but younger consumers increasingly signal through values alignment (sustainability, inclusivity) rather than pure status display.
- **Geographic concentration risk** is a significant strategic vulnerability; Gucci's heavy dependence on Chinese consumers illustrates the dangers of relying on a single nationality for growth.
- **Digital channel strategy** is no longer optional for luxury brands, with online luxury sales nearly doubling from 2019 to 2021 and projected to reach 30% of market share by 2025.
- **Personal branding** applies the same frameworks as corporate brand management -- brand mantra, mental maps, points-of-difference, and positioning -- to individuals seeking to differentiate themselves professionally.
- **The Desirable-Viable-Feasible framework** translates directly to personal branding as Passion-Market-Skills, helping individuals find the intersection where their personal brand is strongest.
- **Scarcity vs. sustainability** presents a core tension for luxury brands that must balance artificial supply limitation with growing consumer expectations for environmental responsibility.

## Key Terms and Definitions Glossary

**Brand Dilution**: The weakening of a brand's core identity and perceived value that can occur when a brand extends into too many categories or becomes too widely accessible. [Related: Brand Equity, Brand Extension, Exclusivity]

**Brand Mantra (Personal)**: A short phrase capturing the essence of an individual's professional identity, structured as emotional modifier + descriptive modifier + brand function. [Related: Brand Positioning, Mental Map, Points-of-Difference]

**Brand Repositioning**: The strategic process of changing a brand's position in consumers' minds relative to competitors, which can range from incremental adjustments to disruptive overhauls. [Related: Perceptual Map, Brand Image, Target Segment]

**Consumption as Signaling**: The use of product purchases and brand choices to communicate social status, identity, values, or group membership to others. [Related: Luxury Consumption, Brand Image, Social Identity]

**Deliverability**: In the context of points-of-difference for personal branding, the extent to which a person can actually and consistently demonstrate or perform the skills and abilities they claim. [Related: Feasibility, Personal Branding, Points-of-Difference]

**Desirability**: In personal branding, the degree to which a person's passion, values, and brand promise resonate with and are wanted by their target audience. [Related: Passion, Personal Branding, Points-of-Difference]

**Differentiation**: In personal branding, the long-term viability and uniqueness of a person's positioning relative to competitors in their professional space. [Related: Points-of-Difference, Competitive Advantage, Positioning]

**Disruptive Repositioning**: A bold, fundamental shift in a brand's market positioning that breaks significantly from its historical identity, as opposed to incremental or evolutionary changes. [Related: Brand Repositioning, Brand Equity, Brand Image]

**Exclusivity**: The perception that a brand or product is rare, limited, or accessible only to a select group, which is a core value driver in the luxury industry. [Related: Scarcity, Luxury Brand Management, Brand Dilution]

**Geographic Market Dependence**: The strategic risk created when a disproportionate share of a brand's revenue comes from consumers of a single country or region. [Related: Market Diversification, Global Brand Strategy, Risk Management]

**Luxury Consumption Drivers**: The underlying motivations for consumers to pay premium prices for luxury goods, including status signaling, quality perception, self-reward, craftsmanship appreciation, and social belonging. [Related: Consumption as Signaling, Brand Equity, Price Premium]

**Mental Map**: A visual diagram showing all the associations and perceptions that people hold about a brand (or person), displayed as attributes radiating outward from a central brand node. [Related: Brand Associations, Brand Image, Brand Knowledge]

**Merchandise Mix**: The assortment of product categories and styles that a brand offers, which in luxury must be carefully managed to balance breadth of appeal with brand coherence. [Related: Product Line, Brand Architecture, Brand Extension]

**New Luxury Paradigm**: The evolved model of luxury consumption characterized by experience over ownership, digital engagement, younger consumers, and values-driven purchasing. [Related: Old Luxury, Generational Shift, Digital Transformation]

**Old Luxury**: The traditional model of luxury consumption characterized by status symbolism, exclusive in-store experiences, ownership-focused consumption, and resistance to digital channels. [Related: New Luxury Paradigm, Heritage Brand, Exclusivity]

**Perceptual Map**: A visual tool that plots brands or products on two or more dimensions (e.g., classic vs. contemporary, older vs. younger demographic appeal) to illustrate competitive positioning. [Related: Brand Repositioning, Competitive Analysis, Positioning]

**Personal Branding**: The practice of applying brand management principles -- including positioning, differentiation, brand mantra, and mental maps -- to build and manage an individual's professional identity and reputation. [Related: Brand Mantra, Mental Map, Points-of-Difference]

**Personal SWOT Analysis**: A self-assessment tool that evaluates an individual's Strengths, Weaknesses, Opportunities, and Threats as part of the personal branding process. [Related: Self-Analysis, Personal Branding, Brand Positioning]

**Points-of-Difference (Personal)**: The unique attributes or benefits that distinguish an individual from competitors in the job market or professional space, evaluated on desirability, deliverability, and differentiation. [Related: Positioning, Competitive Advantage, Personal Branding]

**Scarcity Strategy**: The deliberate limitation of product supply to maintain perceptions of exclusivity and drive premium pricing, commonly used in the luxury industry. [Related: Exclusivity, Luxury Consumption, Supply Limitation]

**Viability**: In personal branding, the extent to which the market (employers, clients, partners) will actually reward and value a person's chosen brand positioning. [Related: Market, Personal Branding, Desirable-Viable-Feasible Framework]
