## Overview

MKT3717 Product and Brand Management is a semester-long course offered at NUS Business School under the Marketing Department, taught by Dr. Dayoung Kim in Semester 2, AY 2025/2026. The course takes an explicitly managerial perspective, premised on the idea that great products and brands are the result of disciplined thinking, creative insight, and consistent strategic choices rather than chance. Students are trained to view products and brands as living systems that must be actively managed across their entire life cycles, from the initial identification of unmet customer needs through design, launch, and long-run brand stewardship.

The course integrates both analytical and creative modes of reasoning. Lectures, case discussions, frameworks, and hands-on exercises are combined so that students develop structural thinking about innovation alongside the judgment required for brand decision-making. A recurring theme is the tension between creativity and control, and between short-term performance metrics and long-term brand health. The course also explicitly addresses the role of AI in reshaping product marketing and brand management, situating traditional frameworks within the contemporary technological context.

Assessment is structured to develop skills progressively across three dimensions: collaborative project work, individual testing, and active class participation. The group project on new product launching anchors the applied learning experience, culminating in both a formal presentation and a written marketing plan report. Individual accountability is maintained through a final test and participation requirements including a 3-minute product idea talk, in-class discussion contributions, and peer evaluation of teammates.

## Key Concepts

### Course Objectives

By the end of the course, students are expected to achieve five core competencies:

1. **Structural innovation thinking** — understanding the full decision sequence from identifying customer needs to developing, launching, and managing new products.
2. **Framework application** — applying both qualitative and quantitative frameworks to analyze market opportunities and position products effectively.
3. **Integrated judgment** — combining consumer insight with managerial judgment to balance creativity with control and short-term performance with long-term brand health.
4. **Strategic communication** — developing the ability to articulate a coherent product or brand vision that aligns with broader business goals.
5. **Personal application** — reflecting on one's own role as a product or brand manager and applying course principles to personal and professional growth.

### Course Policies

- **Textbooks are not required for purchase.** Three classic references are listed as optional resources: Crawford & Benedetto (New Products Management, 12th ed.), Keller & Swaminathan (Strategic Brand Management, 5th ed.), and Ulrich, Eppinger & Yang (Product Design and Development, 7th ed.).
- **Supplementary readings** are drawn from Harvard Business Review, Forbes, The Wall Street Journal, Businessweek, and other contemporary sources; specific assignments are announced during the semester (TBA).
- **Academic honesty** is strictly enforced. Academic dishonesty includes misrepresentation, failure to acknowledge sources, falsification of information, and cheating. Students bear the affirmative obligation to distinguish their own work from others' work; failure to do so entitles the assessor to treat all submitted work as the student's own claim.
- **Plagiarism** is defined as taking someone else's work or ideas and passing them off as one's own (per The New Oxford Dictionary of English) and is not condoned by NUS or NUS Business School.

## Frameworks & Mental Models

### The Course as a Product Development Lifecycle Framework

The 13-week schedule maps directly onto the stages of the new product development (NPD) process, functioning itself as a pedagogical framework for the full innovation pipeline:

| Phase | Weeks | Topics |
|---|---|---|
| **Opportunity Identification** | Week 1–2 | Course intro; product/brand fundamentals; ideation; market sizing |
| **Concept Development & Evaluation** | Week 3–4 | Product architecture; prototyping; concept testing; guest speaker on service design |
| **Launch Planning & Market Testing** | Week 5–6 | Conjoint analysis; market experiment design; demand forecasting; project scheduling |
| **Midterm Review** | Week 7 | Team progress check; midterm presentation |
| **Brand Management** | Week 8–9 | Design thinking; human-centered innovation (IDEO case); personal branding; brand strategy; sustainable brands |
| **Assessment Period** | Week 10–11 | Final Test (26 March 2026, tentative); NUS Well-being Day (2 April 2026) |
| **Project Presentations** | Week 12–13 | Group project presentations (parts 1 and 2) |

### The Managerial Judgment Framework

The course frames all product and brand decisions through a manager's lens, requiring students to hold two tensions simultaneously:
- **Creativity vs. Control**: Innovative thinking must be disciplined by strategic and financial constraints.
- **Short-term vs. Long-term**: Quarterly performance metrics must be balanced against the long-run equity of the brand.

### Products and Brands as Living Systems

A core mental model in the course is that products and brands are not static artifacts but dynamic systems that co-evolve with consumers and culture. This implies that management is ongoing rather than episodic, and that strategic decisions made at launch have compounding effects on long-term brand health.

## Formulas & Quantitative Tools

### Grading Breakdown

The assessment weights sum to 100% and can be grouped as follows:

- **Group work total**: 25% (presentations) + 20% (report) = **45%**
- **Individual test total**: 20% (Final Test) = **20%**
- **Participation total**: 5% (product idea talk) + 25% (in-class discussion) + 5% (peer evaluation) = **35%**

This means participation (35%) carries more weight than the individual test (20%) and nearly as much as the full group project (45%), signaling that active engagement is critical to final grade outcomes.

### Quantitative Methods Referenced in Schedule

- **Market sizing** (Week 2): Quantitative estimation of total addressable market (TAM) and opportunity scale.
- **Conjoint analysis for NPD** (Week 5): A quantitative technique to measure consumer preference trade-offs across product attributes and infer willingness to pay.
- **Demand forecasting** (Week 6): Quantitative and experimental methods for projecting sales and market response prior to full launch.
- **Project scheduling** (Week 6): Scheduling tools (e.g., critical path, Gantt-style planning) applied to product launch timelines.

## Case Studies

### IDEO Human-Centered Service Design (Week 8)

The syllabus explicitly names IDEO's human-centered service design approach as a case study for Week 8, which covers Design Thinking and Human-Centered Innovations. IDEO is widely known as the firm that popularized design thinking as a structured, empathy-driven methodology for innovation. This case is used to ground the conceptual content on design thinking in a real-world practitioner context, illustrating how large organizations apply human-centered methods to service innovation.

### Guest Speaker: Service Design and Brand Storytelling (Week 4)

Week 4 features a guest speaker session focused on service design and brand storytelling. While the specific speaker and organization are not named in the syllabus, this session bridges the concept evaluation phase (Week 3) and the launch planning phase (Week 5), suggesting the practitioner perspective is intended to illustrate how real brands craft narratives around their product concepts.

### Supplementary Contemporary Cases (Ongoing)

Selected articles and cases from Harvard Business Review, Forbes, The Wall Street Journal, and Businessweek are assigned throughout the semester. These cover real-world applications of course frameworks, new developments in brand management, and the role of AI in reshaping product marketing. Specific assignments are TBA.

## Key Terms and Definitions Glossary

**Academic Dishonesty**: Any misrepresentation with intent to deceive, failure to acknowledge sources, falsification of information, inaccuracy of statements, cheating at examinations, or inappropriate use of resources — all prohibited under NUS policy. [Related: Plagiarism, Academic Integrity]

**Brand Equity**: The long-term cumulative value embedded in a brand, built through consistent strategic choices and consumer associations over time. The course frames short-term performance decisions in relation to their effect on brand equity. [Related: Brand Health, Strategic Brand Management]

**Brand Health**: The long-term vitality and consumer relevance of a brand, contrasted with short-term performance metrics. A recurring course tension is between decisions that boost immediate results versus those that sustain brand health. [Related: Brand Equity, Long-term vs. Short-term]

**Brand Storytelling**: The practice of crafting and communicating a coherent narrative around a brand or product to build consumer connection and meaning; featured in the Week 4 guest speaker session. [Related: Brand Strategy, Service Design]

**Concept Evaluation**: The structured process of assessing new product ideas against market, technical, and financial criteria before committing to full development; covered in Week 3. [Related: Concept Generation, Concept Testing, NPD]

**Conjoint Analysis**: A quantitative research technique that measures how consumers trade off among product attributes to reveal preference structures and implied willingness to pay; used in NPD to optimize product design. [Related: Market Testing, Demand Forecasting, NPD]

**Demand Forecasting**: Quantitative and experimental methods for projecting consumer demand and market response before a product's full commercial launch; covered in Week 6. [Related: Market Experiment, Conjoint Analysis]

**Design Thinking**: A structured, human-centered approach to innovation that emphasizes empathy with users, iterative prototyping, and creative problem-solving; exemplified in the course by the IDEO case in Week 8. [Related: Human-Centered Innovation, IDEO]

**Final Marketing Plan Report**: The written deliverable of the group project, worth 20% of the final grade, documenting the full new product launch strategy developed by each team. [Related: Group Project, Group Presentations]

**Group Project on New Product Launching**: The primary applied assessment in MKT3717, comprising group presentations (25%) and a final marketing plan report (20%), requiring teams to develop and present a complete new product launch strategy. [Related: Final Marketing Plan Report, Midterm Presentation]

**Human-Centered Innovation**: An approach to product and service design that places end-user needs, behaviors, and experiences at the center of the innovation process, as opposed to technology-driven or operations-driven approaches. [Related: Design Thinking, IDEO, Service Design]

**Market Sizing**: The quantitative exercise of estimating the total addressable market (TAM) or relevant opportunity size for a new product; practiced as a hands-on exercise in Week 2. [Related: Opportunity Identification, Demand Forecasting]

**New Product Development (NPD)**: The full end-to-end process by which firms move from identifying customer needs to designing, testing, launching, and managing new products; the organizing spine of the first half of the course (Weeks 1–7). [Related: Concept Generation, Concept Evaluation, Market Testing, Product Launch]

**Peer Evaluation**: A structured assessment component (5% of grade) in which students formally evaluate the contributions of their group project teammates, reinforcing accountability in collaborative work. [Related: Group Project, Participation]

**Plagiarism**: The practice of taking someone else's work or ideas and passing them off as one's own, as defined by The New Oxford Dictionary of English; strictly prohibited under NUS academic integrity policy. [Related: Academic Dishonesty, Academic Integrity]

**Product Architecture**: The scheme by which the functional elements of a product are arranged and interact; covered in Week 3 as part of concept development and prototyping. [Related: Prototyping, NPD, Concept Development]

**Product Life Cycle**: The stages a product passes through from introduction through growth, maturity, and decline; the course's managerial perspective involves actively managing products across all these stages. [Related: Brand Management, NPD]

**Service Design**: The activity of planning and organizing a service's components — people, infrastructure, communication, and material — to improve quality and user experience; featured in the Week 4 guest speaker session and the Week 8 IDEO case. [Related: Design Thinking, Human-Centered Innovation]

**3-Minute Product Idea Talk**: An individual participation component (5% of grade) in which each student briefly pitches an original product idea to the class, developing concise communication and concept articulation skills. [Related: Participation, Concept Generation]
