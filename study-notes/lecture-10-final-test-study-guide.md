## Key Concepts

### Topic Area 1: New Product Development

#### What Is a New Product?

**Definition**: A new product is any product that is new to the firm, new to the market, or new to the world. Newness can take several forms: new-to-the-world products (true innovations with no predecessor), new product lines (entering an existing category for the first time), additions to existing product lines (line extensions), improvements/revisions to existing products, repositioned products (same product, new target market or use), and cost reductions (same benefit at lower cost).

**Context**: The classification is important for exam questions because product categories carry different risk profiles, market sizing requirements, and NPD process demands. A line extension draws on existing customer knowledge and distribution, while a new-to-the-world product requires building awareness from scratch. The dishwasher detergent pod example from the practice MCQ (scented, gel detergent in water-soluble bags designed to fit existing dishwasher trays) is correctly classified as a line extension — an addition to Ace Corporation's existing detergent portfolio targeting current markets — not a new-to-the-world product.

**Relationships**: Directly connects to the five-phase NPD process (Crawford), opportunity identification strategy, and risk assessment. The type of newness shapes the appropriate testing and launch strategy.

#### Why Is New Product Development Difficult?

**Definition**: NPD is difficult because of high market uncertainty, high technical uncertainty, long development timelines, resource intensity, cross-functional coordination challenges, and the systematic attrition of ideas through development gates. Most new products fail despite rigorous development processes.

**Context**: The tournament-and-filtering model (e.g., 10,000 pharmaceutical compounds → 1 approved drug; 500 film pitches → 1 successful feature) illustrates the structural difficulty. High failure rates make robust opportunity identification, concept evaluation, and market testing critical.

**Relationships**: Motivates the entire NPD framework — every tool (RWW, conjoint analysis, concept scoring, project management) exists to reduce the probability of investing in a product that will fail.

### Topic Area 2: Opportunity Identification

#### RWW Framework

**Definition**: Real-Win-Worth It (RWW) is a three-question opportunity evaluation framework: (1) Is it Real? — Does a genuine market need exist and is the product technically achievable? (2) Can We Win? — Can the firm establish competitive advantage and execute better than rivals? (3) Is it Worth It? — Will the investment generate sufficient financial return at acceptable risk?

**Context**: Developed by George Day (Harvard Business Review, December 2007). Maps onto the Desirability/Viability/Feasibility triangle: Real = People desirable; Win = Technically feasible; Worth It = Business viable. Applied as a filter at the end of Phase 1 (Opportunity Identification/Selection).

**Relationships**: Complements PESTEL (macro-environment) and Porter's Five Forces (industry environment) as analytical inputs to the Win and Worth It questions. The SOM from market sizing feeds the Worth It assessment.

#### General Environment Analysis (PESTEL)

**Definition**: A framework for scanning macro-environmental factors affecting market attractiveness: Political/Legal, Economic, Social/Cultural, and Technological (sometimes extended with Environmental and Legal as separate categories). Each factor is rated 1 (very unfavorable) to 5 (very favorable) for a specific product-market context.

**Context**: Addresses the outer ring of the environment model. Commonly applied in combination with Porter's Five Forces to produce a holistic environment assessment.

**Relationships**: Feeds into RWW "Is it Real?" and "Is it Worth It?" questions. Works alongside Porter's Five Forces, which addresses the operating/industry environment (inner ring).

#### Porter's Five Forces

**Definition**: A framework for diagnosing the competitive attractiveness of an industry through five structural forces: (1) Threat of New Entrants, (2) Bargaining Power of Buyers, (3) Bargaining Power of Suppliers, (4) Threat of Substitute Products, and (5) Intensity of Rivalry among existing competitors. Each force is rated Favorable/Moderate/Unfavorable.

**Context**: Applied in class to the Nike yoga apparel in Singapore scenario. Industry attractiveness (favorable forces overall) supports the "Can We Win?" assessment.

**Relationships**: Addresses the operating/industry environment; paired with PESTEL for the full situation analysis. Feeds the RWW "Can We Win?" question.

#### Market Sizing (TAM/SAM/SOM)

**Definition**: A structured approach to estimating potential revenue using three nested layers: Total Addressable Market (TAM) — theoretical maximum assuming 100% share; Serviceable Addressable Market (SAM) — the realistic target segment given product scope, geography, and price; Serviceable Obtainable Market (SOM) — the share the firm can credibly capture given competition.

**Context**: Estimated via guesstimation: (1) clarify scope, (2) map out the calculation formula, (3) round and calculate, (4) sense-check. Nike example: TAM = SGD 300M, SAM = SGD 180M, SOM = SGD 18M.

**Relationships**: SOM feeds the RWW "Is it Worth It?" question. TAM/SAM/SOM appears in pitch decks, business plans, and consulting case interviews.

#### Jobs-to-Be-Done Approach

**Definition**: A customer research philosophy that frames customer needs not as product attributes but as "jobs" customers are trying to accomplish in their lives. Rather than asking "what features do you want?" the approach asks "what outcome are you trying to achieve?"

**Context**: Contrasted with traditional attribute-focused customer surveys. Benefits include revealing latent needs, uncovering non-obvious competitors (products from adjacent categories that do the same job), and identifying when customers will switch products.

**Relationships**: Connects to latent needs identification and the customer needs statement translation process.

#### Generating Product Specifications

**Definition**: The process of translating customer voice (raw quotes from interviews or observation) into customer needs statements (what the product should do, not how), and then translating those needs statements into measurable product specifications (metric + target value).

**Context**: Needs statement guidelines: "What" not "How"; specific; positive not negative; an attribute of the product; avoid "must" and "should." Practiced with Nest thermostat and bike lock examples.

**Relationships**: Bridges opportunity identification (Phase 1) and concept generation (Phase 2). Product specifications become the evaluation criteria in the concept scoring matrix.

### Topic Area 3: Concept Generation and Evaluation

#### Product Concept Definition

**Definition**: A product concept is a description of the form, technology, and function of a proposed product in sufficient detail to allow evaluation against customer needs. Per Crawford (pp. 96–102), a concept includes the basic function(s) performed, the form (physical structure, technology), and the benefits delivered to the target customer.

**Context**: Concepts are the inputs to concept selection methods. They evolve from rough opportunity descriptions through stated, tested, and defined concept stages.

**Relationships**: Feeds from Phase 2 (Concept Generation) into Phase 3 (Concept/Project Evaluation). Inputs are customer needs statements and product specifications.

#### Concept Screening Matrix

**Definition**: A coarse concept selection tool that compares each concept to a reference concept (current market leader or current product) using a +/0/- scale (better than/same as/worse than reference) on each criterion. The concept with the most positive and fewest negative ratings proceeds to the scoring matrix.

**Context**: Pros: fast, simple, requires no quantitative data. Cons: ordinal scale lacks precision; outcome depends heavily on the reference concept chosen; cannot distinguish among concepts that all score similarly.

**Relationships**: Precedes the concept scoring matrix. Eliminates clearly inferior or strategically misaligned concepts to reduce the pool before investing in more detailed scoring.

#### Concept Scoring Matrix

**Definition**: A quantitative decision tool that multiplies consumer-derived importance weights by concept ratings on each criterion and sums the products to produce a total score for ranking. The formula is: Total Score = sum of (weight_i × rating_i) across all criteria.

**Context**: Weights are derived from consumer problem surveys (not internal team judgment). Ratings are typically on a 1–5 scale (1 = Much Worse than Reference, 3 = Same as Reference, 5 = Much Better than Reference). The process has six steps: prepare matrix, rate concepts, rank, combine and improve, select, reflect and seek consensus.

**Relationships**: Follows concept screening. The "combine and improve" step is critical — the matrix is a diagnostic tool, not a final verdict; strong features from lower-ranked concepts should be harvested into the winner.

#### Conjoint Analysis

**Definition**: A quantitative market research technique that decomposes an individual consumer's overall product preference into the separate contributions (part-worths) of each product attribute. It is estimated using the regression approach: preference (dependent variable) is regressed on dummy-coded attribute levels.

**Context**: Constructing variables — each attribute level is coded as a binary dummy (0/1). The regression coefficient on each dummy indicates that level's contribution to preference. Attribute importance is calculated from the range of part-worths: the larger the range for an attribute, the more important it is to the consumer. Relative importance is expressed as a percentage of the total range sum.

**Relationships**: Produces the most rigorous individual-level preference data of any concept testing method. Pros: reveals trade-offs, enables market simulation, individual-level analysis. Cons: design complexity, assumes attributes are independent, respondent fatigue at large profile counts. Covered in Crawford pp. 147–151.

### Topic Area 4: Product Design and Architecture

#### Modular Design for Mass Customization

**Definition**: A design architecture in which a product is composed of standardized, interchangeable modules (components) that can be combined in different configurations to produce variety, rather than designing each product variant from scratch.

**Context**: Key benefits under demand uncertainty: (1) flexibility — the firm can reconfigure modules to meet varying customer demands without retooling; (2) reduced inventory risk — hold modules rather than finished goods, assembling to order; (3) faster time-to-market for new variants; (4) scale economies on shared modules. The short-answer exam question asks for a 3–4 sentence description of these benefits.

**Relationships**: Connects to mass customization strategy and postponement logistics. Relevant to the product architecture phase (Phase 4: Development) in the NPD process.

### Topic Area 5: Market Testing — Experimental Approaches

#### Why Causality Matters and How to Establish It

**Definition**: Causality means that a change in variable X directly causes a change in outcome Y, not merely that they are correlated. To establish causality, three conditions must be met: (1) X precedes Y in time, (2) X and Y covary, and (3) all alternative explanations (confounds) are eliminated.

**Context**: Controlled experiments (A/B testing, factorial design) are the primary tools for establishing causality in market testing because random assignment of participants to conditions eliminates systematic confounds.

**Relationships**: Foundational to A/B testing, factorial design, and correct interpretation of regression results from market experiments.

#### A/B Testing

**Definition**: A controlled experiment in which two versions of a product, feature, price, or message (A = control, B = treatment) are randomly assigned to different user groups and outcome metrics are compared to determine which version performs better.

**Context**: Common in digital products where versioning is low-cost and random assignment is feasible at scale. Establishes causality because random assignment eliminates selection bias.

**Relationships**: Final testing stage in the NPD pipeline (after beta testing, at production release). Also used iteratively throughout digital product development for optimization.

#### Factorial Design

**Definition**: An experimental design that tests all combinations of multiple factors (independent variables) simultaneously, enabling estimation of both main effects (the individual impact of each factor) and interaction effects (whether the effect of one factor depends on the level of another).

**Context**: More efficient than running separate A/B tests for each factor. A 2×2 factorial design tests two factors each at two levels — four conditions total. A full factorial 2^k design tests k factors.

**Relationships**: Extension of A/B testing. Results are interpreted using regression coefficients and interaction terms, which may be tested for statistical significance using p-values.

#### Statistical Interpretation (Confidence Interval, p-Value, Regression)

**Definition**: p-value — the probability of observing results at least as extreme as the data, assuming the null hypothesis is true; a p-value < 0.05 (conventional threshold) indicates the result is statistically significant. Confidence interval (CI) — a range of values within which the true population parameter is estimated to fall with a specified probability (e.g., 95% CI); if the CI for a difference does not include zero, the effect is statistically significant. Regression coefficient — the estimated change in the outcome variable for a one-unit increase in the predictor, holding all other predictors constant.

**Context**: Applied to interpret market experiment results. In conjoint analysis, regression coefficients represent part-worths; in market testing, they represent the estimated treatment effect.

**Relationships**: Connects statistical testing (A/B, factorial) with conjoint analysis interpretation. Both contexts require correct reading of coefficients, p-values, and CIs.

### Topic Area 6: Project Management

#### Characteristics of a Project

**Definition**: A project is a temporary endeavor with a defined start and end, producing a unique output. Key characteristics: defined scope, specific deliverables, resource constraints (budget, personnel, equipment), a defined timeline, and interdependent activities.

**Context**: Contrasts with ongoing operational work. NPD projects have all these characteristics: they are initiated at project kick-off, conclude at market launch, and require coordinating multiple cross-functional activities with precedence relationships.

**Relationships**: Motivates the use of network diagram tools (PERT/CPM) for project management.

#### Work-Breakdown Structure (WBS)

**Definition**: A hierarchical decomposition of a project into progressively smaller components — from major deliverables down to individual work packages (tasks) that can be assigned, estimated, and tracked.

**Context**: First step before constructing a network diagram. Each task at the lowest WBS level becomes a node or activity in the network diagram.

**Relationships**: Input to the network diagram. Enables identification of precedence relationships (which tasks must be completed before others can start).

#### Network Diagram and Critical Path

**Definition**: A network diagram (PERT or CPM) represents project activities as nodes or arrows with precedence relationships (arrows) connecting them. The critical path is the sequence of activities from project start to finish that takes the longest time to complete — it determines the minimum project duration. Activities on the critical path have zero slack.

**Context**: Practice problem MCQ answer: the critical path is the sequence with the longest total duration (Answer A), not the sequence with maximum slack (that would be a non-critical path), no precedence relationships, or lowest cost.

**Relationships**: Critical path identification enables project crashing decisions (which activities to accelerate to shorten total project duration at minimum cost).

#### Slack Time Calculation

**Definition**: Slack (or float) for an activity is the amount of time that activity can be delayed without delaying the project completion date. Slack = Late Start (LS) − Early Start (ES) = Late Finish (LF) − Early Finish (EF). Activities on the critical path have slack = 0.

**Context**: Calculated by performing a forward pass (computing ES and EF for each activity) and a backward pass (computing LF and LS). Practice problems from lecture and supplement are the primary study materials.

**Relationships**: Slack directly informs which activities can absorb delays (high slack) and which cannot (zero slack = critical path).

#### Project Crashing

**Definition**: The deliberate shortening of activity durations by adding resources (overtime, additional personnel, equipment) to reduce total project duration, at an additional cost. Crashing decisions prioritize activities on the critical path (crashing non-critical activities does not shorten project duration) and select the activity with the lowest crash cost per time unit saved.

**Context**: Decision rule: crash the critical path activity with the lowest incremental cost per unit of time saved. After crashing, re-identify the critical path because crashing may create new critical paths.

**Relationships**: Applies slack time calculations and critical path identification. A cost-benefit trade-off — crashing saves time but increases cost.

### Topic Area 7: Brand Management

#### Building Blocks of a Brand

**Definition**: The core brand building blocks are: (1) Brand — a name, term, sign, symbol, design, or combination intended to identify goods/services of one seller and differentiate them from competitors; (2) Brand Equity — the added value a brand name gives to a product beyond its functional benefits; (3) Brand Identity — the full set of brand associations the firm intends to create; (4) Brand Image — the set of associations consumers actually hold about the brand; (5) Brand Awareness — the strength of the brand node in memory, reflected by consumers' ability to identify the brand under different conditions; (6) Brand Associations — any informational node linked to the brand in consumer memory, including attributes, benefits, attitudes, and imagery.

**Context**: Definitions must be precise for multiple-choice questions. The distinction between brand identity (intended) and brand image (perceived) is frequently tested.

**Relationships**: All building blocks feed into Keller's Brand Resonance Pyramid as the foundation layer.

#### Keller's Brand Resonance Pyramid

**Definition**: A hierarchical model of brand building that progresses through four levels (bottom to top): (1) Brand Salience — how readily and how easily customers think of the brand (Who are you?); (2) Brand Performance and Brand Imagery — what the brand does (performance: product/service attributes; imagery: intangible associations) (What are you?); (3) Brand Judgments and Brand Feelings — customer evaluations and emotional responses (What do I think/feel about you?); (4) Brand Resonance — the ultimate relationship and level of identification customers have with the brand (What about you and me?). Resonance is characterized by loyalty, attachment, community, and active engagement.

**Context**: The pyramid has six building blocks arranged across four levels in two tracks: rational (left: Salience → Performance → Judgments → Loyalty) and emotional (right: Salience → Imagery → Feelings → Resonance). The goal is to build from the bottom up — salience must be established before performance/imagery, which must precede judgments/feelings, which must precede resonance.

**Relationships**: Provides the strategic architecture for brand building decisions — advertising, product design, pricing, distribution, and community building decisions each map to different levels of the pyramid.

#### Modern Brand Management Challenges

**Definition**: Contemporary brand management must balance two competing imperatives: (1) Sustaining continuity — maintaining consistent brand identity, associations, and equity over time so that the brand remains trustworthy and recognizable; (2) Staying relevant — adapting to changing consumer values, cultural trends, and competitive landscapes so that the brand remains contemporary and meaningful.

**Context**: Illustrated through case examples (e.g., Gucci's repositioning — maintaining luxury heritage while modernizing aesthetics and digital presence). Slack's founder's launch strategy (from the Post It Note History/First Round Review article) illustrates staying relevant through authentic brand storytelling and community building rather than traditional advertising.

**Relationships**: The tension between continuity and relevance maps onto the Brand Resonance Pyramid — continuity builds Salience and Performance (lower levels); relevance is required to maintain Imagery and Resonance (upper levels) over time. Failure to manage this tension results in brand dilution (over-extending for relevance) or brand obsolescence (over-emphasizing continuity).

## Frameworks & Mental Models

### RWW Framework (Real-Win-Worth It)

A three-question filter for opportunity evaluation: Real (genuine market need + technical achievability), Win (competitive advantage achievable), Worth It (sufficient financial return at acceptable risk). Maps onto the Desirability/Viability/Feasibility triangle. Applied sequentially — if "Is it Real?" fails, stop; only proceed to Win and Worth It if the opportunity is real.

### PESTEL General Environment Analysis

A macro-environment audit framework rating Political/Legal, Economic, Social/Cultural, and Technological factors on a 1–5 favorability scale. Applied alongside Porter's Five Forces to produce a complete situation analysis. Use to identify threats and opportunities in the broader operating context.

### Porter's Five Forces

Industry environment diagnosis tool. Five forces: Threat of New Entrants, Bargaining Power of Buyers, Bargaining Power of Suppliers, Threat of Substitutes, Intensity of Rivalry. Each rated Favorable/Moderate/Unfavorable. Use to assess industry attractiveness and competitive dynamics before committing to a market entry strategy.

### Concept Screening Matrix (Pugh Matrix)

Coarse concept selection: compare each concept to a reference concept using +/0/- on each criterion. Sum positives and negatives. Pros: fast, simple. Cons: ordinal only, reference-dependent, cannot distinguish close competitors. Use early in Phase 3 to eliminate clearly inferior concepts.

### Concept Scoring Matrix (Weighted)

Quantitative selection: Total Score = sum of (criterion weight × concept rating). Six-step process: prepare → rate → rank → combine and improve → select → reflect. Weights from consumer surveys. Pros: quantitative, consumer-grounded, enables sensitivity analysis. Cons: only as good as the weights and ratings entered; risks selecting the "best average product" rather than the genuinely best concept.

### Conjoint Analysis — Regression Approach

Preference decomposition method. Steps: (1) define attributes and levels; (2) create product profiles (all or fractional combinations); (3) collect consumer preference rankings or ratings; (4) dummy-code attribute levels; (5) run regression (preference ~ attribute dummies); (6) read part-worth coefficients from regression output; (7) calculate attribute importance as range of part-worths / sum of all ranges × 100%.

The average ranking difference shortcut (used when only two levels per attribute): compute average rank for each level of each attribute; the difference between levels equals the regression coefficient in magnitude; attribute range = that difference; importance % = range / sum of all ranges × 100%.

### Network Diagram (PERT/CPM) Process

Steps: (1) list all activities and durations from WBS; (2) identify precedence relationships; (3) draw the network (nodes and arrows); (4) forward pass — compute Early Start (ES) and Early Finish (EF) for each activity; (5) backward pass — compute Late Finish (LF) and Late Start (LS) for each activity; (6) compute slack = LS − ES for each activity; (7) identify critical path (all activities with slack = 0); (8) evaluate crashing options (cheapest per unit time on critical path).

### Keller's Brand Resonance Pyramid

Four levels, bottom to top: (1) Salience (identity: "Who are you?"), (2) Performance + Imagery (meaning: "What are you?"), (3) Judgments + Feelings (response: "What about you?"), (4) Resonance (relationships: "What about you and me?"). Build from the bottom up. Resonance = loyalty + attachment + community + active engagement.

## Formulas & Quantitative Tools

### Formula 1: Concept Scoring Matrix — Weighted Total Score

**Formula**:

$$\text{Total Score}_{\text{concept}} = \sum_{i=1}^{n} (w_i \times r_{i})$$

**Variables**:

| Variable | Description |
|---|---|
| $w_i$ | Importance weight of criterion $i$ (from consumer survey data; all weights sum to 100) |
| $r_i$ | Raw rating of the concept on criterion $i$ (e.g., 1–5 scale, where 1 = Much Worse, 3 = Same, 5 = Much Better than the reference concept) |
| $n$ | Total number of criteria (or sub-criteria) in the matrix |
| Total Score | Sum of all weighted scores; used to rank concepts |

**Interpretation**: Higher total score = better overall performance across consumer-relevant criteria. Concept with highest total score is ranked first. Do not stop at ranking — apply the "combine and improve" step. Run sensitivity analysis: check whether the ranking changes if uncertain weights shift within a plausible range.

**Example**: Boat cup-holder matrix. Eight criterion categories weighted: Flexible Use = 20, Maintains Drink Condition = 15, Survives Boating Environment = 5, Keeps Drink Container Stable = 20, Requires Little Maintenance = 5, Easy to Use = 15, Attractive in Environment = 10, Manufacturing Ease = 10 (total = 100). Concept C scored 594 (rank 1); Concept O scored 587 (rank 2); Concept F scored 585 (rank 3).

**Common Mistakes**:
- Using weights determined by the internal team rather than from consumer problem surveys.
- Treating the highest-scoring concept as the automatic winner without applying the "combine and improve" step.
- Failing to run sensitivity analysis when some weights are uncertain.
- Applying uniform weights to all criteria (collapses to a simple average).
- Ignoring sub-criteria with extreme scores that signal a concept's unique strength or fatal flaw.

### Formula 2: Market Size (Guesstimation)

**Formula**:

$$\text{Market Size} = \text{Total Units Sold} \times \text{Average Price per Unit}$$

Where:

$$\text{Total Units Sold} = \text{Number of Customers} \times \text{Average Units Bought per Customer}$$

$$\text{Average Units Bought per Customer} = \text{Frequency of Purchase} \times \text{Quantity per Purchase}$$

**Variables**:

| Variable | Description |
|---|---|
| Market Size | Total potential revenue in a defined period and geography |
| Total Units Sold | Aggregate quantity purchased by all customers |
| Number of Customers | Baseline population × relevant ratio (proportion who are potential buyers) |
| Average Price per Unit | Typical transaction price in the category/segment |

**Interpretation**: Compute TAM (100% share), then SAM (realistic segment), then SOM (credibly capturable share). SOM is the operating target. TAM/SAM/SOM ratios must be defensible from market data or well-reasoned assumptions.

**Example**: Nike yoga apparel, Singapore. TAM = 1,000,000 yoga consumers × SGD 300 avg spend = SGD 300M. SAM = SGD 300M × 60% (premium branded segment) = SGD 180M. SOM = SGD 180M × 10% (Nike's realistic capture rate) = SGD 18M.

**Common Mistakes**:
- Reporting TAM as the target (unrealistic; no firm has 100% share).
- Failing to sense-check: does the final number look plausible against known industry data?
- Using inconsistent time periods or geographies across steps.

### Formula 3: Conjoint Analysis — Part-Worth Estimation via Regression

**Formula**:

$$\text{Preference}_j = \beta_0 + \beta_1 X_{1j} + \beta_2 X_{2j} + \cdots + \beta_k X_{kj} + \varepsilon_j$$

**Variables**:

| Variable | Description |
|---|---|
| $\text{Preference}_j$ | Dependent variable: preference score or rank for profile $j$ (note: if using ranks, lower rank number = higher preference; may need to reverse-code) |
| $\beta_0$ | Intercept (baseline utility level) |
| $\beta_k$ | Part-worth coefficient for attribute level $k$ — the contribution of that level to preference relative to the omitted reference level |
| $X_{kj}$ | Dummy variable (0 or 1) indicating whether profile $j$ has attribute level $k$ |
| $\varepsilon_j$ | Error term |

**Interpretation**: The sign and magnitude of each $\beta_k$ indicate whether and how much a given attribute level increases or decreases preference. A larger absolute value of $\beta$ indicates a stronger preference effect for that level. The range of part-worths for an attribute (max $\beta$ − min $\beta$ for levels of that attribute) measures the attribute's importance. Relative importance is the range for that attribute divided by the sum of all attributes' ranges, expressed as a percentage.

**Example**: Using the exam practice problem (smartphone attributes: screen size, battery life, brand; 8 profiles ranked 1–8, lower rank = higher preference):

Average ranks by level:
- Screen: Wide avg = (3+2+1+4)/4 = 2.5; Small avg = (8+7+6+5)/4 = 6.5 → Range = |6.5 − 2.5| = 4.0
- Battery: 16hr avg = (3+7+1+5)/4 = 4.0; 10hr avg = (8+2+6+4)/4 = 5.0 → Range = |5.0 − 4.0| = 1.0
- Brand: Samsung avg = (2+6+1+5)/4 = 3.5; iPhone avg = (8+3+7+4)/4 = 5.5 → Range = |5.5 − 3.5| = 2.0

Total range sum = 4.0 + 1.0 + 2.0 = 7.0

Relative importance: Screen size = 4.0/7.0 = **57.1%**; Battery life = 1.0/7.0 = **14.3%**; Brand = 2.0/7.0 = **28.6%**.

Most important attribute: **Screen size** (largest range).

**Common Mistakes**:
- Forgetting to check the direction of preference (lower rank number = higher preference in ranking-based conjoint; if regressing rank on dummies, a negative coefficient means that level is preferred).
- Dividing by the wrong total (must divide each attribute range by the sum of ALL attribute ranges, not by the maximum range).
- Confusing the regression coefficient (part-worth) with the importance weight — importance weights are derived from the range of part-worths, not from the coefficients directly.
- Failing to handle reversed ranking: if lower rank = more preferred, consider reverse-coding before regression (e.g., use 9 − rank as the utility score for 8 profiles) so that positive coefficients indicate more preferred levels.
- Using percentages that do not sum to 100% (always verify the sum).

### Formula 4: Slack Time in Network Diagrams

**Formula**:

$$\text{Slack} = LS - ES = LF - EF$$

**Variables**:

| Variable | Description |
|---|---|
| ES | Early Start: the earliest time an activity can begin (forward pass) |
| EF | Early Finish: ES + Activity Duration |
| LF | Late Finish: the latest time an activity can finish without delaying the project (backward pass) |
| LS | Late Start: LF − Activity Duration |
| Slack | Amount of time an activity can be delayed without delaying project completion |

**Interpretation**: Slack = 0 means the activity is on the critical path. Any delay to a critical path activity delays the entire project by the same amount. Activities with positive slack have scheduling flexibility. Total project duration = the duration of the critical path.

**Example**: If Activity B has ES = 3, EF = 7, LF = 10, LS = 6, then Slack = 6 − 3 = 3 days. Activity B can be delayed up to 3 days without affecting project completion.

**Common Mistakes**:
- Confusing ES and LS (ES is from the forward pass; LS is from the backward pass).
- Forgetting to complete both the forward pass and backward pass before computing slack.
- Assuming the critical path is the shortest path (it is the longest — the bottleneck that determines project duration).
- Crashing a non-critical activity (wastes money without reducing project duration).

### Formula 5: Project Crashing Decision

**Formula (Crash Cost per Unit Time)**:

$$\text{Crash Cost Rate} = \frac{\text{Crash Cost} - \text{Normal Cost}}{\text{Normal Duration} - \text{Crash Duration}}$$

**Variables**:

| Variable | Description |
|---|---|
| Crash Cost | Total cost of the activity when it is crashed to its minimum duration |
| Normal Cost | Total cost of the activity at its normal (planned) duration |
| Normal Duration | Planned duration of the activity |
| Crash Duration | Minimum achievable duration after adding resources |
| Crash Cost Rate | Additional cost incurred per unit of time saved |

**Interpretation**: To shorten project duration at minimum additional cost, crash the critical path activity with the lowest Crash Cost Rate. After crashing, re-check whether the critical path has changed (a new path may have become critical). Only crash activities on the current critical path — crashing non-critical activities does not reduce project duration.

**Example**: Activity A has Normal Duration = 10 days, Normal Cost = $500, Crash Duration = 8 days, Crash Cost = $700. Crash Cost Rate = ($700 − $500) / (10 − 8) = $200 / 2 = $100/day. If Activity B has a Crash Cost Rate of $150/day, crash Activity A first (cheaper per day saved).

**Common Mistakes**:
- Crashing activities not on the critical path.
- Failing to re-identify the critical path after each crashing step.
- Crashing below the minimum crash duration.
- Not comparing crash cost rates across all critical path activities before deciding which to crash.

### Practice MCQ 1: Product Classification — Ace Corporation Detergent Pod

**Scenario**: Ace Corporation manufactures powder and liquid dishwashing detergents. Due to popular demand, the company introduces a single-load, scented, gel detergent encased in water-soluble bags designed to fit dishwasher detergent trays.

**Question**: The firm's new product is best described as a(n):
- A. new-to-the-world product
- B. line extension
- C. repositioned product
- D. improvised product
- E. cost reduction

**Answer: B — Line Extension**

**Explanation**: A line extension is an addition to an existing product line designed to flesh out the portfolio for the firm's current markets. The detergent pod is a new format (gel in water-soluble bags) added to Ace's existing detergent portfolio for the same target market (dishwasher users). It is not new-to-the-world because detergent pods already existed as a product category concept; it is not a repositioning because the target customer and use case remain the same; it is not a cost reduction because the format change adds product value; it is not an improvised product (not a recognized NPD category).

**Exam Skill Tested**: Accurate classification of new product types.

### Practice MCQ 2: Critical Path Definition

**Scenario**: Project management network diagram question.

**Question**: Which one of the following best describes the critical path of a PERT network?
- A. the sequence of activities between a project's start and finish that takes the longest time to complete
- B. the sequence of activities that has the maximum amount of activity slack
- C. the set of activities that has no precedence relationships
- D. the sequence of activities that has the lowest normal activity cost
- E. None of the above

**Answer: A**

**Explanation**: The critical path is defined as the longest path from start to finish in a project network diagram. It determines the minimum total project duration. Activities on the critical path have zero slack — any delay to them delays the entire project. Option B is wrong: maximum slack means those activities are NOT on the critical path. Option C is wrong: activities with no precedence relationships are unconstrained, but that does not define the critical path. Option D is wrong: cost is irrelevant to the critical path definition.

**Exam Skill Tested**: Precise understanding of project management terminology.

### Practice Short Answer 1: Benefits of Modular Design Under Demand Uncertainty

**Question**: What are the key benefits of designing the product to be modular under demand uncertainty? Describe within 3–4 sentences.

**Model Answer**: Modular design allows a firm to produce a variety of end-product configurations by recombining a set of standardized, interchangeable modules, rather than redesigning entirely different products for each customer segment. Under demand uncertainty, this architecture reduces inventory risk because the firm can hold modules rather than finished goods and assemble to order once customer preferences are revealed. Modularity also enables faster time-to-market for new variants — when customer demand shifts, only the relevant module needs to be redesigned rather than the entire product. Additionally, shared modules across product variants generate scale economies in production, lowering per-unit cost while maintaining the appearance of a customized product portfolio.

**Exam Skill Tested**: Understanding of modular architecture rationale; concise written explanation in 3–4 sentences.

### Practice Short Answer 2: Conjoint Analysis — Smartphone Attribute Importance

**Scenario**: A consumer ranked her preference on 8 combinations of three smartphone attributes (lower rank = higher preference): screen size (small/wide), battery life (10hr/16hr), brand (iPhone/Samsung). Data:

| Screen Size | Battery Life | Brand | Rank |
|---|---|---|---|
| small | 10hr | iphone | 8 |
| wide | 16hr | iphone | 3 |
| wide | 10hr | samsung | 2 |
| small | 16hr | iphone | 7 |
| small | 10hr | samsung | 6 |
| wide | 16hr | samsung | 1 |
| wide | 10hr | iphone | 4 |
| small | 16hr | samsung | 5 |

**Question 2: Which attribute is most important for this consumer?**

**Step-by-step solution**:

Compute average rank for each level of each attribute (lower average rank = more preferred):

**Screen Size**:
- Wide (ranks 3, 2, 1, 4): average = 10/4 = 2.5
- Small (ranks 8, 7, 6, 5): average = 26/4 = 6.5
- Range = |6.5 − 2.5| = **4.0**

**Battery Life**:
- 16hr (ranks 3, 7, 1, 5): average = 16/4 = 4.0
- 10hr (ranks 8, 2, 6, 4): average = 20/4 = 5.0
- Range = |5.0 − 4.0| = **1.0**

**Brand**:
- Samsung (ranks 2, 6, 1, 5): average = 14/4 = 3.5
- iPhone (ranks 8, 3, 7, 4): average = 22/4 = 5.5
- Range = |5.5 − 3.5| = **2.0**

**Answer: Screen size** (largest range = 4.0; this attribute most changes the consumer's preference).

---

**Question 3: Distribute the relative importance of the three attributes.**

Total range sum = 4.0 + 1.0 + 2.0 = 7.0

| Attribute | Range | Relative Importance |
|---|---|---|
| Screen size | 4.0 | 4.0 / 7.0 = **57.1%** |
| Battery life | 1.0 | 1.0 / 7.0 = **14.3%** |
| Brand | 2.0 | 2.0 / 7.0 = **28.6%** |
| **Total** | **7.0** | **100.0%** |

**Answer: Screen size 57.1%, Battery life 14.3%, Brand 28.6%**

## Key Takeaways

- **New product classification** matters on the exam: a line extension adds to an existing product line for existing markets; a new-to-the-world product has no predecessor; a repositioned product targets a new market or use case with essentially the same product.
- **Conjoint analysis attribute importance** is calculated as: range of average ranks for an attribute / sum of all attributes' ranges × 100%. The attribute with the largest range is the most important. This calculation must be practiced until automatic.
- **The concept scoring matrix** is not an automatic selector — the "combine and improve" step is always required; criteria weights must come from consumer surveys, not internal judgment; and sensitivity analysis guards against selecting the "best average product."
- **The critical path** is the longest path through a project network (not the shortest, not the lowest cost, not the path with maximum slack); activities on the critical path have zero slack and any delay to them delays the entire project.
- **Slack time** = LS − ES = LF − EF; computed by completing both the forward pass (ES, EF) and backward pass (LS, LF) through the network diagram.
- **Project crashing** prioritizes the critical path activity with the lowest crash cost per unit of time saved; after each crashing step, re-identify the critical path before deciding the next crash.
- **Modular design** under demand uncertainty enables mass customization, reduces finished-goods inventory risk (hold modules, assemble to order), speeds new variant development, and achieves scale economies on shared modules.
- **Keller's Brand Resonance Pyramid** builds from bottom to top: Salience → Performance + Imagery → Judgments + Feelings → Resonance; resonance (loyalty, attachment, community, active engagement) is the ultimate goal and cannot be achieved without the lower levels being established first.
- **A/B testing and factorial design** establish causality through random assignment — correlation alone is not causality; experiments require control and treatment groups, and results are interpreted via p-values (< 0.05 = statistically significant), confidence intervals (not containing zero = significant effect), and regression coefficients (estimated effect size).
- **RWW (Real-Win-Worth It)** is the primary opportunity screening framework: real = genuine market need + technical achievability; win = sustainable competitive advantage; worth it = sufficient financial return at acceptable risk; all three must be satisfied to proceed.

## Key Terms and Definitions Glossary

**A/B Testing**: A controlled market experiment assigning participants randomly to a control version (A) and a treatment version (B) to compare outcome metrics and establish causal impact of a product, feature, price, or message change. [Related: Factorial Design, Causality, Market Testing]

**Brand**: A name, term, sign, symbol, design, or combination thereof intended to identify the goods or services of one seller and differentiate them from competitors. [Related: Brand Equity, Brand Identity, Brand Image]

**Brand Equity**: The added value a brand name confers on a product beyond its functional benefits, reflected in consumer perceptions, preferences, and behaviors. [Related: Brand, Brand Associations, Keller's Pyramid]

**Brand Resonance**: The highest level of Keller's Brand Resonance Pyramid, characterized by a deep psychological relationship between consumer and brand, expressed through behavioral loyalty, attitudinal attachment, sense of community, and active engagement. [Related: Keller's Brand Resonance Pyramid, Brand Salience, Brand Judgments]

**Concept Scoring Matrix**: A quantitative concept selection tool that multiplies consumer-derived importance weights by concept ratings on each criterion and sums the products to produce a total score per concept; used after concept screening to make finer distinctions among surviving concepts. [Related: Concept Screening Matrix, Weighted Score, Criteria Weighting]

**Concept Screening Matrix**: A coarse concept selection tool using a +/0/− scale relative to a reference concept to eliminate clearly inferior alternatives before applying the more detailed scoring matrix. [Related: Concept Scoring Matrix, Reference Concept]

**Conjoint Analysis**: A quantitative concept testing technique that decomposes overall consumer preference into separate part-worth contributions of each product attribute and level, estimated via regression; used to identify the most important attributes and simulate preference for new product configurations. [Related: Part-Worth, Attribute Importance, Regression]

**Critical Path**: The sequence of activities from project start to finish that takes the longest time to complete, thereby determining the minimum total project duration; all activities on the critical path have zero slack. [Related: Network Diagram, Slack Time, PERT]

**Delayed Differentiation (Postponement)**: A supply chain strategy in which a product is manufactured in generic (undifferentiated) form up to a late stage, with final customization deferred until actual demand is known; reduces inventory risk by avoiding commitment to specific variants before demand is certain. Enabled by modular product architecture. [Related: Modular Design, Mass Customization, Demand Uncertainty]

**Endowment Effect**: A cognitive bias in which people assign higher value to objects they already own than to identical objects they do not own, simply by virtue of ownership; first demonstrated by Kahneman, Knetsch, and Thaler (1990) using coffee mugs; relevant to product trials, free samples, and pricing strategy. [Related: Behavioral Economics, A/B Testing, Randomized Controlled Trial]

**Factorial Design**: An experimental design that tests all combinations of multiple independent variables (factors) simultaneously, enabling estimation of main effects and interaction effects; more efficient than running separate A/B tests for each factor. [Related: A/B Testing, Main Effect, Interaction Effect]

**Jobs-to-Be-Done**: A customer research philosophy that frames needs as functional, social, or emotional "jobs" consumers are trying to accomplish, revealing latent needs and non-obvious competitors more effectively than attribute-focused surveys. [Related: Latent Needs, Customer Needs Analysis, Opportunity Identification]

**Keller's Brand Resonance Pyramid**: A four-level model of brand building progressing from Brand Salience (identity) → Brand Performance + Brand Imagery (meaning) → Brand Judgments + Brand Feelings (response) → Brand Resonance (relationship); each level must be built before the next can be achieved. [Related: Brand Equity, Brand Salience, Brand Resonance]

**Line Extension**: A new product that is an addition to an existing product line of the firm, designed to broaden the portfolio for the firm's current markets (e.g., a new flavor, format, or size within an existing category). [Related: New Product Types, Product Portfolio]

**Modular Design**: A product architecture composed of standardized, interchangeable modules that can be combined in different configurations to produce product variety without redesigning from scratch; enables mass customization, reduces inventory risk under demand uncertainty, and generates scale economies on shared modules. [Related: Mass Customization, Product Architecture, Demand Uncertainty]

**Network Diagram (PERT/CPM)**: A graphical representation of project activities and their precedence relationships used to identify the critical path, compute slack times, and make crashing decisions; requires both a forward pass (computing ES, EF) and backward pass (computing LF, LS). [Related: Critical Path, Slack Time, Work-Breakdown Structure]

**Part-Worth**: The contribution of a specific attribute level to a consumer's overall product preference, estimated as a regression coefficient in conjoint analysis; used to calculate attribute importance (range of part-worths) and to simulate preference for new product profiles. [Related: Conjoint Analysis, Attribute Importance, Regression]

**Poka-Yoke**: A design principle or device that makes it impossible or very difficult to use a product incorrectly, eliminating error at the source rather than detecting it after the fact; a key principle of quality-by-design that reduces defects through mistake-proofing. [Related: Modular Design, Product Architecture, Quality]

**Porter's Five Forces**: A framework for evaluating industry competitive attractiveness through five structural forces: Threat of New Entrants, Bargaining Power of Buyers, Bargaining Power of Suppliers, Threat of Substitute Products, and Intensity of Rivalry among existing competitors. [Related: PESTEL, Industry Environment, RWW Analysis]

**Project Crashing**: The deliberate reduction of activity durations by adding resources to shorten total project duration, at additional cost; crashing decisions prioritize the critical path activity with the lowest crash cost per unit of time saved, and the critical path must be re-identified after each crash step. [Related: Critical Path, Slack Time, Crash Cost Rate]

**RWW Analysis (Real-Win-Worth It)**: A three-question opportunity evaluation framework: Is it Real? (genuine market need + technical achievability), Can We Win? (sustainable competitive advantage), Is it Worth It? (sufficient financial return at acceptable risk); all three must be satisfied to pursue an opportunity. [Related: Opportunity Identification, Desirability/Viability/Feasibility, Porter's Five Forces]

**Slack Time (Float)**: The amount of time an activity in a project network can be delayed without delaying the total project completion date; computed as LS − ES or LF − EF; activities on the critical path have slack = 0. [Related: Critical Path, Network Diagram, Forward Pass, Backward Pass]

**TAM/SAM/SOM**: Three nested market sizing estimates — Total Addressable Market (theoretical maximum at 100% share), Serviceable Addressable Market (realistic target segment), and Serviceable Obtainable Market (credibly capturable share given competition); SOM is the operationally relevant planning figure. [Related: Market Sizing, RWW Analysis, Guesstimation]

**Work-Breakdown Structure (WBS)**: A hierarchical decomposition of a project into progressively smaller components and individual work packages (tasks), serving as the input for constructing a project network diagram and identifying precedence relationships. [Related: Network Diagram, Project Management, Critical Path]
