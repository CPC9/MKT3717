# Supplementary Reading: Slack Launch Strategy — From 0 to $1B

## Overview

Slack — the internal team-communication platform founded by Stewart Butterfield (previously co-founder of Flickr) — grew from zero to a $1 billion valuation in roughly two years without running a single major integrated marketing campaign, without buying billboards, without an elaborate email strategy, and without ever hiring a CMO. Its explosive growth was driven instead by an obsessive commitment to customer feedback, a disciplined focus on a small number of differentiating product features, and a go-to-market approach that prioritized real adoption over vanity metrics. The article, published by First Round Review, is an exclusive interview with Butterfield in which he unpacks every major decision that shaped Slack's launch.

The central strategic insight running through Butterfield's account is that Slack's success was engineered through iteration, not inspiration. Beginning in May 2013 with just six to ten hand-picked friend companies, the team shared progressively larger beta cohorts, watched how the product broke under new conditions, fixed what was broken, and then repeated the cycle. This process revealed problems that the founding team could never have discovered in isolation — most notably, that team-scale dynamics (channel proliferation, onboarding for new hires, veto power of individual dissenters) are fundamentally different from individual-user dynamics. Slack treated its entire preview period, which lasted more than six months, as a structured learning exercise rather than a countdown to a "real" launch.

Equally important was how Slack defined and pursued retention. Rather than relying on industry-standard metrics, Butterfield identified a product-specific "magic number" — 2,000 messages exchanged by a team — as the threshold at which 93% of teams remained active users. This kind of custom activation metric gave the team a concrete target around which to align product improvements, onboarding flows, and support interventions. Combined with treating every support interaction as a marketing opportunity, maintaining a 24/7 Twitter response team, and investing heavily in customer experience as a first-class function, Slack converted what most companies treat as a cost center into one of its primary growth engines.

## Key Concepts

### Go-to-Market Strategy

#### Customer Feedback as Strategy Core
**Definition:** Making the systematic collection, processing, and actioning of user feedback the central organizing principle of all go-to-market decisions, rather than treating it as a peripheral quality-control activity.

**Context:** Slack's entire preview period (August 2013 – February 2014) was structured around feedback loops. Every email Slack sent allowed users to reply directly. Every help ticket was treated as an opportunity to improve the product and build loyalty. Butterfield personally handled the lion's share of Twitter responses in the early days. The company received approximately 8,000 Zendesk help tickets and 10,000 tweets per month at the time of the article, and responded to all of them.

**Relationships:** Directly enables Word-of-Mouth Growth (excellent support converts complainers into advocates), Product Differentiation (feedback reveals which features actually matter), and the Magic Number framework (feedback data reveals where users are failing to reach activation).

#### Bottoms-Up Enterprise Adoption
**Definition:** A go-to-market motion in which a product enters large organizations through individual teams or departments — bypassing top-level IT procurement — because the per-team price point is low enough to be expensed by mid-level managers.

**Context:** Adobe had nine separate paid Slack teams before any company-wide procurement decision was made. Slack deliberately avoided requiring CIO sign-off or security audit reviews in its early phase. Mid-level managers could try Slack on their team, find it valuable, and expense it — creating organic expansion within enterprises without a traditional enterprise sales cycle.

**Relationships:** Connected to Team-Level Veto Risk (the downside of team-level adoption is that a single resister can kill the deal), Category Education (managers need to understand why the product category exists to make the expense decision), and Freemium/Low-Friction Pricing (the price point must be low enough to expense without approval).

#### Category Creation and Market Education
**Definition:** When a product operates in a nascent or undefined market, a significant portion of the go-to-market strategy must be dedicated to convincing prospects that the product category itself is legitimate and necessary — before selling the specific product.

**Context:** Butterfield found that 70–80% of potential Slack users said they used "nothing" for internal communication when asked. In reality, they were using a fragmented patchwork of email, mailing lists, Hangouts, SMS, Skype chat, private Facebook groups, and Google+ pages — but they did not conceptualize this as a software category requiring a dedicated solution. Slack had to sell the category (team messaging as software) before it could sell itself.

**Relationships:** Closely linked to Bottoms-Up Adoption (teams can experiment without formal category recognition), Product Positioning (positioning must address both category recognition and product superiority), and Market Education Materials (content must educate before it persuades).

#### Team-Level Veto Dynamics
**Definition:** In B2B products adopted at the team level, any single team member who refuses to use the product can effectively block the entire team's adoption, making individual satisfaction across all members — not just the decision-maker — a prerequisite for success.

**Context:** Butterfield identified this as Slack's most acute early risk: "If one engineer at a startup tries Slack and says, 'I hate it. I am not going to use this,' that's it for us. We won't get evaluated." This asymmetry — where one dissenter can override many enthusiasts — makes reducing friction and maximizing first-impression quality far more critical for team tools than for individual-user tools.

**Relationships:** Drives the need for Onboarding Resource Investment (resources built for both individual users and team administrators), Beta Testing with Diverse Teams (testing across many team dynamics to find failure points), and the 2,000-Message Magic Number (reaching the threshold requires the whole team to participate).

### Launch Mechanics

#### Preview Release vs. Beta Framing
**Definition:** The deliberate choice to label an early, limited-availability product as a "preview release" rather than a "beta" in order to signal quality and reliability rather than unfinished and potentially unstable software.

**Context:** Slack's August 2013 announcement was functionally a beta, but Butterfield explicitly avoided the word "beta" because it connotes flakiness and unreliability. By calling it a preview release and pairing it with a press blitz that generated 8,000 sign-up requests on day one and 15,000 within two weeks, Slack signaled that the product was ready for serious use while still controlling the rollout pace.

**Relationships:** Connected to Press and PR Strategy (framing only works if paired with credible media coverage), Invitation-Based Rollout (controlling access reinforces scarcity and quality perception), and Active Listening (the preview period exists to harvest feedback, not to delay launch).

#### Press and PR as Launch Multiplier
**Definition:** Traditional media coverage is a necessary but insufficient component of launch success; the article estimates that earned media accounts for only 20% of media impact, with the remaining 80% coming from secondary social sharing of that coverage.

**Context:** Slack leveraged the founders' prior reputations (Butterfield's Flickr background) to generate press coverage at launch. Butterfield's formula: the direct audience reading a news article is a small fraction of the total reach; what actually drives awareness is people in your network sharing that article. Therefore, maximizing secondary sharing — by engaging influencers and high-follower accounts who cover the story — is more valuable than the original placement.

**Relationships:** Connected to Word-of-Mouth Growth (social sharing is a form of digital word of mouth), Twitter as a Growth Channel (Twitter was Slack's primary secondary-sharing amplifier), and Preview Release Framing (a well-framed story is more shareable).

#### Invitation-Based Rollout and Batch Testing
**Definition:** Releasing a product by inviting cohorts of users in controlled batches, observing how each batch uses the product, iterating on the product, and then inviting a larger or different batch — using each wave as a structured experiment.

**Context:** Slack began with 6–10 friend companies in May 2013, including Cozy and Rdio. Rdio, at 120 employees, revealed scale problems with channel management that the small Slack team had never encountered. The team fixed those issues and then widened the next cohort. This continued through the August 2013 public preview (8,000 day-one requests) and through February 2014, when Slack publicly launched after processing those initial 15,000 sign-ups and improving the new-user experience systematically.

**Relationships:** Linked to Active Listening (each batch generates structured feedback), Scaling Feedback Amplification (larger teams surface different problems), and Magic Number Identification (batch data reveals which usage patterns predict retention).

### Metrics and Retention

#### The Magic Number
**Definition:** A product-specific activation threshold — identified through observation of actual usage patterns rather than industry benchmarks — at which retention becomes highly predictable and reliable.

**Context:** Slack's magic number is 2,000 messages exchanged by a team. Butterfield's analysis: for a team of 50 people, that represents roughly 10 hours of messages; for a team of 10, roughly one week of messages. The critical finding is that after crossing the 2,000-message threshold, 93% of teams remain active Slack users, regardless of any other variable. This single number became the focal point for onboarding design, activation campaigns, and product iteration.

**Relationships:** Enables Activation Metric Design (the number defines what "activated" means), Product Iteration Focus (the team can design features specifically to help teams cross the threshold), and Retention Prediction (93% retention rate is the business case for investing in activation).

#### Activation, Conversion, and Retention Metrics
**Definition:** The three distinct stages of a SaaS user lifecycle — activation (first meaningful use), conversion (becoming a paying customer), and retention (continuing to use over time) — each requiring a company-specific definition rather than relying on generic industry metrics.

**Context:** Butterfield noted that more than 90% of people who complete Slack's sign-up form never invite a single team member or send a message. Of roughly 220,000 teams created, only about 30,000 were actively using Slack. However, because active teams averaged 8–9 members, Slack had approximately 250,000 daily active users — more daily active users than total teams ever created. The key insight: losing the majority of sign-ups is acceptable if the minority who activate are deeply retained.

**Relationships:** Directly tied to the Magic Number (defines when activation is complete), Onboarding Investment (the gap between sign-up and activation is the problem to solve), and Bottoms-Up Adoption (team-level retention is the real unit of measurement).

### Product Strategy

#### Focused Differentiation: Do Three Things Brilliantly
**Definition:** Rather than building a complete feature set, choose a small number (Butterfield says three) of specific capabilities to execute at an exceptional level, accepting mediocrity or absence in other areas in exchange for being unambiguously best-in-class at the chosen few.

**Context:** Slack's three chosen areas were: (1) Search — users must be able to find any past message or file instantly, removing the need to manually organize or label content; (2) Synchronization — "leave-state synchronization" means Slack tracks exactly where each user left off in every conversation across all devices, solving the friction of switching between laptop and phone; (3) Simple file-sharing — intuitive drag-and-drop and paste-image capabilities that reduce daily friction for power users. These were not flashy or buzzworthy features; they were foundational to the core use case.

**Relationships:** Inspired by the Buchheit Framework (Paul Buchheit's "If your product is Great, it doesn't need to be Good"), connected to Active Listening (feedback data validates which three features matter most), and drives Product Positioning (differentiation must be built before it can be communicated).

#### Customer Experience as a First-Class Function
**Definition:** Organizationally treating customer support not as a cost center but as a strategic capability that generates qualitative insight, drives product improvement, and converts service interactions into word-of-mouth marketing.

**Context:** Slack combined quality assurance and customer support into a single group called "customer experience" very early in the company's life. At the time of the article, that group had grown to 18 people in customer support plus 6 dedicated exclusively to Twitter monitoring around the clock. Butterfield personally handled the majority of Twitter responses himself in the early days. Every help ticket and tweet was tagged, collated, and stored in searchable Slack channels, giving the product team a direct line to real user pain.

**Relationships:** Enables Word-of-Mouth Growth (exceptional service turns complaints into advocates), Qualitative Data Collection (support interactions are rich signals), and the Twitter Wall of Love (the organic testimonials Slack became famous for originated as customer service interactions).

## Frameworks & Mental Models

### The Buchheit Framework: "If Your Product Is Great, It Doesn't Need to Be Good"

**Visual Description:** Imagine a horizontal bar divided into many feature categories. In a conventional product strategy, resources are spread thinly across all bars trying to make each "good enough." The Buchheit framework collapses most bars to zero and redirects all resources into making just a few bars extraordinarily tall — far above the baseline of any competitor.

**How to Apply:** Identify the two to four features or capabilities that are most essential to your specific value proposition. Deliberately invest in making those features world-class. Accept that the product will be missing features that competitors have and that customers might ask for. Use customer feedback not to expand the feature list but to validate that the chosen features are delivering exceptional value. Build product roadmap around deepening these capabilities before adding new ones.

**When to Use:** During the product definition and early launch phases of a new product, particularly when entering a market where competitors already exist with broader but shallower feature sets. Also valuable when the team has limited resources and cannot afford to build everything competently. Less appropriate once the product is mature and table-stakes feature parity is required to compete.

### The Progressively Larger Beta Cohort Model

**Visual Description:** Picture a series of concentric rings. The innermost ring is the founding team. The next ring is a handful of trusted friend companies (6–10). Each subsequent ring is larger — first a dozen companies, then dozens, then hundreds of sign-ups from a public invitation — with a "fix and iterate" checkpoint between each ring before advancing to the next.

**How to Apply:** Begin by using the product internally to identify the most obvious deficiencies. Select 6–10 external teams that you trust and who represent the user profile you are targeting. Watch exactly how they use the product, not just what they say about it. Fix the problems uncovered, then invite a new, larger cohort that includes different team sizes and functions. Repeat until the new-user experience is smooth enough that incremental feedback is delivering diminishing returns. Do not advance to the next ring until you have genuinely resolved the issues found in the current ring.

**When to Use:** Ideal for products where the core value is collaborative or social — i.e., the product only works if multiple people at an organization adopt it. Also applicable when the founding team suspects that scale (team size, company size, number of integrations) will reveal qualitatively different problems than small-scale testing. Less necessary for products with purely individual use cases.

### The 80/20 Media Amplification Model

**Visual Description:** A two-part pie chart. The smaller slice (20%) represents the direct readership of a published article or press placement. The larger slice (80%) represents the secondary audience reached when readers share that article through their own social networks, email, and conversations. Traditional PR focuses on earning the 20%; the Butterfield model treats the 80% as the primary target.

**How to Apply:** After securing press coverage, immediately share the article through every available channel — the company's social accounts, personal accounts of founders and employees, investor networks, and early customer communities. Identify and engage with high-follower individuals who have already organically shared or commented on the story; their resharing reaches a disproportionately large secondary audience. Do not worry about appearing repetitive; the goal is sustained top-of-mind presence across the extended network, not a single spike.

**When to Use:** At every media moment, but especially at launch when the goal is to maximize awareness among potential early adopters who are unlikely to encounter a news article directly. Particularly powerful in B2B SaaS, where target buyers are concentrated in professional networks on platforms like Twitter and LinkedIn where articles are frequently shared.

### The Magic Number Framework for Activation Design

**Visual Description:** A timeline of a team's journey inside a product. On the far left: sign-up. On the far right: long-term retention. In between, mark a single vertical line — the "magic number" — the threshold at which retention probability becomes very high. The goal of the entire onboarding and activation strategy is to move teams from sign-up to that line as quickly as possible.

**How to Apply:** Analyze cohort retention data to identify the usage milestone (could be a number of messages, sessions, integrations configured, files shared, or any other measurable action) at which retained users diverge sharply from churned users. Define this as the company's magic number. Then design every onboarding touchpoint — email sequences, in-product prompts, help content, administrator guides — to push users toward that milestone. Measure activation rate (percentage of sign-ups that cross the threshold) as a primary growth metric.

**When to Use:** As soon as the company has enough cohort data to detect a retention pattern. This framework is applicable to virtually any SaaS product but is especially important for collaborative tools where adoption requires convincing multiple people simultaneously. The magic number should be revisited as the product and user base evolve.

## Case Studies

### Slack: From Gaming Pivot to $1 Billion in Two Years

**Company:** Slack (formally Tiny Speck), founded by Stewart Butterfield. Butterfield previously co-founded Flickr. The company originally attempted to build a web-based massively multiplayer game before pivoting to internal team communication software. Development began at the end of 2012; the product reached $1B valuation within approximately two years of founding.

**Challenge:** Slack faced a compound challenge at launch. First, the product was entering what most potential users did not recognize as a defined software category — most teams believed they used "nothing" for internal communication, when they were actually using fragmented combinations of email, SMS, Hangouts, Skype, and informal tools. Second, selling a collaboration tool to teams rather than individuals introduced a veto dynamic: any single team member who rejected the product could block the entire team's adoption. Third, Slack had no CMO, no large marketing budget, and no established enterprise sales motion. It had to grow primarily through direct user love and word of mouth.

**What Was Done:**
- Began with internal team use (March 2013), then expanded to 6–10 trusted friend companies (May 2013), including Cozy and Rdio, to observe how the product performed across different team sizes and dynamics.
- Used progressively larger beta cohorts, iterating on the product between each cohort based on observed usage problems. Rdio's 120-person team revealed channel-management problems at scale (e.g., inability for new hires to navigate proliferating channels) that led to specific product improvements like adding channel descriptions and member counts.
- Deliberately avoided calling the August 2013 release a "beta" to signal quality and reliability; paired the preview launch with a press blitz leveraging founders' reputations, generating 8,000 sign-up requests on day one and 15,000 within two weeks.
- Spent six months (August 2013 – February 2014) in controlled preview, systematically processing the initial sign-up queue, improving the new-user experience, and building educational materials for both individual users and team administrators.
- Identified the 2,000-message magic number as the activation threshold beyond which 93% of teams were retained, and focused product and onboarding efforts on getting teams to that milestone.
- Built customer experience as a core organizational function from day one: combined QA and support, made every email replyable, treated every support ticket as a retention and marketing opportunity, and personally maintained a heavy Twitter presence. Grew to 18 support staff plus 6 on Twitter 24/7.
- Executed a bottoms-up enterprise strategy: priced and designed the product so that mid-level managers could adopt it for their team without CIO approval, enabling organic spread within large enterprises (e.g., Adobe with 9 paid teams).
- Focused all product investment on three differentiating features — search, leave-state synchronization, and simple file-sharing — guided by Paul Buchheit's principle that excellence in a few dimensions outweighs adequacy across many.

**Takeaway:** Slack's growth demonstrates that in B2B SaaS, especially for a new product category, the most powerful go-to-market strategy combines structured feedback loops, a ruthlessly focused product, low-friction bottoms-up adoption, and a customer experience operation that converts every interaction into a marketing moment. Traditional advertising and a large marketing organization are not prerequisites for rapid, authentic growth — but an exceptional product that people want to tell others about is non-negotiable. The company's willingness to stay in preview longer than conventional wisdom might suggest, and to prioritize learning over speed, was a key competitive advantage.

## Key Takeaways

- **Customer feedback as a growth engine:** Slack made customer feedback the operational center of its entire go-to-market strategy — not a periodic input, but a continuous loop that drove product iteration, support investment, and word-of-mouth advocacy. Every email was replyable; every help ticket was a learning opportunity.
- **Progressively larger beta cohorts:** Rather than launching broadly and iterating reactively, Slack used controlled cohort expansion — sharing the product with progressively larger and more diverse groups, fixing problems between cohorts, and only widening when the current issues were resolved. This approach de-risked the public launch and eliminated "gnarly" product failures before mass exposure.
- **The magic number drives activation strategy:** Identifying a product-specific activation threshold (Slack's: 2,000 messages = 93% retention) gives a company a concrete target around which to align onboarding design, email sequences, in-product prompts, and support interventions. Industry-standard metrics cannot substitute for this company-specific analysis.
- **Bottoms-up enterprise adoption bypasses procurement friction:** By pricing Slack at a level that mid-level managers could expense without IT approval, Slack entered large enterprises team by team, building adoption organically before ever facing a formal procurement process. Adobe had nine paid Slack teams before any company-wide decision.
- **Category education is part of the go-to-market strategy:** When 70–80% of target customers do not recognize that they have a problem your product solves, selling the category must come before selling the product. Slack invested as heavily in explaining what team messaging software is as in explaining what Slack does.
- **Team-level veto risk demands exceptional first impressions:** Because a single resistant team member can block a collaborative tool's adoption entirely, minimizing friction and maximizing the quality of the first experience is more critical for team tools than for individual-user products. Slack built both individual user guides and administrator resources specifically to arm internal advocates.
- **Focused differentiation beats comprehensive feature parity:** Guided by Paul Buchheit's principle, Slack chose three features to execute at an exceptional level — **search**, **leave-state synchronization**, and **simple file-sharing** — and accepted incompleteness in other areas. Users who loved those three things were not deterred by missing features.
- **Traditional media provides 20% of reach; social amplification provides 80%:** Earning press coverage is necessary but not sufficient. The majority of awareness impact comes from secondary sharing through social networks. Founders should plan the amplification strategy (engaging influencers, sharing across all channels, sustaining momentum) as carefully as they plan the press pitch itself.
- **Every customer service interaction is a marketing moment:** Slack's "Wall of Love" — the stream of enthusiastic tweets about the product — was largely built from customer service interactions that started as complaints. Going above and beyond in support converts detractors into advocates who broadcast their satisfaction publicly.
- **Qualitative and quantitative data must tell the same story:** Hard metrics (DAUs, retention rates, revenue) should be validated against qualitative signals (support tickets, tweets, user interviews). When the two diverge, the company has either a measurement problem or a product problem that the numbers are not yet capturing.

## Key Terms and Definitions Glossary

**Activation:** The moment at which a user or team crosses a product-specific usage threshold that predicts sustained retention. For Slack, activation is defined as a team exchanging 2,000 messages. [Related: Magic Number, Retention, Conversion]

**Bottoms-Up Adoption:** A B2B go-to-market motion in which a product enters organizations through individual teams or departments at a price point that does not require senior management approval, spreading organically before formal enterprise procurement begins. [Related: Team-Level Veto, Freemium, Enterprise Sales]

**Buchheit Principle:** Paul Buchheit's framework (articulated in his blog post "If your product is Great, it doesn't need to be Good") stating that excellence in a few critical dimensions is more valuable than broad adequacy across many features. Named for the creator of Gmail. [Related: Focused Differentiation, Product Strategy, Core Differentiators]

**Category Creation:** The go-to-market challenge faced by products that operate in markets where prospective customers do not yet recognize the problem the product solves, requiring the company to first establish the legitimacy of the product category before selling its specific solution. [Related: Market Education, Positioning, Nascent Market]

**Customer Experience (CX) Function:** Slack's organizational unit that combined quality assurance and customer support into a single team responsible for processing feedback, routing it to product teams, and resolving user issues — treated as a strategic asset rather than a cost center. [Related: Active Listening, Word of Mouth, User Feedback Loop]

**Daily Active Users (DAU):** The number of unique users who engage with a product on a given day, used as a measure of genuine product engagement distinct from total registered accounts or total teams created. Slack had approximately 250,000 DAUs despite having only ~30,000 active teams, because each active team averaged 8–9 members. [Related: Retention, Activation, Magic Number]

**Go-to-Market (GTM) Strategy:** The plan a company executes to bring a product to market, encompassing positioning, pricing, distribution, launch tactics, customer acquisition, and retention — for Slack, built primarily around feedback loops, bottoms-up adoption, and word-of-mouth rather than paid advertising. [Related: Category Creation, Bottoms-Up Adoption, Positioning]

**Leave-State Synchronization:** Slack's proprietary cross-device synchronization feature that tracks exactly where each user left off in every conversation across all devices in real time, eliminating the friction of losing context when switching between laptop and mobile. One of Slack's three core differentiating features. [Related: Focused Differentiation, Product Design, Synchronization]

**Magic Number:** A product-specific activation threshold, determined empirically from cohort retention data, at which a user's or team's probability of long-term retention becomes highly predictable. Slack's magic number is 2,000 messages, after which 93% of teams remain active. [Related: Activation, Retention, Cohort Analysis]

**Market Education:** The go-to-market activity of teaching prospective customers that the product category exists and that they already have the problem the product solves — required when a significant portion of the target market does not recognize the category. Slack found 70–80% of prospects believed they used "nothing" for team communication. [Related: Category Creation, Positioning, Content Marketing]

**Nascent Market:** A product category that is new or undefined, in which customers have not yet established buying criteria, recognized solution providers, or standard evaluation processes. Competing in a nascent market requires educating buyers before persuading them. [Related: Category Creation, Market Education, Positioning]

**Preview Release:** A framing choice for a controlled early-access product release that signals quality and intentionality — as opposed to calling the same release a "beta," which connotes instability and incompleteness. Slack used this framing to maintain credibility while still controlling rollout pace in August 2013. [Related: Beta Testing, Invitation-Based Rollout, Brand Perception]

**Qualitative Data:** Non-numerical user insights gathered through support tickets, social media mentions, interview responses, and open-ended feedback — which Slack treated as equally important to quantitative metrics, systematically tagging and storing all incoming qualitative signals in searchable Slack channels. [Related: User Feedback Loop, Customer Experience, Active Listening]

**Retention:** The rate at which users or teams continue using a product over time after activation. Slack's 93% retention rate among teams crossing the 2,000-message threshold was the business case for investing in activation. [Related: Magic Number, Activation, Churn]

**Team-Level Veto:** The dynamic in collaborative software products where a single team member who refuses to use the product can prevent the entire team from adopting it, making it qualitatively different from individual-user product adoption. [Related: Bottoms-Up Adoption, Onboarding, Consensus Selling]

**Twitter Wall of Love:** Slack's curated collection of enthusiastic, unsolicited public tweets from users expressing extreme satisfaction with the product — used as social proof and a symbol of organic word-of-mouth advocacy. Many of these interactions began as customer service complaints that Slack resolved exceptionally. [Related: Word of Mouth, Customer Experience, Social Proof]

**Word-of-Mouth Growth:** Organic product awareness and adoption driven by users spontaneously recommending or publicly praising a product to their networks, without paid promotion. Slack's primary growth mechanism, enabled by exceptional product quality and customer service. [Related: Twitter Wall of Love, Customer Experience, Social Amplification]
