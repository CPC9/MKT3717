## Overview

This study guide covers all seven major topic areas of MKT3717 Product and Brand Management, spanning the full product development lifecycle from new product identification through brand management. The course integrates both quantitative and qualitative tools, ranging from market sizing and conjoint analysis to brand equity frameworks and project scheduling. The final test is a closed-book, closed-notes examination consisting of multiple-choice, short-answer, and short-essay questions worth 20% of the module grade. Lecture slides take priority over textbook definitions in cases of any discrepancy.

The first half of the course (Lectures 1-6) focuses on the product development process: identifying opportunities through situation analysis (RWW framework, PEST analysis, Porter's Five Forces), generating and evaluating product concepts (concept screening and scoring matrices, conjoint analysis), designing product architecture (modular design for mass customization), testing products in the market (A/B testing, factorial design, statistical interpretation), and managing product development projects (network diagrams, critical path analysis, project crashing). These topics are heavily quantitative and students should expect calculation-based questions on conjoint analysis, critical path identification, slack time computation, and market sizing.

The second half of the course (Lecture 7 onward) transitions to brand management, covering the building blocks of brands, Keller's brand resonance pyramid, and the modern challenges of sustaining brand continuity while remaining relevant. The study guide emphasizes that students are responsible for all materials presented in class, including case and article discussions, and that the listed topics are not exhaustive. Reference chapters from the Crawford textbook ("New Products Management") provide additional context for the product development topics covered in early lectures.

## Key Concepts

### New Product Development (Lecture 1)

#### What is a New Product
- **Definition**: A product that is new to the company or new to the market. New products span a spectrum from new-to-the-world products (entirely novel inventions) to incremental improvements like line extensions, repositioned products, and cost reductions.
- **Context**: Understanding the spectrum of "newness" is important because different types of new products carry different levels of risk, resource requirements, and market potential. The example MCQ illustrates that a scented gel detergent in water-soluble bags from an existing detergent manufacturer is a line extension, not a new-to-the-world product.
- **Relationships**: Connected to product line extensions, new product categories, opportunity identification, and the new products process.

#### Why New Product Development is Difficult
- **Definition**: The inherent challenges in developing new products, including high failure rates, uncertainty in consumer demand, technological complexity, competitive dynamics, and organizational barriers.
- **Context**: Understanding why NPD is difficult motivates the need for structured processes, frameworks, and analytical tools covered throughout the course.
- **Relationships**: Connected to all subsequent topics as motivation for systematic NPD approaches.

### Opportunity Identification (Lecture 2)

#### Situation Analysis
- **Definition**: A comprehensive assessment of the marketplace to identify opportunities for new product development, encompassing both the general environment and the industry-specific competitive environment.
- **Context**: Situation analysis is the starting point of the new product development process and employs multiple frameworks to ensure thorough coverage of external factors.
- **Relationships**: Connected to RWW framework, PEST analysis, Porter's Five Forces, market sizing, and Jobs-to-be-done.

#### RWW Framework (Real-Win-Worth It)
- **Definition**: A screening framework that evaluates new product opportunities by asking three sequential questions: Is the opportunity Real (does a genuine market need exist)? Can we Win (do we have competitive advantage)? Is it Worth it (will the financial return justify the investment)?
- **Context**: Used during opportunity identification to quickly filter ideas before committing significant resources. Each question acts as a gate that must be passed.
- **Relationships**: Connected to situation analysis, feasibility assessment, and go/no-go decision-making.

#### General Environment Analysis (PEST)
- **Definition**: A framework for analyzing macro-environmental factors that affect all industries: Political, Economic, Social, and Technological forces (sometimes extended to PESTEL with Environmental and Legal).
- **Context**: Used as part of situation analysis to understand broad external trends that may create or eliminate product opportunities. Political factors include regulation and trade policy; Economic factors include GDP growth and consumer spending; Social factors include demographic shifts and cultural trends; Technological factors include innovation trajectories and emerging platforms.
- **Relationships**: Connected to situation analysis, industry environment analysis, and opportunity identification.

#### Industry Environment Analysis (Porter's Five Forces)
- **Definition**: Michael Porter's framework for analyzing the competitive intensity and attractiveness of an industry by examining five forces: threat of new entrants, bargaining power of suppliers, bargaining power of buyers, threat of substitute products, and intensity of competitive rivalry.
- **Context**: Used to assess whether an industry is attractive for new product entry and to understand the competitive dynamics that will affect a new product's success.
- **Relationships**: Connected to PEST analysis, competitive strategy, and market attractiveness assessment.

#### Market Sizing
- **Definition**: The process of estimating the total potential revenue or unit volume for a product in a defined market, typically using top-down or bottom-up approaches.
- **Context**: Simple market sizing exercises are testable content. Students should be able to estimate market size using logical assumptions and basic arithmetic.
- **Relationships**: Connected to opportunity identification, feasibility assessment, and business case development.

#### Jobs-to-be-Done (JTBD) Approach
- **Definition**: A framework that focuses on understanding the underlying "job" that customers are trying to accomplish, rather than focusing on product features or demographic segments.
- **Context**: The benefit of the JTBD approach is that it reveals unmet needs that may not be apparent from traditional market research. It shifts the focus from "what features do customers want" to "what problem are customers trying to solve."
- **Relationships**: Connected to customer needs identification, opportunity identification, and concept generation.

#### Customer Voice to Customer Needs Statement
- **Definition**: The process of translating raw customer feedback (verbatim quotes, observations, complaints) into structured needs statements that can guide product specification development.
- **Context**: Part of generating product specifications. Customer voice data must be interpreted and reframed as actionable needs statements before they can be translated into engineering specifications.
- **Relationships**: Connected to product specifications, voice of the customer (VOC), and requirements engineering.

#### Freezing Time Considerations
- **Definition**: The strategic decision about when to "freeze" product specifications and stop making changes, balancing the desire for better information against time-to-market pressures.
- **Context**: Freezing specs too early risks building the wrong product; freezing too late delays launch and increases development costs. Key considerations include market window, competitive timing, and information quality.
- **Relationships**: Connected to product specifications, project management, and time-to-market strategy.

### Concept Generation and Evaluation (Lecture 3)

#### Product Concept
- **Definition**: A clearly written description of a proposed new product that includes what the product is, what benefits it provides, and who the target customer is (Crawford textbook, p. 96-102). A product concept specifies the form, technology, and benefits of the product idea.
- **Context**: Product concepts serve as the input for concept evaluation methods. They must be specific enough to test with consumers but flexible enough to allow design iteration.
- **Relationships**: Connected to concept generation methods, concept testing, and conjoint analysis.

#### Concept Screening Matrix
- **Definition**: A method for quickly narrowing a large set of product concepts to a smaller set of promising candidates by comparing each concept against a reference concept across multiple criteria using ratings of "better than," "same as," or "worse than."
- **Context**: Used as a first-pass filter when there are many concepts to evaluate. It is qualitative and fast, but lacks precision. Pros include speed and simplicity; cons include subjectivity and loss of nuance.
- **Relationships**: Connected to concept scoring matrix, concept selection, and Pugh matrix methodology.

#### Concept Scoring Matrix
- **Definition**: A more detailed and quantitative method for evaluating product concepts that assigns weighted scores to each evaluation criterion and rates each concept on a numerical scale.
- **Context**: Used after concept screening to make finer distinctions among the surviving concepts. Pros include greater precision and transparency; cons include the subjectivity of weight assignments and the potential for false precision.
- **Relationships**: Connected to concept screening matrix, weighted decision-making, and multi-criteria evaluation.

#### Conjoint Analysis
- **Definition**: A statistical technique used to determine how consumers value different attributes (features) of a product by having them rank or rate product profiles that combine different attribute levels.
- **Context**: A heavily testable topic. Students must know how to construct attribute variables, estimate part-worth utilities using a regression approach, and interpret results including identifying the most important attribute and calculating relative importance percentages. The study guide includes a practice problem with a smartphone conjoint analysis (screen size, battery life, brand).
- **Relationships**: Connected to concept testing, regression analysis, consumer preference modeling, and product design.

### Product Design and Architecture (Lecture 4)

#### Modular Design for Mass Customization
- **Definition**: A product architecture approach where a product is designed as a collection of interchangeable modules or components that can be mixed and matched to create different product variants, enabling mass customization while maintaining manufacturing efficiency.
- **Context**: A testable short-answer topic. Key benefits under demand uncertainty include: ability to offer variety without forecasting which specific configurations will sell; reduced inventory risk because common modules can be shared across variants; faster response to changing customer preferences by swapping modules; and lower development costs through component reuse.
- **Relationships**: Connected to product architecture, platform strategy, demand uncertainty, and manufacturing flexibility.

### Market Testing Methods (Lecture 5)

#### Causality in Experimental Approaches
- **Definition**: The ability to establish that a change in one variable (independent variable) actually causes a change in another variable (dependent variable), as opposed to merely being correlated with it.
- **Context**: Building causality is important because business decisions based on correlations alone can be misleading. Experimental methods (A/B testing, factorial design) are designed to establish causal relationships through random assignment and controlled manipulation of variables.
- **Relationships**: Connected to A/B testing, factorial design, statistical significance, and evidence-based decision-making.

#### A/B Testing
- **Definition**: A controlled experiment where two versions of a product, feature, or marketing element (version A and version B) are randomly shown to different groups of users, and their responses are compared to determine which version performs better.
- **Context**: The simplest form of market testing experiment. One independent variable is manipulated with two levels. Widely used in digital product development and marketing.
- **Relationships**: Connected to factorial design, statistical significance, p-value, and confidence intervals.

#### Factorial Design
- **Definition**: An experimental design that simultaneously tests two or more independent variables (factors) at multiple levels, allowing researchers to assess both the main effects of each factor and the interaction effects between factors.
- **Context**: More complex and informative than A/B testing. A 2x2 factorial design tests two factors each at two levels, resulting in four experimental conditions. Factorial designs are efficient because they provide information about interactions that would require multiple separate A/B tests to detect.
- **Relationships**: Connected to A/B testing, interaction effects, experimental design, and regression analysis.

#### Confidence Interval
- **Definition**: A range of values that, with a specified probability (typically 95%), contains the true population parameter being estimated. Wider intervals indicate greater uncertainty.
- **Context**: Used to interpret the results of market testing experiments. If a confidence interval for the difference between two conditions does not include zero, the difference is statistically significant.
- **Relationships**: Connected to p-value, statistical significance, regression analysis, and hypothesis testing.

#### P-Value
- **Definition**: The probability of observing results as extreme as (or more extreme than) the observed results, assuming the null hypothesis (no real effect) is true. A small p-value (typically < 0.05) suggests the observed effect is unlikely to be due to chance.
- **Context**: Used to determine statistical significance in market testing experiments. Students need to interpret p-values in the context of regression output and experimental results.
- **Relationships**: Connected to confidence interval, statistical significance, hypothesis testing, and regression analysis.

### Project Management (Lecture 6)

#### Characteristics of a Project
- **Definition**: A project is a temporary endeavor with a defined beginning and end, specific objectives, and constraints on resources, time, and scope. Projects are distinct from ongoing operations.
- **Context**: Understanding what makes something a project is foundational to applying project management tools and techniques.
- **Relationships**: Connected to network diagrams, critical path method, work breakdown structure, and project scheduling.

#### Work Breakdown Structure (WBS)
- **Definition**: A hierarchical decomposition of the total scope of work in a project into smaller, manageable components called work packages or activities.
- **Context**: The WBS is the first step in building a network diagram. Each activity in the WBS has an estimated duration and defined dependencies (predecessor activities).
- **Relationships**: Connected to network diagram, critical path, activity sequencing, and project scope management.

#### Network Diagram
- **Definition**: A graphical representation of the sequence and dependencies of activities in a project, showing which activities must be completed before others can begin.
- **Context**: Used to identify the critical path, calculate slack times, and determine the overall project duration. Students should be able to construct network diagrams from WBS data and practice problem sets.
- **Relationships**: Connected to critical path, slack time, project crashing, and PERT/CPM methods.

#### Critical Path
- **Definition**: The longest sequence of dependent activities from project start to finish, which determines the minimum project duration. Any delay in a critical path activity will delay the entire project.
- **Context**: A heavily testable concept. The example MCQ confirms that the critical path is "the sequence of activities between a project's start and finish that takes the longest time to complete." Students must be able to identify the critical path from a network diagram.
- **Relationships**: Connected to slack time, project crashing, network diagram, and project duration.

#### Slack Time (Float)
- **Definition**: The amount of time an activity can be delayed without delaying the overall project completion date. Activities on the critical path have zero slack.
- **Context**: Students must be able to calculate slack time for each activity in a network. Slack = Latest Start - Earliest Start = Latest Finish - Earliest Finish.
- **Relationships**: Connected to critical path, network diagram, forward/backward pass, and project scheduling.

#### Project Crashing
- **Definition**: The process of reducing the duration of critical path activities by adding resources (at additional cost), in order to shorten the overall project completion time.
- **Context**: Students must be able to decide which activities to crash based on cost-per-time-unit savings. Only critical path activities should be crashed. The activity with the lowest crash cost per unit of time saved should be crashed first.
- **Relationships**: Connected to critical path, slack time, cost-time tradeoff, and resource allocation.

### Brand Management (Lecture 7)

#### Building Blocks of Brand
- **Definition**: The fundamental elements that constitute a brand, including brand name, logo, symbols, packaging, associations, perceived quality, brand awareness, and brand loyalty.
- **Context**: Understanding brand building blocks provides the vocabulary and conceptual foundation for all brand management activities, including brand equity measurement and brand strategy.
- **Relationships**: Connected to Keller's brand resonance pyramid, brand equity, and brand identity.

#### Keller's Brand Resonance Pyramid
- **Definition**: A framework by Kevin Lane Keller that describes brand building as a sequential process with four levels: (1) Brand Identity (who are you?), (2) Brand Meaning (what are you?), (3) Brand Response (what about you?), and (4) Brand Resonance (what about you and me?). The pyramid has six building blocks: salience, performance, imagery, judgments, feelings, and resonance.
- **Context**: The most important brand management framework in the course. It describes how brands move from basic awareness to deep loyalty and engagement. Each level must be established before the next can be achieved effectively.
- **Relationships**: Connected to brand equity, brand building blocks, brand loyalty, and brand management challenges.

#### Sustaining Continuity vs. Staying Relevant
- **Definition**: The fundamental strategic tension in modern brand management between maintaining a consistent brand identity over time (continuity) and adapting the brand to remain appealing to evolving consumer tastes and market conditions (relevance).
- **Context**: Identified as a key modern brand management challenge. Brands that overemphasize continuity risk becoming irrelevant; brands that overemphasize relevance risk losing their core identity. This tension is particularly acute for heritage and luxury brands.
- **Relationships**: Connected to brand repositioning, brand equity, brand sustainability (Lecture 8), and the new luxury paradigm.

## Frameworks & Mental Models

### RWW Framework (Real-Win-Worth It)

- **Visual Description**: A three-gate sequential filter. Each gate poses a question: Gate 1 asks "Is it Real?" (Is there a genuine market need and can the product be made?). Gate 2 asks "Can we Win?" (Do we have a competitive advantage?). Gate 3 asks "Is it Worth it?" (Does the financial return justify the investment?). An opportunity must pass all three gates to proceed.
- **How to Apply**: For each new product opportunity, systematically answer all three questions with evidence. Start with "Real" -- validate market need and technical feasibility. Then "Win" -- assess competitive position and internal capabilities. Finally "Worth it" -- build a business case with financial projections. If any answer is "no," stop or pivot.
- **When to Use**: Use at the opportunity identification stage to screen ideas before investing significant resources. Also useful as a periodic checkpoint throughout development to ensure the project still makes sense.

### PEST Analysis (Political-Economic-Social-Technological)

- **Visual Description**: A four-quadrant matrix. Each quadrant represents one macro-environmental dimension. Political factors include government policy, regulation, trade agreements, and political stability. Economic factors include GDP growth, inflation, interest rates, and consumer spending power. Social factors include demographics, cultural trends, lifestyle changes, and consumer attitudes. Technological factors include innovation, R&D activity, automation, and digital transformation.
- **How to Apply**: For each quadrant, list current and emerging trends. Assess whether each trend creates an opportunity or threat for the proposed product. Prioritize factors by impact and likelihood.
- **When to Use**: Use at the beginning of opportunity identification to understand the macro-environmental context. Complements Porter's Five Forces, which focuses on industry-specific dynamics.

### Porter's Five Forces Model

- **Visual Description**: A diagram with five boxes arranged around a central "Competitive Rivalry" box. The four surrounding forces point inward: Threat of New Entrants (top), Bargaining Power of Suppliers (left), Bargaining Power of Buyers (right), and Threat of Substitutes (bottom). Stronger forces reduce industry attractiveness and profit potential.
- **How to Apply**: Rate each force as high, medium, or low for the target industry. High threat of new entrants or substitutes, and high bargaining power of buyers or suppliers, reduce industry attractiveness. Look for industries where multiple forces are weak, indicating higher profit potential for new products.
- **When to Use**: Use during industry environment analysis to assess whether an industry is attractive for entry with a new product.

### Concept Screening and Scoring Matrices

- **Visual Description**: The screening matrix is a simple grid with concepts as columns and criteria as rows. Each cell contains +, 0, or - (better, same, worse than reference). Net scores are summed. The scoring matrix replaces +/0/- with numerical ratings (e.g., 1-5) and adds criterion weights. The weighted sum produces a total score for each concept.
- **How to Apply**: For screening, compare each concept against a reference concept on all criteria and count net positives. For scoring, assign weights to criteria reflecting their importance, rate each concept on each criterion, multiply ratings by weights, and sum for total scores. Rank concepts by total score.
- **When to Use**: Use screening when you have many concepts and need a quick initial filter. Use scoring for more careful evaluation of the surviving concepts, when you need to justify and document the selection decision.

### Conjoint Analysis (Regression Approach)

- **Visual Description**: A table of product profiles (combinations of attribute levels) with consumer preference rankings. Attributes are coded as binary dummy variables (0/1) for regression. The regression coefficients (part-worth utilities) indicate how much each attribute level contributes to consumer preference. The range of coefficients within each attribute indicates that attribute's relative importance.
- **How to Apply**: (1) Code attribute levels as dummy variables. (2) Run regression with rank as the dependent variable and dummy variables as independent variables. (3) Interpret coefficients -- the sign and magnitude indicate direction and strength of preference. (4) Calculate relative importance for each attribute as (max part-worth - min part-worth) / sum of all attribute ranges x 100%.
- **When to Use**: Use during concept testing to understand how consumers trade off between product attributes. Useful for product design decisions, pricing strategy, and market segmentation.

### Network Diagram and Critical Path Method

- **Visual Description**: A directed acyclic graph where nodes represent activities (or milestones) and arrows represent dependencies. Each activity has an estimated duration. The critical path is the longest path through the network, highlighted or marked differently. Forward pass calculates earliest start/finish times; backward pass calculates latest start/finish times. Slack = Latest Start - Earliest Start.
- **How to Apply**: (1) List all activities, durations, and predecessors from the WBS. (2) Draw the network diagram. (3) Perform forward pass to find earliest start and finish for each activity. (4) Perform backward pass to find latest start and finish. (5) Calculate slack for each activity. (6) Identify the critical path (zero slack activities). (7) For crashing, identify the critical path activity with the lowest cost per unit of time reduction.
- **When to Use**: Use during project planning to schedule activities, identify the critical path, determine the minimum project duration, and make cost-time tradeoff decisions for project crashing.

### Keller's Brand Resonance Pyramid

- **Visual Description**: A four-level pyramid. The base (Level 1) is Identity (brand salience -- deep, broad brand awareness). Level 2 is Meaning, split into Performance (left side: product reliability, durability, serviceability, style, price) and Imagery (right side: user profiles, purchase/usage situations, personality, values, heritage). Level 3 is Response, split into Judgments (left: quality, credibility, consideration, superiority) and Feelings (right: warmth, fun, excitement, security, social approval, self-respect). The apex (Level 4) is Resonance (behavioral loyalty, attitudinal attachment, sense of community, active engagement).
- **How to Apply**: Assess a brand at each level of the pyramid. Start from the base: Is the brand salient (do consumers know it)? Then meaning: What do consumers think the brand stands for? Then response: How do consumers evaluate and feel about it? Finally resonance: Do consumers have deep loyalty and active engagement? Weaknesses at lower levels undermine higher levels.
- **When to Use**: Use for brand audit, brand strategy development, and diagnosing brand equity problems. Also useful for comparing the brand-building progress of competitors.

## Formulas & Quantitative Tools

### Conjoint Analysis: Relative Importance Calculation

- **Formula**: `Relative Importance of Attribute i (%) = (Range of Attribute i / Sum of All Attribute Ranges) x 100`
- **Variables**:

| Variable | Description |
|---|---|
| Range of Attribute i | The difference between the highest and lowest part-worth utility values within attribute i |
| Sum of All Attribute Ranges | The total of all individual attribute ranges across all attributes |

- **Interpretation**: The percentage indicates how much influence each attribute has on the consumer's overall preference relative to other attributes. Higher percentages mean the attribute matters more to the consumer's decision.
- **Example**: From the study guide practice problem -- for a smartphone with attributes Screen Size (small/wide), Battery Life (10hr/16hr), and Brand (iPhone/Samsung), compute the average rank for each level of each attribute. The attribute whose levels produce the largest average rank difference is the most important. To compute relative importance, divide each attribute's range by the sum of all ranges and multiply by 100%.
- **Common Mistakes**: Confusing rank order (lower rank = higher preference in many conjoint setups) with rating scales; forgetting that regression coefficients in conjoint represent half the range when using -1/+1 coding vs. the full range with 0/1 coding; miscalculating relative importance by using raw coefficients instead of ranges.

### Slack Time Calculation

- **Formula**: `Slack = LS - ES = LF - EF`
- **Variables**:

| Variable | Description |
|---|---|
| LS | Latest Start time for the activity |
| ES | Earliest Start time for the activity |
| LF | Latest Finish time for the activity |
| EF | Earliest Finish time for the activity |

- **Interpretation**: Slack represents the maximum time an activity can be delayed without delaying the project. Activities with zero slack are on the critical path.
- **Example**: If an activity has ES = 5, EF = 8, LS = 7, LF = 10, then Slack = 7 - 5 = 2 days. This activity can be delayed by up to 2 days without impacting the project.
- **Common Mistakes**: Confusing forward pass (ES/EF) with backward pass (LS/LF); using wrong predecessor relationships when computing earliest start times; forgetting that ES for an activity is the maximum EF of all its predecessors (not the minimum).

### Project Crashing Decision

- **Formula**: `Crash Cost per Unit Time = (Crash Cost - Normal Cost) / (Normal Duration - Crash Duration)`
- **Variables**:

| Variable | Description |
|---|---|
| Crash Cost | The total cost of completing the activity at its shortest (crashed) duration |
| Normal Cost | The total cost of completing the activity at its normal duration |
| Normal Duration | The regular time to complete the activity |
| Crash Duration | The shortest possible time to complete the activity with additional resources |

- **Interpretation**: Lower crash cost per unit time means the activity is cheaper to accelerate. Always crash the critical path activity with the lowest crash cost per unit time first. Only activities on the critical path are worth crashing.
- **Example**: Activity A has Normal Duration = 10 days, Crash Duration = 7 days, Normal Cost = $5,000, Crash Cost = $8,000. Crash cost per day = ($8,000 - $5,000) / (10 - 7) = $1,000/day.
- **Common Mistakes**: Crashing non-critical-path activities (which does not shorten the project); failing to re-identify the critical path after crashing (a new critical path may emerge); crashing beyond the point where a parallel path becomes critical.

### Regression Interpretation (for Conjoint and Market Testing)

- **Formula**: `Y = b0 + b1*X1 + b2*X2 + ... + bn*Xn`
- **Variables**:

| Variable | Description |
|---|---|
| Y | Dependent variable (e.g., consumer preference rank, purchase intent) |
| b0 | Intercept (baseline value when all independent variables are 0) |
| b1...bn | Regression coefficients (part-worth utilities in conjoint; treatment effects in experiments) |
| X1...Xn | Independent variables (attribute dummy variables in conjoint; experimental factors in testing) |

- **Interpretation**: Each coefficient represents the change in Y associated with a one-unit change in the corresponding X, holding other variables constant. In conjoint analysis, coefficients indicate the utility contribution of each attribute level. Statistical significance is assessed via p-values and confidence intervals.
- **Example**: In a conjoint study, if b_ScreenSize(wide) = -1.5 (with rank where lower = more preferred), this means switching from small to wide screen size decreases the rank by 1.5 (i.e., makes it more preferred).
- **Common Mistakes**: Interpreting coefficients without considering the direction of the preference scale; ignoring statistical significance; confusing correlation with causation in non-experimental settings.

## Case Studies

### Ace Corporation Line Extension (from Example MCQ)

- **Company**: Ace Corporation (illustrative example from study guide)
- **Challenge**: Ace Corporation, a manufacturer of powder and liquid dishwashing detergents, wanted to expand its product offering in response to popular demand.
- **What Was Done**: The company introduced a single-load, scented, gel detergent encased in water-soluble bags designed to fit dishwasher detergent trays.
- **Takeaway**: This is a product line extension, not a new-to-the-world product. Line extensions are additions to existing product lines designed to flesh out what the firm offers to its current markets. Understanding the distinction between types of new products (new-to-the-world, line extension, repositioned product, cost reduction) is critical for the test.

### Smartphone Conjoint Analysis (from Example Short Answer)

- **Company**: Illustrative exercise (smartphone market)
- **Challenge**: Determine which product attribute (screen size, battery life, or brand) is most important to a consumer and calculate the relative importance of each attribute.
- **What Was Done**: A consumer ranked eight smartphone profiles (combinations of small/wide screen, 10hr/16hr battery, iPhone/Samsung brand) from 1-8 (lower rank = higher preference). Students must compute average rankings for each attribute level and determine which attribute has the largest average rank difference.
- **Takeaway**: The conjoint analysis regression approach connects average ranking differences to regression coefficients. The attribute with the largest range of part-worth utilities is the most important. Students must be comfortable with both the calculation mechanics and the interpretation of relative importance percentages that sum to 100%.

## Key Takeaways

- **Critical path analysis** is the most heavily tested quantitative topic; students must identify critical paths, calculate slack times, and make project crashing decisions from network diagrams and practice problems.
- **Conjoint analysis** is the second most heavily tested quantitative topic; students must construct variables, estimate part-worth utilities via regression, identify the most important attribute, and calculate relative importance percentages.
- **The RWW framework** (Real-Win-Worth It) is the primary screening tool for evaluating new product opportunities and appears in the opportunity identification section of the course.
- **Keller's brand resonance pyramid** is the foundational framework for brand management, describing the sequential progression from brand identity through meaning and response to resonance.
- **Modular design** under demand uncertainty is a testable short-answer topic; key benefits include product variety, inventory risk reduction, faster response to demand changes, and component reuse.
- **Statistical interpretation** (confidence intervals, p-values, regression output) is required for understanding both conjoint analysis results and market testing experimental outcomes.
- **Product concept definition** (form, technology, benefits, target) and the distinction between concept screening and scoring matrices are testable conceptual topics.
- **PEST and Porter's Five Forces** are complementary situation analysis frameworks -- PEST covers macro-environmental factors while Five Forces covers industry-specific competitive dynamics.
- **Types of new products** (new-to-the-world, line extension, repositioned product, cost reduction) must be distinguished clearly, as illustrated by the Ace Corporation MCQ example.
- **Brand continuity vs. relevance** is the key modern challenge in brand management, requiring brands to maintain identity consistency while adapting to evolving consumer expectations.

## Key Terms and Definitions Glossary

**A/B Testing**: A controlled experiment comparing two versions (A and B) of a single variable, with random assignment of subjects, to determine which version produces a better outcome. [Related: Factorial Design, Causality, Statistical Significance]

**Brand Equity**: The added value that a brand name gives to a product beyond its functional benefits, derived from consumer awareness, associations, perceived quality, and loyalty. [Related: Keller's Brand Resonance Pyramid, Brand Building Blocks, Brand Awareness]

**Brand Identity**: The first level of Keller's brand resonance pyramid, focused on creating deep and broad brand awareness (salience) so that consumers know who you are. [Related: Brand Salience, Brand Resonance Pyramid, Brand Awareness]

**Brand Mantra**: A short three- to five-word phrase that captures the irrefutable essence or spirit of a brand's positioning, structured as emotional modifier + descriptive modifier + brand function. [Related: Brand Positioning, Brand Identity, Points-of-Difference]

**Brand Meaning**: The second level of Keller's brand resonance pyramid, encompassing what the brand stands for through its Performance associations (functional) and Imagery associations (abstract). [Related: Brand Performance, Brand Imagery, Brand Resonance Pyramid]

**Brand Resonance**: The fourth and highest level of Keller's pyramid, describing the ultimate relationship and level of identification a consumer has with a brand, characterized by behavioral loyalty, attitudinal attachment, sense of community, and active engagement. [Related: Brand Loyalty, Brand Resonance Pyramid, Customer Engagement]

**Brand Response**: The third level of Keller's brand resonance pyramid, encompassing what consumers think (Judgments) and feel (Feelings) about the brand. [Related: Brand Judgments, Brand Feelings, Brand Resonance Pyramid]

**Causality**: The relationship where a change in one variable (independent) directly produces a change in another variable (dependent), as opposed to mere correlation; established through experimental methods with random assignment and controlled manipulation. [Related: A/B Testing, Factorial Design, Experimental Design]

**Concept Scoring Matrix**: A quantitative concept evaluation method that uses weighted criteria and numerical ratings to produce a total score for each concept, enabling finer distinctions than concept screening. [Related: Concept Screening Matrix, Weighted Decision-Making, Concept Evaluation]

**Concept Screening Matrix**: A qualitative concept evaluation method that compares each concept to a reference concept on multiple criteria using +/0/- ratings to quickly narrow down a large set of concepts. [Related: Concept Scoring Matrix, Pugh Matrix, Concept Selection]

**Confidence Interval**: A range of values within which the true population parameter falls with a specified level of probability (typically 95%); used to assess the precision and significance of experimental results. [Related: P-Value, Statistical Significance, Regression Analysis]

**Conjoint Analysis**: A multivariate statistical technique that determines how consumers value different product attributes by analyzing their preferences across product profiles with varying attribute combinations. [Related: Part-Worth Utility, Regression Analysis, Concept Testing]

**Cost Reduction (Product Type)**: A type of new product that provides similar performance to existing products but at a lower cost, achieved through redesign, materials substitution, or manufacturing process improvements. [Related: New Product Types, Line Extension, Product Development]

**Critical Path**: The longest sequence of dependent activities in a project network that determines the minimum project duration; any delay to a critical path activity delays the entire project. [Related: Slack Time, Network Diagram, Project Crashing]

**Customer Needs Statement**: A structured expression of what customers require from a product, translated from raw customer voice data into a format that can guide product specification development. [Related: Voice of the Customer, Product Specifications, Jobs-to-be-Done]

**Factorial Design**: An experimental design that tests two or more factors simultaneously at multiple levels, allowing assessment of both main effects and interaction effects. [Related: A/B Testing, Interaction Effects, Experimental Design]

**Freezing Time**: The point in the product development process at which product specifications are locked and no further changes are accepted; requires balancing information quality against time-to-market pressure. [Related: Product Specifications, Time-to-Market, Development Process]

**Jobs-to-be-Done (JTBD)**: A framework that defines market opportunities based on the underlying task or job that customers are trying to accomplish, rather than focusing on product features or customer demographics. [Related: Customer Needs, Opportunity Identification, Market Segmentation]

**Keller's Brand Resonance Pyramid**: A four-level brand-building framework progressing from Identity (salience) through Meaning (performance and imagery) and Response (judgments and feelings) to Resonance (loyalty, attachment, community, engagement). [Related: Brand Equity, Brand Building Blocks, Brand Management]

**Line Extension**: An addition to an existing product line offered to the firm's current markets, designed to provide additional variety, fill gaps, or preempt competitors within an established category. [Related: New Product Types, Brand Extension, Product Line]

**Market Sizing**: The process of estimating the total potential demand (in revenue or units) for a product within a defined market, using top-down, bottom-up, or analogous estimation approaches. [Related: Opportunity Identification, TAM/SAM/SOM, Business Case]

**Modular Design**: A product architecture approach where a product is composed of independent, interchangeable modules that can be mixed and matched to create different product variants, enabling mass customization with manufacturing efficiency. [Related: Mass Customization, Product Architecture, Platform Strategy]

**Network Diagram**: A graphical representation of project activities and their dependencies, used to identify the critical path, calculate slack times, and determine minimum project duration. [Related: Critical Path, Work Breakdown Structure, PERT/CPM]

**New-to-the-World Product**: A product that creates an entirely new category or market; the highest-risk, highest-potential type of new product, representing true innovation. [Related: New Product Types, Line Extension, Disruptive Innovation]

**P-Value**: The probability of observing results as extreme as the observed data assuming no true effect exists; values below 0.05 typically indicate statistical significance and rejection of the null hypothesis. [Related: Confidence Interval, Statistical Significance, Hypothesis Testing]

**Part-Worth Utility**: The numerical value assigned to a specific level of a product attribute in conjoint analysis, representing the contribution of that attribute level to overall consumer preference. [Related: Conjoint Analysis, Regression Coefficients, Relative Importance]

**PEST Analysis**: A macro-environmental analysis framework examining Political, Economic, Social, and Technological factors that affect all industries and may create or eliminate product opportunities. [Related: Situation Analysis, Porter's Five Forces, General Environment Analysis]

**Porter's Five Forces**: An industry analysis framework assessing competitive intensity through five forces: threat of new entrants, bargaining power of suppliers, bargaining power of buyers, threat of substitutes, and competitive rivalry. [Related: PEST Analysis, Industry Attractiveness, Competitive Strategy]

**Product Concept**: A written description of a proposed new product specifying its form, the technology it employs, and the benefits it delivers to a target customer. [Related: Concept Generation, Concept Testing, Conjoint Analysis]

**Product Specifications**: The detailed technical and functional requirements that define what a product must do and how it must perform, translated from customer needs statements. [Related: Customer Needs Statement, Freezing Time, Engineering Requirements]

**Project Crashing**: The practice of reducing a critical path activity's duration by adding resources at additional cost, applied to the activity with the lowest crash cost per unit of time saved. [Related: Critical Path, Slack Time, Cost-Time Tradeoff]

**Regression Analysis**: A statistical method that models the relationship between a dependent variable and one or more independent variables; used in this course for conjoint analysis estimation and interpretation of experimental results. [Related: Conjoint Analysis, P-Value, Confidence Interval]

**Relative Importance (Conjoint)**: The percentage measure of how much influence each product attribute has on consumer preference, calculated as the range of part-worth utilities for that attribute divided by the sum of all attribute ranges. [Related: Conjoint Analysis, Part-Worth Utility, Attribute Importance]

**Repositioned Product**: An existing product that is targeted at a new market segment or positioned differently in consumers' minds, without fundamental changes to the product itself. [Related: New Product Types, Brand Repositioning, Market Segmentation]

**RWW Framework**: A three-question screening tool for evaluating new product opportunities: Is it Real? Can we Win? Is it Worth it? Each question must be affirmatively answered to proceed with development. [Related: Opportunity Identification, Feasibility Assessment, Go/No-Go Decision]

**Slack Time (Float)**: The amount of time an activity can be delayed without delaying overall project completion, calculated as Latest Start minus Earliest Start; critical path activities have zero slack. [Related: Critical Path, Network Diagram, Project Scheduling]

**Situation Analysis**: A comprehensive assessment of the external environment (general and industry-specific) to identify opportunities and threats for new product development. [Related: PEST Analysis, Porter's Five Forces, RWW Framework]

**Statistical Significance**: A result that is unlikely to have occurred by chance alone, typically indicated by a p-value below 0.05 or a confidence interval that does not include zero. [Related: P-Value, Confidence Interval, Hypothesis Testing]

**Work Breakdown Structure (WBS)**: A hierarchical decomposition of total project scope into smaller, manageable activities or work packages, each with estimated durations and defined dependencies. [Related: Network Diagram, Critical Path, Project Planning]
