## Overview

These notes capture the in-class discussions from the first four sessions of MKT 3717 (January 15 – January 29, 2026), covering the foundational concepts of product and brand management, the new product development (NPD) process, and customer need analysis. The sessions progressively built a working vocabulary and analytical toolkit, beginning with how products and brands are defined and distinguished, and advancing toward the practical mechanics of opportunity identification, concept generation, and translating customer observations into actionable product specifications.

A central theme across all four classes is the importance of understanding customers at a level deeper than surface-level wants. The course introduced latent needs, the Jobs-to-Be-Done (JTBD) framework, and structured customer research methods as tools for surfacing insights that customers themselves may not be able to articulate. These insights are treated not as marketing inputs alone, but as the raw material from which great products — and therefore great brands — are built. The reminder that "companies make products and consumers make brands" underscores that the brand is ultimately a customer perception, and poor product quality (as illustrated by Fyre Festival) cannot be rescued by branding alone.

The environmental analysis frameworks introduced in Class 2 — PESTEL and Porter's Five Forces — provide the macro and competitive lenses through which opportunity spaces are evaluated before resources are committed. Together with the Opportunity Tournament Filtering Model and the Real-Win-Worth It (RWW) framework, these tools form a disciplined, risk-aware approach to deciding which opportunities are worth pursuing. Class 3 then shifted inward, detailing how customer interviews, ethnography, and observation feed directly into needs statements and product specifications that guide development teams with clarity and measurability.

## Key Concepts

### Product and Brand Fundamentals

#### Product
**Definition:** Anything that can be offered to a market for attention, acquisition, use, or consumption — satisfying a want or need. Products may be physical goods, services, or any other market offering.

**Context:** Introduced in Class 1 as the foundational unit of analysis. The definition is deliberately broad so that services, digital offerings, and experiences all fall within scope.

**Relationships:** A product is the prerequisite for a brand. Without a quality underlying product, brand-building efforts fail (see Fyre Festival example). Related to the Three Innovation Challenge — a product must be desirable (people), viable (business), and feasible (technical).

#### Brand
**Definition:** More than a product — a brand includes dimensions that differentiate it from competing products by shaping customer perceptions, expectations, and experiences. Branding is the act of "giving a soul" to a product.

**Context:** Introduced alongside product in Class 1. The phrase "Companies make products and consumers make brands" reflects that brand equity is co-created; firms can only manage inputs (positioning, messaging, quality) while customers determine the brand's meaning.

**Relationships:** Directly related to de-commoditization — branding removes a product from pure price competition. Depends on the product being genuinely good; branding cannot substitute for product quality.

#### De-Commoditization
**Definition:** The process by which branding differentiates an otherwise interchangeable product from competitors, reducing price sensitivity and building customer preference.

**Context:** Mentioned in Class 1 as a core purpose of branding. Without differentiation, products compete only on price, eroding margins.

**Relationships:** Achieved through consistent brand-building. Related to Porter's Five Forces (intensity of rivalry decreases as differentiation increases) and to the concept of latent needs (addressing unmet needs is a primary route to differentiation).

### New Product Development Process

#### Product Development Process (NPD)
**Definition:** The sequence of activities an organization undertakes to conceive, design, and commercialize a product, transforming a set of inputs into market-ready outputs.

**Context:** Introduced in Class 1 and elaborated in Class 2. The process is structured and sequential, not ad hoc, reflecting that innovation at scale requires repeatable systems.

**Relationships:** Encompasses opportunity identification/selection, concept generation, concept/project evaluation, development, and launch. Each stage connects to risk-mitigation strategies.

#### Three Innovation Challenge
**Definition:** A framework asserting that successful products must satisfy three simultaneous requirements: desirability (people need/want it), viability (the business model works), and feasibility (the technology or capability exists to build it).

**Context:** Introduced in Class 1 and reinforced throughout. The three dimensions map directly onto the Real-Win-Worth It (RWW) framework used in opportunity evaluation.

**Relationships:** People/desirability aligns with "Is the opportunity real?"; Business/viability aligns with "Is it worth it?"; Technical/feasibility aligns with "Can we win it?". Failures on any single dimension can doom an otherwise promising product.

#### Opportunity Tournament Filtering Model
**Definition:** A risk mitigation strategy in which a large pool of raw initial opportunities is passed through successive filters, progressively narrowing the field until only a small number of exceptional, well-validated opportunities remain.

**Context:** Introduced in Class 2 as a structured approach to managing innovation portfolios under uncertainty. The model prevents premature commitment of resources to low-quality ideas.

**Relationships:** Draws on two verified risk-mitigation strategies: (1) diversification — maintaining a broad initial portfolio — and (2) delaying investment until more information is known. Related to Real-Win-Worth It analysis, which provides criteria for each filter.

#### Real-Win-Worth It (RWW) Analysis
**Definition:** An opportunity evaluation framework with three sequential questions — Is the opportunity real? Can we win this opportunity? Is this opportunity worth it? — that maps directly to the Three Innovation Challenge dimensions.

**Context:** Introduced in Class 2 as a practical decision tool for concept/project evaluation within the NPD process.

**Relationships:** "Real?" tests desirability and market existence. "Win?" tests competitive feasibility. "Worth it?" tests business viability. Connects to PESTEL and Porter's Five Forces for environmental evidence.

### Customer Need Analysis

#### Types of Needs
**Definition:** Customer needs fall into three categories: (1) Explicit needs — easily expressed by customers and likely already fulfilled; (2) Unfulfilled needs — easily expressed but not yet generally satisfied; (3) Latent needs — needs customers may not be aware of or cannot yet articulate.

**Context:** Introduced in Class 2. Latent needs are the highest-value target for product innovation because addressing them can delight customers in entirely new ways and exceed expectations.

**Relationships:** Directly informs which research methods are most appropriate — explicit needs can be captured via surveys; latent needs require ethnography, observation, and the Jobs-to-Be-Done lens.

#### Jobs-to-Be-Done (JTBD) Approach
**Definition:** A customer insight framework based on the premise that people do not buy products; they "hire" them to accomplish a job in their lives. Product managers frame jobs as: "When [situation], I want to [motivation], so I can [desired outcome]."

**Context:** Introduced in Class 3 as a superior lens for understanding true customer motivation rather than surface-level preferences. Directs marketers and PMs to market the "why" rather than the "what."

**Relationships:** Particularly powerful for surfacing latent needs. Connects to needs statement writing — the JTBD job statement becomes the foundation for a crisp, actionable needs statement.

#### Needs Statements
**Definition:** Clear, concise written descriptions of a customer need, following strict conventions: written in "what" not "how," specific, positive (not negative), an attribute of the product (not a user action), using "can" rather than "must/should."

**Context:** Introduced in Class 3 as the output of customer research that guides the development team. A poorly written needs statement introduces ambiguity that propagates through the entire development process.

**Relationships:** Derived from JTBD job statements and customer interviews/observation. Feeds directly into product specifications, which quantify the need into measurable metrics.

#### Product Specifications
**Definition:** Quantified translations of customer needs into measurable product requirements. Each specification requires three elements: a metric (what is being measured), a value (the target), and units (the scale of measurement).

**Context:** Introduced in Class 3 as the bridge between qualitative customer insight and engineering/design targets. An importance column is added to the specification list to prioritize which metrics matter most.

**Relationships:** Downstream of needs statements and customer research. The importance ranking reflects trade-off decisions that tie back to the RWW analysis and strategic priorities.

## Frameworks & Mental Models

### PESTEL Analysis
Used to analyze the general (macro) environment surrounding an organization. The six dimensions are:
- **Political** — government policy, regulation, political stability
- **Economic** — economic growth, inflation, exchange rates
- **Social** — demographic trends, cultural attitudes, lifestyle changes
- **Technological** — innovation rates, automation, R&D activity
- **Environmental** — ecological impact, sustainability expectations, climate factors
- **Legal** — employment law, consumer protection, industry regulation

PESTEL sits in the outermost ring of the environmental analysis model (general environment), outside the operating environment analyzed by Porter's Five Forces.

### Porter's Five Forces
Used to analyze the operating (competitive) environment. The five forces are:
1. Threat of new entrants
2. Bargaining power of buyers
3. Bargaining power of suppliers
4. Threat of substitute products (from other industries)
5. Intensity of rivalry among existing competitors

Together with PESTEL, Porter's Five Forces helps teams evaluate whether an identified opportunity exists in an attractive competitive environment before committing to development.

### The Three-Ring Environmental Model
A layered model of organizational context: the organization sits at the center, surrounded by the operating environment (analyzed via Porter's Five Forces), which is in turn surrounded by the general environment (analyzed via PESTEL). Marketing mix decisions flow from the inside out, but must be informed by conditions in all three rings.

### Opportunity Sourcing Heuristics
Several practical heuristics for generating opportunities were discussed in Class 2:
- Follow personal passion
- Leverage core competencies (existing capabilities and resources)
- Study customers and list unmet needs
- Imitate and improve on existing solutions
- Import geographically isolated innovations into new markets

The largest sources of ideas empirically are internally generated opportunities (~46%) and customer-driven opportunities (~23%) — together the two largest categories; they are separately counted in the survey data, not a literal Venn intersection.

### Push vs. Pull Opportunity Generation
- **Push:** Technology or capability that already exists is pushed toward a market application (supply-driven).
- **Pull:** An identified customer need pulls the organization toward developing a solution (demand-driven).

Both are valid entry points into the NPD process. The distinction matters for how teams frame the opportunity and which research methods are prioritized.

## Formulas & Quantitative Tools

No explicit mathematical formulas were introduced in Classes 1-4. However, the following quantitative tool structure was discussed:

**Product Specification Table Structure:**

| Need | Metric | Importance | Units | Value/Target |
|------|--------|------------|-------|--------------|
| [Customer need] | [What is measured] | [High/Med/Low or numeric weight] | [Scale] | [Target figure] |

The importance column is added after creating the initial list of metrics. This prioritization step is implicitly quantitative — teams may use numeric weights or ordinal rankings — but the specific scoring methodology was not prescribed in these class sessions.

Note: Quantitative tools such as conjoint analysis and survey-based measurement of subjective product characteristics were referenced as possible methods (Class 3 mentioned that surveys can yield quantitative data on subjective characteristics), but formulas were not detailed in these sessions. See lecture notes for Lectures 5-6 for conjoint analysis specifics.

## Case Studies

### Fyre Festival (Class 1)
Fyre Festival was cited as the cautionary example of branding without a product. The festival was marketed with high-production influencer campaigns promising a luxury music experience; the actual event was disastrously under-delivered, with inadequate food, shelter, and infrastructure. The case illustrates that branding is de-commoditizing only when the underlying product genuinely delivers value — branding a bad product accelerates its failure by raising expectations that cannot be met. This example anchored the course's foundational principle: you need a great product to make a great brand.

### AI in Product Design (Class 2)
The class discussed artificial intelligence as a tool in the product design process. The key insight was not that AI replaces human judgment, but that it amplifies scale and speed in ways that make human judgment more — not less — important. AI expands the possibility space of design options; humans must then select what matters, which requires stronger conceptual clarity, sharper criteria, and more deliberate prioritization. This framing positions AI as a capability multiplier that raises the stakes for good process and good thinking.

### Post-It Note History (Referenced, Class 2)
The CNN article on Post-It Note history was assigned reading for Class 2 and referenced in discussion as an example of how a product can emerge from an unexpected source (3M's failed adhesive) and succeed by identifying a latent need (a repositionable but not permanently adhesive note). The case illustrates push-driven opportunity identification and the value of not discarding "failed" R&D outputs before understanding their potential market application.

## Key Takeaways

- **Branding is de-commoditization** — it removes a product from pure price competition by shaping customer perceptions, but it requires a genuinely good product as its foundation; the Fyre Festival disaster shows that branding without substance destroys rather than creates value.
- **The Three Innovation Challenge** demands that every product opportunity pass three simultaneous tests — desirable to people, viable for the business, and technically feasible — and failure on any single dimension is disqualifying.
- **Latent needs** represent the highest-value innovation target because customers cannot yet articulate them; addressing latent needs creates delight and competitive differentiation that is difficult to copy.
- **The Jobs-to-Be-Done framework** reframes product development around the customer's underlying motivation ("When [situation], I want to [motivation], so I can [outcome]") rather than surface-level product features, leading to more durable and differentiated solutions.
- **The Opportunity Tournament Filtering Model** manages innovation portfolio risk by maintaining a broad initial set of ideas and eliminating them through successive evidence-based filters rather than committing resources prematurely.
- **PESTEL and Porter's Five Forces** operate at different levels of environmental analysis — macro and competitive respectively — and together provide the full context for evaluating whether an opportunity space is attractive.
- **Needs statements must be written in "what" not "how"** — describing the customer's need rather than prescribing a solution — to preserve design freedom and prevent premature closure on suboptimal approaches.
- **Product specifications require a metric, a value, and units** — qualitative customer needs cannot guide development until they are translated into measurable, prioritized targets.
- **AI expands the design possibility space** but makes human judgment more critical, not less; the ability to choose what matters becomes the scarce and valuable skill.
- **Internally generated and customer-driven opportunities** together form the largest empirical source of successful new product ideas, underscoring the value of both organizational capability awareness and deep customer research.

## Key Terms and Definitions Glossary

**Brand**: The full set of dimensions — including perceptions, associations, expectations, and experiences — that differentiate a product in the minds of customers beyond its physical or functional attributes. [Related: De-commoditization, Product, Branding]

**Brand Equity**: The added value a well-known brand name confers on a product beyond its functional benefits, accumulated through consumer perceptions, associations, trust, and loyalty over time; enables premium pricing and reduces price sensitivity. [Related: Brand, De-Commoditization, Branding]

**Concept Generation**: The NPD stage in which multiple product concepts are created and explored before one is selected for further development. [Related: NPD Process, Opportunity Identification, Concept Evaluation]

**De-Commoditization**: The strategic act of differentiating a product through branding so that it competes on dimensions other than price alone, reducing substitutability. [Related: Brand, Porter's Five Forces, Intensity of Rivalry]

**Ethnography**: A qualitative research method in which researchers observe customers in their natural environments (homes, workplaces, daily activities) to surface behaviors, workarounds, and needs that customers cannot articulate in interviews or surveys; especially effective for discovering latent needs. [Related: Latent Needs, Customer Need Analysis, Voice of the Customer]

**Explicit Needs**: Customer needs that are readily articulated and are typically already being addressed by existing market offerings. [Related: Unfulfilled Needs, Latent Needs, Customer Need Analysis]

**Feasibility**: The technical dimension of the Three Innovation Challenge — whether the organization has or can develop the capability to build and deliver the product. [Related: Three Innovation Challenge, RWW Analysis, Viability]

**Jobs-to-Be-Done (JTBD)**: A customer insight framework holding that customers "hire" products to accomplish a job, framed as "When [situation], I want to [motivation], so I can [desired outcome]." [Related: Latent Needs, Needs Statements, Customer Research]

**Latent Needs**: Needs that customers may not be aware of or cannot yet express; addressing them can produce products that dramatically exceed expectations. [Related: Explicit Needs, Unfulfilled Needs, JTBD, Customer Research]

**Needs Statement**: A clear, concise written expression of a customer need following five strict conventions — written in "what" not "how," specific, positive (not negative), an attribute of the product (not a user action), using "can" not "must/should." [Related: JTBD, Product Specifications, Customer Research]

**New Product Development (NPD) Process**: The end-to-end sequence of organizational activities — from opportunity identification through launch — used to conceive, design, and commercialize a product. [Related: Opportunity Identification, Concept Generation, Product Specifications]

**Opportunity Identification**: The first stage of the NPD process, in which the organization identifies and selects among potential market needs or technology opportunities to pursue. [Related: NPD Process, Opportunity Tournament, Push vs. Pull]

**Opportunity Tournament Filtering Model**: A portfolio risk-management approach in which a large initial pool of opportunities is progressively narrowed through multiple evidence-based filters until only the most promising opportunities remain. [Related: Opportunity Identification, RWW Analysis, Diversification]

**PESTEL Analysis**: A macro-environmental scanning framework covering Political, Economic, Social, Technological, Environmental, and Legal factors affecting an organization's general environment. [Related: Porter's Five Forces, General Environment, Opportunity Identification]

**Porter's Five Forces**: A competitive analysis framework examining Threat of New Entrants, Bargaining Power of Buyers, Bargaining Power of Suppliers, Threat of Substitutes, and Intensity of Rivalry in an organization's operating environment. [Related: PESTEL, Operating Environment, De-Commoditization]

**Product**: Any offering — physical good, service, or other market offering — that can satisfy a want or need and is offered to a market for attention, acquisition, use, or consumption. [Related: Brand, NPD Process, Product Specifications]

**Product Innovation Charter (PIC)**: A guiding strategic document — also called a mission statement for the NPD project — that defines the product description, benefit proposition, key business goals, primary and secondary markets, constraints, assumptions, and stakeholders before development begins; sets the strategic scope for the entire development team. [Related: NPD Process, Opportunity Identification, Real-Win-Worth It Analysis]

**Product Specifications**: Quantified product requirements expressed as a metric, a target value, and units, derived from customer needs statements and prioritized by importance. [Related: Needs Statements, Customer Research, NPD Process]

**Real-Win-Worth It (RWW) Analysis**: An opportunity evaluation framework testing whether a market opportunity is real (is demand genuine?), winnable (can the firm compete?), and worth it (does it create sufficient business value?). [Related: Three Innovation Challenge, Opportunity Tournament, Opportunity Identification]

**TAM/SAM/SOM**: A three-level market sizing framework: Total Addressable Market (the entire theoretical revenue opportunity at 100% share), Serviceable Addressable Market (the realistic target segment given product scope and geography), and Serviceable Obtainable Market (the share the firm can credibly capture given competition and execution constraints). [Related: Market Sizing, Real-Win-Worth It Analysis, Opportunity Identification]

**Three Innovation Challenge**: The requirement that a successful product simultaneously be desirable to people, viable for the business, and technically feasible — failure on any dimension disqualifies the opportunity. [Related: RWW Analysis, NPD Process, Feasibility, Viability]

**Unfulfilled Needs**: Needs that customers can easily express but that are not yet broadly addressed by available market offerings — representing a clear, validated opportunity space. [Related: Explicit Needs, Latent Needs, Opportunity Identification]

**Viability**: The business dimension of the Three Innovation Challenge — whether the product can generate sufficient revenue, margin, or strategic value to justify investment. [Related: Three Innovation Challenge, RWW Analysis, Feasibility]

**Voice of the Customer (VoC)**: The raw, verbatim language in which customers express their needs, frustrations, and desires, gathered through interviews, focus groups, observation, and ethnography; the primary input to customer needs analysis and needs statement writing. [Related: Customer Need Analysis, Needs Statements, Latent Needs]
