## Overview

Lecture 4 serves as a review and consolidation of the concept selection and testing methods introduced in the previous weeks of MKT3717. The session centers on the Concept Scoring Matrix, a quantitative tool used to evaluate and rank competing product concepts against weighted criteria. Students are reminded that concept evaluation is not merely about selecting the highest-scoring concept but about creating and developing the best possible concept through combination and refinement of features drawn from multiple candidates.

The lecture also situates concept testing within the broader product development process, distinguishing among the various types of testing that occur at different stages -- from early qualitative concept testing through prototype testing, alpha and beta testing, and eventually A/B testing at launch. The Product Innovation Charter (PIC) is revisited as a filtering mechanism: concepts that fall outside the firm's technological capabilities, target unfamiliar customers, or misalign on innovativeness or cost should be eliminated at this stage. Testing evolves from asking "Should we build this?" to "How do we make it work better at scale?"

A guest lecture by Jeungmin Kim, Director of the Contents Lab at SeeArt Foundation and Library Director at For My Stories, offered a practical case of experience redesign -- reimagining public libraries as exploration-driven environments for young people. The lecture concluded with a discussion of group creativity and brainstorming best practices, reinforcing the idea that structured ideation processes are critical for generating strong product concepts before they enter the screening and scoring pipeline.

## Key Concepts

### Concept Selection Process

#### Concept Screening
- **Definition**: The initial stage of concept selection where product concepts are compared against a reference concept using a simple rating scale (+, 0, -) or a numeric scale (1-5) to quickly eliminate weak candidates and retain promising ones.
- **Context**: Concept screening is the first pass in the two-stage concept selection process. Teams create and name concept combinations, prepare a matrix with criteria and a reference concept, and rate each concept relative to that reference. It precedes the more detailed concept scoring step.
- **Relationships**: Feeds into Concept Scoring; relies on criteria derived from customer needs analysis and product specifications (Lectures 2-3).

#### Concept Scoring
- **Definition**: A more rigorous quantitative evaluation method where product concepts are rated against weighted criteria, producing a total weighted score that enables ranking and comparison.
- **Context**: Concept scoring uses criteria weights derived from customer surveys and market research. Each concept receives a rating on each criterion, which is multiplied by the criterion's weight. The sum of weighted scores determines the concept's rank. The process includes steps for combining and improving concepts after initial scoring.
- **Relationships**: Builds on Concept Screening; results feed into Concept Testing (Phase 3); related to the Concept Scoring Matrix framework.

#### Reference Concept
- **Definition**: A benchmark product concept against which all other concepts are compared during screening and scoring. It provides a consistent standard for evaluation.
- **Context**: The reference concept is typically an existing product or a well-understood concept that the team uses as a baseline. In screening, concepts are rated as better than (+), same as (0), or worse than (-) the reference.
- **Relationships**: Central to both Concept Screening and Concept Scoring processes.

### Concept Testing (Phase 3)

#### Product Innovation Charter (PIC) Filtering
- **Definition**: The use of the Product Innovation Charter as a gate to eliminate concepts that fall outside the firm's strategic boundaries -- including technology gaps, unfamiliar customers, misaligned innovativeness levels, or unfavorable cost structures.
- **Context**: Concept testing is described as "a stage of elimination." The PIC acts as a strategic filter, ensuring that only concepts aligned with the firm's mission, capabilities, and market knowledge advance to development.
- **Relationships**: Connects to the overall NPD process; relates to the Three Innovation Challenge (People, Business, Technical) from Lectures 1-2.

### Testing in the Product Development Process

#### Types of Testing Across Development Stages
- **Definition**: A taxonomy of testing activities that occur at different phases of product development, from planning through production ramp-up.
- **Context**: The product development process flows through Planning, Concept Development, System-Level Design, Detail Design, Testing and Refinement, and Production Ramp-Up. Different types of testing are deployed at each stage:
  - Go/No-Go Decision Gates (at multiple transition points)
  - Specification Testing / Benchmarking (early stages)
  - Prototype Testing / Design Iterations (concept and system-level design)
  - Qualitative Concept Testing (early in concept development)
  - Performance and User Interface Testing / Alpha (detail design)
  - Quantitative Market Testing (testing and refinement)
  - Validation / Acceptance Testing / Beta (late testing)
  - A/B Testing / Release Versioning (production ramp-up)
- **Relationships**: Provides the overarching framework within which concept scoring, conjoint analysis (Lecture 5), and market experiments (Lecture 6) fit.

### Group Creativity

#### Brainstorming Best Practices
- **Definition**: A structured group ideation technique governed by specific rules designed to maximize the quantity and diversity of ideas generated.
- **Context**: Common rules include: defer judgment, build on others' ideas, encourage wild ideas, express ideas visually, stay focused, maintain one conversation at a time, and use topic-related stimuli. Setup requires advance preparation, a stimulating space, 4-8 participants, physical materials (paper, markers, whiteboards), refreshments, and a skilled facilitator.
- **Relationships**: Supports the Concept Generation phase (Lecture 2) that precedes Concept Screening and Scoring.

## Frameworks & Mental Models

### Concept Scoring Matrix

- **Visual Description**: A table with selection criteria as rows (grouped under weighted categories) and product concepts as columns. Each cell contains a rating and a weighted score (rating multiplied by the criterion's sub-weight within the category weight). The bottom row shows the total weighted score and overall rank for each concept. The spill-proof cup holder example had 8 major criteria categories (Flexible Use at 20%, Maintains Drink Condition at 15%, Survives Boating Environment at 5%, Keeps Drink Container Stable at 20%, Requires Little Maintenance at 5%, Easy to Use at 15%, Attractive in Environment at 10%, Manufacturing Ease at 10%) with sub-criteria under each.
- **How to Apply**:
  1. Prepare the matrix using criteria weights from customer/problem surveys.
  2. Rate each concept on each criterion using scores based on quantitative analysis.
  3. Calculate weighted scores and sum them to get total scores.
  4. Rank concepts by total score.
  5. Combine and improve -- examine whether negatives can be offset by borrowing features from other concepts.
  6. Select one or more concepts per function.
  7. Reflect and get consensus -- identify metrics with low confidence and check for missing criteria.
- **When to Use**: When multiple product concepts need to be compared systematically using customer-derived criteria. Most effective when criteria weights are well-established through market research.

### Six-Step Concept Scoring Process

- **Visual Description**: A linear six-step workflow: (1) Prepare the Matrix, (2) Rate Concepts, (3) Rank Them, (4) Combine and Improve, (5) Select, (6) Reflect and Get Consensus.
- **How to Apply**: Follow the steps sequentially. After ranking, critically examine whether the top-ranked concept is truly the best or merely the "best average." Steal standout features from lower-ranked concepts and combine them into an improved design. Revisit uncertain weightings and ratings before finalizing.
- **When to Use**: After concept screening has narrowed the field to a manageable number of promising concepts. Particularly valuable when team members have differing opinions about which concept is best.

### Product Development Process Flow (Testing View)

- **Visual Description**: A sequential pipeline of six stages (Planning, Concept Development, System-Level Design, Detail Design, Testing and Refinement, Production Ramp-Up) with different types of testing activities mapped beneath each stage as colored dots. Go/No-Go Decision Gates appear as diamond shapes at key transition points.
- **How to Apply**: Use this framework to identify which type of testing is appropriate at each stage of development. Early stages use qualitative methods; later stages use quantitative and market-facing tests. Decision gates determine whether to proceed, iterate, or kill the project.
- **When to Use**: When planning the testing strategy for a new product development project, or when determining whether a concept is ready to advance to the next development stage.

## Formulas & Quantitative Tools

### Weighted Concept Score

- **Formula**: Total Weighted Score = Sum of (Criterion Weight x Rating) for all criteria

- **Variables**:

| Variable | Description | Units |
|----------|-------------|-------|
| Criterion Weight | Importance weight assigned to each major criterion category (e.g., Flexible Use = 20%) | Percentage (must sum to 100%) |
| Rating | Performance score of the concept on each criterion | Numeric (e.g., 1-5) |
| Weighted Score | Product of weight and rating for a single criterion | Numeric |
| Total Weighted Score | Sum of all weighted scores across all criteria | Numeric |

- **Interpretation**: Higher total weighted scores indicate concepts that perform better across the most important criteria. The concept with the highest total score ranks first. However, a high total score does not guarantee the concept is the best possible product -- it may be the "best average" that excels nowhere.

- **Example**: In the spill-proof cup holder example, seven concepts (A, C, F, I, J, K, O) were evaluated across criteria weighted from 5% to 20%. Concept C scored 594 (Rank 1), followed by Concept O at 587 (Rank 2) and Concept F at 585 (Rank 3). Concept I scored lowest at 484 (Rank 7).

- **Common Mistakes**:
  - Assuming the highest-scoring concept is definitively "the best" without examining whether features from lower-ranked concepts could improve it.
  - Failing to check sensitivity of results to changes in weights or uncertain ratings.
  - Using weights that do not reflect actual customer priorities.
  - Not performing concept selection separately for different customer segments.

## Case Studies

### Spill-Proof Cup Holder for Boats

- **Company**: In-class exercise (product design context)
- **Challenge**: Design a cup holder for use on boats that keeps drinks secure, is easy to use, survives the boating environment, and is attractive and manufacturable.
- **What Was Done**: Seven product concepts (A, C, F, I, J, K, O) were evaluated using a concept scoring matrix with eight weighted criteria categories including Flexible Use (20%), Keeps Drink Container Stable (20%), Easy to Use (15%), and Maintains Drink Condition (15%). Each concept was rated on sub-criteria within these categories, and weighted scores were computed.
- **Takeaway**: Concept C ranked first with 594 points, but the close scores of several concepts (C at 594, O at 587, F at 585) demonstrate that small changes in weights or ratings could alter the ranking. The exercise shows why teams must combine and refine concepts rather than simply selecting the top scorer.

### Mechanical Pencil Concept Scoring Debrief

- **Company**: In-class exercise
- **Challenge**: Different student teams chose different "best" concepts for the same mechanical pencil design problem.
- **What Was Done**: Teams applied the concept scoring matrix independently, leading to different results due to different weighting choices, communication challenges, and subjective rating differences.
- **Takeaway**: A concept scoring matrix can select a product that is objectively worse if weights or ratings are miscalibrated. Features from losing concepts should be "stolen" and combined into the winning concept. In a real firm, resolving these debates requires consensus-building and re-examination of assumptions.

### Library Redesign as Experience Design (Guest Lecture)

- **Company**: SeeArt Foundation / For My Stories (Korea)
- **Challenge**: Traditional public libraries fail to engage young people and ignite curiosity.
- **What Was Done**: Jeungmin Kim reimagined libraries as exploration-driven environments integrating youth exploration, story-centered experience design, and behavioral insights.
- **Takeaway**: Product and brand management principles extend beyond physical goods -- redesigning service experiences requires the same customer-need-driven thinking as new product development. Understanding latent needs is critical for designing experiences that delight users.

## Key Takeaways

- **Concept Scoring Matrix** is a systematic, weighted evaluation tool that produces quantitative rankings of product concepts, but it is a starting point for refinement -- not a final verdict.
- **Combine and refine** is the ultimate goal of concept evaluation: the best concept is often created by merging the strongest features from multiple candidates rather than simply selecting the top scorer.
- **Beware of the "best average" trap** -- a concept scoring matrix can favor mediocre all-rounders over concepts with standout features that truly differentiate in the market.
- **Sensitivity analysis** should always be performed on importance weightings and uncertain ratings to ensure the selected concept is robust to changes in assumptions.
- **Different customer segments** may yield different concept rankings, so concept selection should be performed separately for each target group and results compared.
- **Testing evolves** across the product development process from qualitative concept testing early on through quantitative market testing and A/B testing near launch.
- **PIC filtering** ensures that concepts requiring unavailable technologies, targeting unknown customers, or misaligned on innovativeness are eliminated before resources are wasted.
- **Brainstorming effectiveness** depends on structured rules (defer judgment, build on ideas, encourage wild ideas) and proper setup (skilled facilitator, small groups, physical materials).
- **Go/No-Go Decision Gates** at key transition points in development ensure that only viable concepts advance to the next stage.

## Key Terms and Definitions Glossary

**A/B Testing**: A method of comparing two versions of a product or feature by exposing different user groups to each version and measuring which performs better. Used during production ramp-up and release versioning. [Related: Quantitative Market Testing, Beta Testing]

**Alpha Testing**: Internal performance and user interface testing conducted during the detail design phase, typically by the development team or internal users. [Related: Beta Testing, Prototype Testing]

**Beta Testing**: Validation and acceptance testing conducted with real users in real-world conditions, typically during the testing and refinement phase before full launch. [Related: Alpha Testing, A/B Testing]

**Brainstorming**: A structured group ideation technique designed to generate a large quantity of creative ideas by following rules such as deferring judgment, building on others' ideas, and encouraging wild thinking. [Related: Concept Generation, Group Creativity]

**Concept Scoring**: A quantitative evaluation method that rates product concepts against weighted criteria to produce total weighted scores and rankings, enabling systematic comparison and selection. [Related: Concept Screening, Concept Scoring Matrix, Weighted Score]

**Concept Scoring Matrix**: A structured table used to evaluate multiple product concepts against weighted selection criteria, producing ranked scores that guide concept selection and refinement. [Related: Concept Scoring, Reference Concept, Sensitivity Analysis]

**Concept Screening**: The initial stage of concept selection that uses simple scales (+, 0, -) to compare concepts against a reference concept and quickly eliminate weak candidates. [Related: Concept Scoring, Reference Concept]

**Concept Testing**: Phase 3 of the product development process in which selected concepts are evaluated for feasibility, desirability, and strategic fit before advancing to full development. It is fundamentally a stage of elimination. [Related: PIC Filtering, Go/No-Go Decision Gates]

**Go/No-Go Decision Gate**: A formal checkpoint in the product development process where stakeholders decide whether a project should proceed to the next stage, be revised, or be terminated. [Related: Product Development Process, Concept Testing]

**Product Innovation Charter (PIC)**: A strategic document that defines the boundaries of a product development effort, including target markets, technology requirements, and innovation goals. Used as a filter during concept testing to eliminate misaligned concepts. [Related: Concept Testing, Three Innovation Challenge]

**Prototype Testing**: Design iteration testing conducted during concept development and system-level design phases, using physical or digital prototypes to validate design decisions. [Related: Alpha Testing, Specification Testing]

**Reference Concept**: A benchmark product concept used as the standard against which all other concepts are compared during screening and scoring evaluations. [Related: Concept Screening, Concept Scoring]

**Sensitivity Analysis**: The practice of testing how changes in importance weightings or uncertain ratings affect the concept ranking, ensuring the selected concept is robust to assumption changes. [Related: Concept Scoring Matrix, Weighted Score]

**Specification Testing**: Benchmarking tests conducted early in the product development process to verify that product specifications meet defined standards. [Related: Prototype Testing, Product Specifications]

**Weighted Score**: The product of a criterion's importance weight and a concept's rating on that criterion, used to compute total concept scores in a scoring matrix. [Related: Concept Scoring Matrix, Concept Scoring]
